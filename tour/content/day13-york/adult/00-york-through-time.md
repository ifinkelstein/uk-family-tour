# Two Thousand Years in an Afternoon
Most cities give you one great age and ask you to imagine the rest. York gives you all of them at once, stacked, and lets you walk through the pile in a single afternoon. Stand almost anywhere in the old center and there is Roman stone beneath your feet, a Viking street name on the sign above your head, a medieval church at the corner, and a Georgian shopfront filling the gap between them. This is a small place, only about two hundred thousand people today, yet for the better part of two thousand years it was one of the most important cities in Britain, and the reasons kept changing.

It began as Roman Eboracum, a legionary fortress planted in the year seventy one where two rivers met, a spot the army liked because it could be supplied by boat and defended on two sides. It grew into a provincial capital, a place where emperors held court and, in one case, where an emperor was made. When Rome withdrew, the Angles took the ruins and renamed them; then a Danish army arrived and made York the capital of a Viking kingdom, a story we will save for tomorrow. Out of all of that came the medieval city you are standing in, a place of guilds and pilgrims and one of the largest cathedrals north of the Alps.

And then, crucially, the story slowed. York stopped being essential, and that is exactly why so much of it survived. What you are about to walk through, the walls, the crooked butchers' street, the enormous Minster, is not a reconstruction. It is the actual thing, left standing because the rest of the world moved on. Listen on for how the layers got here, and how to read them.
