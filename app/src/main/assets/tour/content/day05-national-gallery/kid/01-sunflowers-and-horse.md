# Sunflowers and a Giant Horse
Two paintings to hunt for first. Number one: Vincent van Gogh's Sunflowers, one of the most famous paintings on Earth. Van Gogh painted it in 1888 to decorate a bedroom for his friend, the painter Gauguin, who was coming to stay. He painted sunflowers because he loved yellow — he believed yellow meant happiness and friendship — and he worked fast, because cut sunflowers droop within days. Look closely: the paint is so thick in places it stands up off the canvas like frosting. In his whole life, Van Gogh barely sold any paintings; today this one is worth more money than you could spend in a hundred lifetimes. Number two: find Whistlejacket — you can't miss him. He's a life-sized rearing horse, nearly three meters tall, painted by George Stubbs around 1762, with nothing else in the picture. No rider, no field, no sky. Just horse. Whistlejacket was a real, famous, extremely grumpy racehorse. There's a legend that when the horse saw the finished painting, he thought it was a rival stallion and tried to attack it. Stubbs knew horses inside out — literally. He spent eighteen months dissecting horses and drawing every muscle, so his horses look more real than anyone else's ever had.
