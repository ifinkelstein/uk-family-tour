# Dinosaurs: The Word Was Invented Here
Here's something amazing: the word "dinosaur" was invented by the man who built this museum. In 1842, scientist Richard Owen looked at giant fossil bones and realized they belonged to a whole lost group of animals. He named them Dinosauria — "terrible lizards." Then he spent decades fighting for a "cathedral to nature" grand enough to hold them: this building, opened in 1881. In the dinosaur gallery, look for three things. First, the roaring animatronic T. rex — younger kids sometimes bail, so decide your bravery level in advance. Second, the first Iguanodon fossils ever studied: early scientists found a spike and stuck it on the nose, like a rhino horn. Totally wrong — it was a thumb spike! Iguanodon basically had built-in daggers for thumbs. Scientists make mistakes and fix them; that's the whole job. Third, look for fossils collected by Mary Anning, a poor girl from the seaside town of Lyme Regis who, starting when she was about twelve, in the early 1800s, dug up some of the first ichthyosaurs and plesiosaurs — sea monsters of the dinosaur age. The fancy scientists of London bought her fossils and often took the credit. Today she's celebrated as one of the greatest fossil hunters ever. Not bad for a twelve-year-old.
