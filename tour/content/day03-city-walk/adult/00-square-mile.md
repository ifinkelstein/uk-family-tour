# The Square Mile: A City Inside the City
Here is a thing almost no visitor grasps until someone says it out loud. When Londoners talk about the City, with a capital C, they do not mean the sprawling metropolis of nine million people you flew into. They mean this. A patch of ground barely one mile across, one point one two square miles to be exact, that sits like a walnut inside the vast shell of Greater London. This is the original. This is where the whole thing started, and it has been lived in, without a single break, for very nearly two thousand years.

The Romans founded it around the year 47, a trading town they called Londinium, on the north bank of the Thames. Everything since has been built on top of that, layer over layer. Saxons and Vikings fought over it. Medieval kings walled it and taxed it. The Great Fire of 1666 burned most of it to ash, and it rose again. German bombs gutted it in 1940, and it rose again. And today, on those same Roman streets, stands one of the two or three greatest concentrations of money on earth, glass towers with silly nicknames leaning over churches older than most countries. Here is the oddest fact of all. Barely nine thousand people actually live in the square mile, yet roughly half a million pour in to work each morning and drain out again each night, and the little City keeps its own mayor, its own ancient traditions, even its own police force, quite separate from the London all around it.

Today you walk it. You will pass a ruined garden church, then the great dome of St Paul's, and you will end at the Tower of London, the fortress a conquering king planted here to remind everyone who was in charge. Keep one idea in your pocket all day. Almost everything you touch today is standing on something older, and usually on something that once burned.
