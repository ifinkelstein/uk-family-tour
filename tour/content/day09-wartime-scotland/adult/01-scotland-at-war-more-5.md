# What Scotland Built and What It Cost
In both wars Scotland was as much workshop as fortress, and nowhere more than along the Clyde. Clydebuilt had been a worldwide byword for quality since Victorian times, and in wartime the river's yards turned out warships at a rate the enemy could only envy. That's exactly why the Luftwaffe came for it. On the nights of the thirteenth and fourteenth of March, nineteen forty-one, over four hundred bombers attacked Clydebank, the shipbuilding town at John Brown's yard where the great liners had been launched. It was Scotland's worst bombing of the war. The official count was five hundred and twenty-eight dead in Clydebank alone, with the toll across wider Clydeside put well above a thousand, and the town was effectively destroyed: of roughly twelve thousand houses, only a handful came through undamaged, and some thirty-five thousand people were left homeless out of a population of about fifty thousand. Yet the yards themselves recovered fast, and the workers came back, many walking in daily from the rest centres they'd been scattered to. Clydebank kept building ships to the end of the war, which was its own kind of defiance.

Much of that workforce, in both wars, was women. In the First World War the largest factory on earth ran along the border at Gretna, nine miles of cordite works where thirty thousand workers, twelve thousand of them women, kneaded gun-cotton and nitroglycerine into shell propellant with their bare hands. Arthur Conan Doyle visited and named the explosive paste the devil's porridge, and the wages and independence those munitions girls earned helped push the vote for women over the line.

Scotland's greatest single contribution, though, may have been invisible. Robert Watson-Watt, a radio scientist from Brechin in Angus, and by family tradition a descendant of the engineer James Watt, proved in February nineteen thirty-five that an aircraft could be detected by bouncing radio waves off it. From that experiment grew Chain Home, the radar network that told Fighter Command where the Luftwaffe was in nineteen forty, and without which the Battle of Britain is probably lost. There's a postscript Scots love. Years later in Canada, Watson-Watt was pulled over for speeding, caught by a radar gun, and the story goes he told the officer that if he'd known what they'd do with it, he'd never have invented it. He even wrote a rueful poem about being the victim of his own creation.

The war's geography still shows if you know where to look. Atlantic convoys marshalled in the Clyde, and from nineteen forty-two the Arctic convoys to Russia assembled in Loch Ewe, a remote sea loch in the far northwest where up to ninety-five ships at a time lay at anchor before the terrible run to Murmansk. Nearly five hundred merchant ships sailed from that one quiet loch. And all around the coast, the defences of nineteen forty were simply left where they stood: pillboxes in the dunes, rows of concrete anti-tank cubes marching along beaches, airfields gone back to farmland. On day thirteen, as your train south hugs the East Lothian and Berwickshire coast, watch the beaches below the line. Those grey concrete blocks half-buried in the sand were put there to stop German tanks, and Scotland never bothered to tidy them away. The wars ended. The stone, as usual in Scotland, stayed.
