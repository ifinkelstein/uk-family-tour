# The Chapel Royal and the Word of God
Of all the rooms at Hampton Court, the Chapel Royal is the one where the past is least interrupted. It has been a place of continuous Christian worship for more than four hundred and eighty years, since Wolsey's day, and services are still sung here by its own choir, a living thread of sound running unbroken back to the Tudors. Step in and look up, because the ceiling is the great survivor, a vault of deep blue studded with gold, ribbed and pendant, crowded with cherubs and gilded mottoes, begun for Henry in the fifteen thirties. Unlike almost everything else in the palace, it was never swept away by a later fashion. Kings and queens knelt beneath it then, and a congregation still kneels beneath it now.

This chapel has sat at the center of England's religious convulsions. It was here, according to a well-documented account, that Henry received the written evidence of Catherine Howard's alleged betrayal, passed to him during Mass in the autumn of fifteen forty-one, the moment that set her destruction in motion. But the chapel's largest claim on history is quieter, and it happened just along the corridor. In January of sixteen oh four, the new king, James the First, freshly arrived from Scotland to inherit Elizabeth's throne, summoned England's leading churchmen and a delegation of Puritan reformers to Hampton Court, to settle what kind of Protestant country this was going to be.

The meeting, known ever after as the Hampton Court Conference, mostly disappointed the reformers. James, who liked bishops and royal authority, gave them little of the change they had come for. But out of the wrangling came one almost accidental decision that outlived every other word spoken in those rooms. A Puritan scholar named John Rainolds proposed that England should have a single new, authoritative translation of the Bible. James, who disliked the popular Bible then in circulation for its anti-royal marginal notes, warmed to the idea at once. And so, from a conference held in this palace, came the order that produced, seven years later, the King James Bible, the book that would go on to shape the English language more profoundly than any other. It is an extraordinary thing to stand here and grasp. The cadences of half the phrases in the language were set moving inside this building, at a meeting that was really about something else entirely.
