# A Timeline You Can Hang This On
Dates can wash over you in a place this old, so let me give you a scaffold using events you already know, and hang Stirling's story on it.

Begin in the year ten sixty six, a date every schoolchild in Britain learns. That was the Norman Conquest, when William the Conqueror sailed from France, defeated the English at the Battle of Hastings, and made himself king of England. For centuries afterward, England's rulers spoke French and looked to France. Scotland, meanwhile, went its own way as an independent kingdom to the north. Hold that image, two separate realms, one of them freshly conquered by French speaking knights.

Now jump forward about two hundred and thirty years, to the twelve nineties and thirteen hundreds. This is the age of knights in mail, of the Crusades winding down, and just after Magna Carta, the famous charter English barons forced on their king in twelve fifteen. It is in this window that Stirling has its first great moment. William Wallace wins his ambush at Stirling Bridge in twelve ninety seven, and Robert the Bruce secures Scottish independence at Bannockburn, just over the hill, in thirteen fourteen. To place it in world terms, this is a generation before the Black Death, the great plague that swept Europe and killed perhaps a third of its people around thirteen forty eight.

Skip ahead again, roughly two more centuries, to the fifteen hundreds. Now we are in the Renaissance. Columbus has crossed the Atlantic in fourteen ninety two. Leonardo and Michelangelo are at work in Italy. In England, Henry the Eighth is breaking with the Pope, and you will have seen his palace at Hampton Court earlier on this trip. In exactly these decades, the Scottish king James the Fifth builds the dazzling Renaissance palace at the heart of this castle, and his daughter Mary, Queen of Scots, is born and crowned here. Stirling is now a royal showpiece, not just a fort.

Then two hinge dates to finish. In sixteen oh three, the crowns of Scotland and England come together under a single king, though the countries stay separate for another century. In seventeen oh seven, their parliaments merge, and Great Britain as a single state is born. And in seventeen forty five, the last great attempt to overturn all this, the Jacobite rising of Bonnie Prince Charlie, sends an army past this very castle. Stirling's story runs from the age of Wallace to the age of Bonnie Prince Charlie, more than four centuries, and you can see all of it from these walls.
