# Racing North on the Flying Scotsman's Line
You're riding one of the most famous railway lines in the world: the East Coast Main Line, from London King's Cross to Edinburgh — the route of the legendary Flying Scotsman. Trains have run this exact route since the 1850s, and in the old days, rival railway companies literally raced each other to Scotland, shaving minutes off, night after night, in what newspapers called the Races to the North. In 1934, the locomotive called Flying Scotsman became the first steam engine ever officially recorded at one hundred miles per hour. Four years later, on a stretch of this very line, a blue streamlined engine called Mallard hit one hundred twenty-six miles per hour — still the world speed record for steam, nearly ninety years later. Your train is faster and considerably less smoky. Things to watch for out the window: about halfway, look for Durham Cathedral and castle rising on a cliff above a river bend — one of the best views from any train in Britain, on the right side going north. Then comes the seaside: the line runs along the North Sea cliffs, and if you watch carefully near Alnmouth and Berwick you might spot castles on the coast. Keep a tally: sheep, castles, and the sea. First one to a hundred sheep wins.
