# The Most Dangerous Room Has No Swords
Quick quiz. Where was the most dangerous place in Tudor England? You might guess a battlefield, or the Tower of London with its axes. But there is a good argument that it was a quiet room here at Hampton Court, with nothing scarier in it than a table and some chairs. It is called the Council Chamber, and this is where the Privy Council met, about a dozen of the king's most trusted advisers, the men who actually ran England day to day. Privy is an old word for private, so this was the king's private, inner team. While the court was here, they met nearly every day, usually around midday, sat down at the table, and worked through the kingdom's problems like the world's most terrifying committee meeting. And what problems. In rooms like this one, they thrashed out England's break with the Pope in Rome and the creation of a whole new church with Henry as its head, a decision so enormous it still shapes England today. They handled the end of Henry's marriage to Anne Boleyn, which finished with her execution. They untangled his marriage to Anne of Cleves. And in the autumn of fifteen forty-one, while the court was living right here, the councillors investigated the secrets of the young queen, Catherine Howard, the one whose scream is said to echo in the Haunted Gallery, gathering statements and evidence while she waited, terrified, in her rooms just a few corridors away. Here is the thing to really get your head around. None of this was done with swords. It was done with words and paper. Somebody read reports out loud. Somebody wrote things down. Somebody prepared a document, and when the king's signature went on it, that piece of paper could close down monasteries, change what the whole country believed, or end a queen's life. Ink could be deadlier than any blade. Look at the walls when you visit. They are hung in blue and gold cloth covered in Tudor roses and in fleur-de-lis, which are lily flowers, the symbol of France, because English kings still cheekily claimed they were the rightful kings of France too. Then look at the table and picture the meeting. A dozen powerful men, every one of them brilliant, every one of them ambitious, and every one of them scared, because if the king turned against you, the next name in the paperwork could be your own. No dragons, no dungeons. Just a table where a signature could change the world. That is real power, and it is quieter than you think.
