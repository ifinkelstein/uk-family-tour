# Whiskies Around the World
Here is a surprise. Scotland is famous for whisky, but it is not the only place that makes it. Lots of countries make their own kind, all from the same idea, grain and water turned into a strong golden drink. Every country changes the recipe a little, so they all taste different. It is a bit like how loads of countries make bread, but a French loaf and an Indian naan and a pizza base are not the same thing at all.

First, a spelling puzzle you can amaze the grown-ups with. In Scotland they spell it whisky, with no letter e. But in Ireland and America they spell it whiskey, WITH an e. Same drink, different spelling! The story goes that long ago the Irish stuck an extra e in their word so people would know their whiskey was not the same as Scotland's. Then Irish families sailed off to America and took the spelling with them. So now you can look at a bottle and often guess where it came from, just from one little letter.

The Irish make their whiskey extra smooth by cooking it three times instead of two. In America they make a kind called bourbon out of corn, the same yellow corn you eat off the cob, and they keep it in brand-new barrels that have been burnt black and crispy on the inside, on purpose. That crispy black wood makes bourbon taste sweet, a bit like vanilla and toffee. And here is a brilliant recycling trick. Once an American barrel has been used, it gets shipped all the way across the ocean to Scotland, and the Scots fill it up with their whisky. So the barrel gets two whole lives, one in America and one in Scotland.

And then there is Japan, right on the other side of the world. About a hundred years ago a Japanese man travelled all the way to Scotland just to learn how whisky was made. He watched carefully, went home, and made it the Scottish way. For a long time nobody outside Japan noticed. Now Japanese whisky wins some of the biggest prizes in the whole world. Not bad for a copycat.

You will not be tasting any of these, of course. But if you ever spot a whisky bottle, you can be a detective. Look for the letter e. No e, and it probably comes from Scotland or Japan. An extra e, and it probably comes from Ireland or America. Whisky, or whiskey. One tiny letter, and a whole world of clues.
