# The Grossest Secrets of the Palace
Every gorgeous palace has a secret it does not put on the postcards, and here is Hampton Court's. Six hundred people lived and worked here, all eating two big meals a day, and every single one of them needed the toilet. So where did they all go?

Henry the Eighth built something for exactly this, and it had a very grand name, the Great House of Easement. That sounds like a fancy sitting room. It was not. It was an enormous shared toilet. Picture a long wooden bench with holes cut in it, side by side, over a deep brick pit, and up to fourteen people sitting there at the same time, doing their business together and chatting away. There was no privacy, there were no separate cubicles, and there was definitely no toilet paper. Instead people used soft cloth, or moss, or even, believe it or not, the soft neck feathers of a goose. Feel free to say yuck. Everybody does.

Now, all of that waste had to go somewhere, and mostly it drained through pipes and pits, and a lot of it ended up in the moat and eventually the river. So the palace could get very smelly, very fast, especially in summer. That is actually one reason the whole court packed up and left every so often. When the king went off travelling, an army of cleaners moved in to scrub the palace from top to bottom, air out the rooms, and, as they charmingly put it, sweeten the house, before the king came back. A royal palace, it turns out, was a bit like a giant, beautiful, hard-to-clean toilet with a king living on top of it. Stick around, because it gets even grosser, and much more surprising, when you find out who had the job of looking after the king's own private toilet.
