# Weird and Wonderful Feast Food
You have heard how much food went through these kitchens. But on a really big feast day, the cooks did not just cook a lot, they showed off, and some of the things they made were spectacular, and a little bit bonkers.

Take the peacock. Nobody actually ate peacock because it tasted nice, it was tough and chewy. They ate it because it looked amazing. The cooks would very carefully peel the whole feathery skin off the bird in one piece, roast the meat, and then dress the cooked bird back up in its own feathers so it arrived at the table looking alive, tail fanned out in all its colours. Sometimes they even soaked a scrap of cotton in something that would burn, tucked it in the peacock's beak, and lit it, so the bird seemed to breathe fire and smoke as the servants carried it in. Imagine that gliding toward you through the candlelight.

Then there were subtleties. That is the Tudor word for sculptures made entirely out of sugar and almond paste, sometimes as big as a model castle, with towers and walls and little figures, and all of it could be eaten. A subtlety might show a castle, or a lion, or a whole scene from a story, and it was carried in between courses purely so that everyone would gasp. Sugar was fantastically expensive back then, so building a palace out of it was a bit like building one out of gold. It said, look how rich the king is, he can eat treasure. There was even a sweet almond paste called marchpane, moulded into fruits and flowers and coats of arms, and often brushed with real gold so it glittered.

And here is a detail that might make you laugh. There is an old nursery rhyme about four and twenty blackbirds baked in a pie, that begin to sing when the pie is opened. That actually came from a real Tudor party trick. Cooks would bake an empty pastry crust, then slip live birds underneath it just before serving, so that when the pie was cut open at the table, the birds all flapped out in a panic. No, you could not eat that pie afterwards. That was never the point. The point, once again, was to make every single guest say wow.
