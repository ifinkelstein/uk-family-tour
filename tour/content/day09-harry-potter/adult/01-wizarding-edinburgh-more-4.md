# Room Five Five Two

At the east end of Princes Street, the Balmoral's great clock tower rules the skyline. It was built in nineteen oh two as the North British Station Hotel, the railway hotel for Waverley below, and its clock is famously kept three minutes fast so that travelers don't miss their trains. The one exception is Hogmanay, New Year's Eve, when it's set to the correct time for the midnight bells, then nudged fast again. Remember that the whole story you've been following started with a woman getting off a train at Waverley. It ends upstairs in this hotel.

By early two thousand and seven, Rowling was deep in the seventh and final book, Deathly Hallows, and struggling to finish it at home. Her solution was the classic writer's retreat, minus the budget constraints of nineteen ninety-three. She checked into room five five two at the Balmoral and worked. The contrast is the whole story in miniature. Fourteen years earlier she'd been stretching a single coffee through a writing session because that was what she could afford. Now she was finishing the series in a five-star suite overlooking the castle. And on the eleventh of January, two thousand and seven, in that room, she wrote the last words of the last book. Deathly Hallows was published that July and became the fastest-selling book in history.

Then came the vandalism. The suite contained a marble bust of Hermes, and Rowling, in the giddiness of the moment, wrote on it in ink. J K Rowling finished writing Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows in this room, five five two, on eleventh January two thousand and seven. She has cheerfully admitted to defacing the thing. The hotel's response was not to bill her for damages but to canonize the object. The bust sits in the suite to this day, protected under glass, and the room has been rebranded the J K Rowling Suite. Its door is painted purple, fitted with a brass owl door knocker, and renumbered five five two and three quarters, in honor of a certain railway platform. Sleeping in it costs north of a thousand pounds a night, and superfans book it well ahead, some to attempt their own novels at her writing desk.

It's worth pausing on what Edinburgh and Rowling have meant to each other across those fourteen years. The city sheltered her at her lowest, and it has collected the dividends ever since, in tourism, in bookshop windows, in walking tours. She, in turn, has stayed. She wrote all seven books wholly or partly here, took an honorary degree from the University of Edinburgh in two thousand and four, and remains an Edinburgh resident. Whatever you make of the shakier shrines around town, the cafes and gravestones and painted streets, this one is documented in her own handwriting, on a Greek god's marble shoulders, behind glass, five floors above the station where she first arrived.
