# From Barley to Glass
Making whisky is a slow trick played on a field of barley, and it runs in five stages. It begins with malting. The barley is soaked in water and fooled into thinking spring has come, so it starts to sprout, which unlocks the sugars sleeping inside each grain. At exactly the right moment the maltster halts the growth by drying the grain with heat. And here is the first great fork in the road. In some places, above all the island of Islay, that drying is done over a fire of peat, the dark ancient turf cut from the bogs, and the smoke sinks deep into the grain. That is where a smoky whisky gets its smoke, decided right at the start, in the kiln.

Next comes mashing. The dried malt is ground and stirred into hot water in a great vat, and out runs a warm, sweet liquid, almost a thin unhopped beer, called the wort. Then fermenting. The wort goes into huge wooden or steel washbacks, yeast is thrown in, and for a couple of days the whole thing froths and hisses and roars as the yeast eats the sugar and turns it to alcohol. What you have now is a rough, sour beer of about eight percent, called the wash.

Then the heart of it. The wash is boiled in those beautiful onion-shaped copper stills, some of them fifteen or twenty feet tall, gleaming and dented. Alcohol boils before water does, so it rises first as a vapour, travels up the swan neck of the still, and is caught and cooled back into a liquid far stronger than before. Scotch is usually distilled twice, in two different stills, each pass leaving the spirit cleaner and stronger. The stillman has to judge exactly which part of the run to keep, the pure "heart," and throw the rest back to be run again. It is a knack learned over years, by nose as much as by instrument.

And then the longest stage of all. Waiting. The clear, fiery new spirit is filled into oak casks, very often old barrels that once held sherry or American bourbon, and rolled into a dark warehouse to sleep. By law it must rest at least three years before it can even be called Scotch, and the good stuff sleeps for ten, fifteen, twenty years and more. In that quiet the harsh spirit mellows, turns gold, and drinks colour and flavour out of the wood. All the while a little escapes through the pores of the oak, roughly two percent a year, gone into the air. The distillers have a fine name for it. They call it the angels' share, the cut the angels take for guarding the barrels. After a dozen years in the cask the angels may have drunk nearly a quarter of it. That is the price of patience, and you can taste every year of it.
