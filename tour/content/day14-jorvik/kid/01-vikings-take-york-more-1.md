# The Great Horned Helmet Lie
Here is one of the biggest fibs in all of history, and today you get to bust it. Vikings did not wear horns on their helmets. Not one. Never. If you look around the JORVIK galleries, and later at a helmet called the Coppergate Helmet in the Yorkshire Museum across town, you will not find a single horn, because horns on a battle helmet would be a terrible idea. Imagine swinging a heavy axe at someone whose helmet has two handy handles sticking out, perfect for grabbing, twisting, and knocking his head sideways. No warrior would do that on purpose.

Real Viking helmets were simple, strong iron caps, round like a bowl, sometimes with a bar to guard the nose and eyes. In fact, in all the digging that has ever been done, archaeologists have found only one helmet complete enough to rebuild from the whole Viking age, pieced back together from rusty fragments found on a farm in Norway. One. And it has no horns.

So where did the horns come from? From the theatre. Almost nine hundred years after the Vikings, in the eighteen seventies, a German artist named Carl Emil Doepler designed the costumes for a giant, booming set of operas by the composer Richard Wagner, full of gods and heroes and doom. He gave some of the characters helmets with horns and wings to make them look wild and ancient and dramatic on stage. The audiences loved it. Painters copied it, then storybooks copied the paintings, then cartoons copied the storybooks, and before long every picture of a Viking, everywhere, had horns. A costume designer basically invented the look of the Viking in an afternoon, and the whole world believed him.

Here is the funny part. There are a few really old horned helmets from Scandinavia, but they come from more than a thousand years before the Vikings, back in the Bronze Age, and they were not for fighting at all. They were special objects for ceremonies, probably worn by priests, and only a couple of people ever wore them. The Vikings never saw them either.

So next time you spot a cartoon Viking with big curly horns, you can say, with total confidence, nope. The real Vikings who walked the streets right here under your feet wore plain iron caps, or often no helmet at all, just a wool hat and a stubborn attitude. The horns are pretend. You know the truth now.
