# The Church Where a King Was Crowned
Step into the Church of the Holy Rude and you are standing in the second oldest building in Stirling, after the castle itself. There has been a church on this spot since 1129, though the one around you was rebuilt in the early fourteen hundreds, after fire swept through the old town. Look up. That dark oak roof, held together not with nails but with wooden pegs, is the oldest surviving timber roof of its kind in Scotland. It has hung over this nave for six centuries.

Now the moment that makes this church truly extraordinary. Up in the castle, you heard how Mary, Queen of Scots was crowned as a baby in the castle chapel. Her story did not end happily at home. In 1567 she was forced from her throne and imprisoned, and her enemies needed a new monarch to crown in her place. That someone was her own son, a boy of just thirteen months. And they crowned him not in the castle above, but down here, in this town church, on the twenty ninth of July, 1567. He became James the Sixth of Scotland, and later, when the two crowns joined, James the First of England as well.

The sermon that day was preached by John Knox, the fierce leader of Scotland's Protestant reformation, the same firebrand you may hear about on the Royal Mile in Edinburgh. He chose a Bible story about a boy king crowned after a wicked queen is pushed aside, which was not exactly subtle. So here is the neat symmetry of your day: the mother crowned as a baby up the hill, the son crowned as a baby down the hill, the two ceremonies barely twenty four years apart.

Outside, the graveyard tumbles down the slope, packed with the tilting, weathered headstones of Stirling's merchants and ministers and soldiers. One last curiosity. For nearly three hundred years, from 1656 until 1936, a solid wall ran straight across the inside of this church, splitting it into two separate churches for two separate congregations who did not much care for one another. Very Scottish, that. And besides Westminster Abbey, this is the only church still in use in all of Britain to have hosted a coronation.
