# The Train That Squished a Whole Country
Try to imagine doing this exact trip a very long time ago, before there were any trains at all. To get from London all the way up to Scotland, you would have to climb into a wooden coach pulled by horses, and then bounce and rattle along bumpy roads for almost two whole days and nights, stopping again and again to swap the tired horses for fresh ones. Two days! You are going to do the very same trip today in about four and a half hours, sitting in a comfy seat, probably munching a snack.

That is the amazing thing trains did. They did not actually move the cities any closer together. But they made them feel closer, because suddenly you could zoom between them in a day instead of a week. Before trains, the top and the bottom of this island were almost like separate worlds, and most people never travelled far enough to see the other end of their own country. Trains squished the whole place together and let everybody finally go and visit everywhere.

Here is a bonus bit of magic. Before trains, every town set its own clock by looking up at the sun, so the time in one town could be a few minutes different from the time in the next town along, and nobody cared, because nobody moved fast enough to notice. But a train has to arrive on time, so the railways made everybody use the exact same clock. Trains did not just carry people around. They made the whole country agree on what time it was.

So here is your job for the rest of the ride. Watch out the window and try to catch the land changing. Down south it is soft and green, full of hedges and gentle little fields. But the further north you go, the more it turns wild and rugged and rocky, with big open hills and bare grey stone instead of tidy farms. If you look closely enough, your own eyes will spot the country changing long before anybody tells you that you have crossed into Scotland.
