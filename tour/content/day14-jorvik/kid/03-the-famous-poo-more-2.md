# Your Ride Through a Thousand Years
In a few minutes you are going to climb into a little car and ride straight into the past. Before you do, here is what is really going on, because the JORVIK ride is much cleverer than it looks.

The carriages are called time cars, and they carry you slowly through a rebuilt Viking street. But this is not a made-up fairy-tale village. The people who built it used what the diggers found in the ground right here, so the houses stand exactly where real houses stood, the workshops make the things the real workshops made, and even the muddy paths follow the real streets. It is a full-size copy of Jorvik, built on the very spot where Jorvik once stood.

Look at the faces of some of the figures. A few of them were built from real Viking skulls found in York. Scientists can take a skull and, layer by layer, add on where the muscles and skin and nose and lips would have gone, until a real face from a thousand years ago stares back at you. So some of the people you glide past are not invented. They are portraits of actual Vikings who lived and died in this city.

Now, the part everyone talks about. The smells. As you ride, you will smell things, and they are there on purpose. Woodsmoke from the fires, fish from the market, leather from the workshops, and, yes, the pong of the toilet pit. The designers really did engineer Viking smells, because your nose remembers things your eyes forget, and they wanted you to feel how a crowded, busy, whiffy city actually felt. Try not to hold your breath. That is the whole point.

Listen too. The voices you hear are speaking Old Norse, the real language of Jorvik, worked out by scholars from old writings. You are hearing, more or less, what these streets actually sounded like.

One more thing to know. This whole ride was underwater not long ago. In December of two thousand fifteen, terrible floods poured into York and swamped the Viking street. The centre had to shut, and clever people spent a year and a half rebuilding it, better than before, adding everything scientists had learned since. Water once buried and saved the real Jorvik in the mud, and water later tried to destroy the copy, and people rebuilt it anyway.

So climb aboard, breathe in, and keep your eyes and ears and nose wide open. You are about to travel one thousand years, and every smelly, noisy, muddy detail is trying to tell you the truth.
