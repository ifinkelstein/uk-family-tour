# The Divorce That Split a Church
The single most consequential thing the Tudors did began as a family problem. Henry the Eighth had married a Spanish princess, Catherine of Aragon, and after many years and many pregnancies she had given him a daughter but no surviving son. Henry convinced himself he needed a male heir to secure the dynasty, and he had also fallen for a young woman at his court named Anne Boleyn. So he asked the Pope, the head of the Catholic Church in Rome and at that time the ultimate authority over all of Western Christianity, to annul his marriage, to declare in effect that it had never been valid.

The Pope refused. And here Henry did something remarkable. Rather than accept the answer, he decided that no foreign churchman in Rome should have authority over him at all. Through a series of laws passed by his Parliament, above all the Act of Supremacy of fifteen thirty-four, Henry declared himself the supreme head of a separate Church of England, answerable to no Pope. He got his annulment, married Anne, and in doing so pulled an entire nation out of the Catholic Church.

This was part of a much larger storm sweeping Europe at the time, known as the Reformation, a movement to reform, and in many places to break away from, the Roman Catholic Church. Those who broke away came to be called Protestants. Henry's own motives were personal and political rather than deeply religious, but the consequences were enormous and lasting. Monasteries across the land were shut down and their wealth seized. The monarch, not the Pope, now stood at the head of the national church, and still does today. Elizabeth the First would later settle England firmly as a Protestant country, and much of the conflict of the next hundred years, in England and beyond, flowed from this split.

It is worth pausing on that. A king's wish to change wives ended up changing the religion of a country, and reshaping the balance of power between crown, church and Parliament for centuries to come.
