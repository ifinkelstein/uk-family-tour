# Best Rooms for Kids: The British Museum Treasure Route
The British Museum is too big to conquer. Do not try. The secret is to treat it like a treasure map. Pick a few brilliant rooms, give each one a mission, and leave while everyone still likes history.

Mission one: Room 4, giant Egypt. Find the Rosetta Stone, but do not spend all your energy fighting the crowd. Your job is to spot three things: the stone with three scripts, one giant king, and one animal symbol. The giant king is Ramesses the Second, whose broken statue still looks as if it could order an army around. For the animal, choose a cat, falcon, scarab, jackal, or snake. Ask what power that animal might stand for. Fast? Watchful? Dangerous? Reborn? Egyptian art is full of clues once you know animals are not just animals.

Mission two: Rooms 62 and 63, mummies and the afterlife. This is the big one, but go slowly. First, find a coffin that looks more like a person than a box. Then find a shabti, a tiny servant figure made to work for someone in the afterlife. Then look for an animal mummy. The best question here is not "is that gross?" It is "what problem were they trying to solve?" The ancient Egyptians were trying to keep a person safe after death. Bandages, coffins, amulets, spells, painted eyes, tiny workers, gods with animal heads: all of it was part of one huge survival plan.

Mission three: Room 41, Sutton Hoo. This is where Britain gets its own buried treasure. Find the helmet. It looks like a face from a legend: eyebrows, nose, moustache, metal mask. The treasure came from a ship burial in Suffolk, where the wooden ship had rotted away and left its shape in the sand. Imagine finding a ghost ship underground. Then find the Lewis Chessmen if they are nearby on display. Look at their expressions. Some queens look exhausted. Some warriors bite their shields as if they are too angry to wait for their turn. These are not boring chess pieces. They are tiny personalities carved in walrus ivory.

Mission four: the Assyrian rooms. Find the lamassu, the giant winged guardian creatures with human heads and lion or bull bodies. Count the legs. You may get five. That is not a mistake. From the front, the creature stands still with two legs. From the side, it strides forward with four. The sculptor gave it an extra leg so it works from both directions. That is a three-thousand-year-old visual effect. Nearby, hunt for the lion hunt reliefs. The king wanted everyone to admire his bravery, but many visitors today end up feeling more for the lions. That is what makes the art powerful.

Mission five: Room 1, Enlightenment. This room looks quieter, but it is a good final stop because it feels like stepping into an old library of the world. Cabinets, shells, books, carved things, scientific instruments, objects from everywhere. It shows how collectors tried to organize knowledge in the eighteenth century. But here is the grown-up twist kids can understand: collecting is not neutral. Some things were studied carefully. Some things were bought. Some things were taken because Britain had power. So your mission here is to choose one object and ask: how did it get here, and who gets to tell its story?

If you still have energy, add one bonus treasure: the turquoise serpent from Mexico, a two-headed snake covered in tiny blue stones. It is the perfect "look closely" object. From far away it is a bright flash of colour. Up close it is thousands of patient little pieces fitted together.

Here is the route rule. Each room gets seven minutes, not thirty. In each room, everyone picks one favourite object and one question. That is enough. A museum day should not feel like being dragged through every case by your shoelaces. It should feel like unlocking rooms in a game. Egypt gives you codes and mummies. Sutton Hoo gives you a ghost ship. Assyria gives you monster guardians. Room 1 gives you the mystery of collecting itself. If you leave with four objects you can describe from memory, you have won the British Museum.
