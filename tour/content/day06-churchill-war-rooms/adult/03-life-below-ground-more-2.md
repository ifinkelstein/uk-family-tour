# The Beauty Chorus and Other Relics
The Map Room is the beating heart of the whole site, and it repays close attention. Its most charming feature is a bank of coloured telephones, and the staff nicknamed them the beauty chorus, after the rows of costumed chorus girls in West End shows, because ordinary telephones of the era were uniformly black. The colours were a code. White handsets linked directly to the armed services, green to the intelligence and scrambler network, and another to the outside world. Crucially, none of them rang. Churchill demanded quiet for concentration, so the phones announced an incoming call with a silent flashing light rather than a bell.

The maps themselves are historical documents. Look for the great convoy chart, so densely pricked with pins tracking Atlantic shipping that in places the paper was worn through and had to be patched. And hidden among the serious cartography is one of the bunker's best jokes, a tiny pencil caricature of Hitler, sprawled on his back giving a cross-eyed salute, doodled by some anonymous member of staff. Nobody knows who drew it, or exactly when, which only adds to its charm, a flicker of gallows humour in a very grave place.

Then there are the accidental relics, the ones nobody meant to leave. Decades after the war, staff opening an old desk drawer found sugar cubes still inside, hoarded by someone in an age of rationing and forgotten in the rush of peace. It is a small, oddly moving thing, that little cache of sugar, a reminder that everyone who worked down here was an ordinary person improvising through extraordinary times. Hunt for these details as you go. They are the fingerprints of the living.
