# Standing on a Shaking Floor
Walk into the little Japanese supermarket in the earthquakes gallery and put your hand on the rail, because in a moment the floor is going to move. What you are about to feel is a copy of a real disaster. On the seventeenth of January, nineteen ninety-five, a powerful earthquake struck the city of Kobe in Japan, early in the morning while people were still waking up. It measured around seven on the scale scientists use, which is very strong, and it shook the ground so hard that buildings, roads, and railways came crashing down.

Inside the mock supermarket, the shelves begin to rattle, tins and packets tremble, the lights flicker, and on a little screen you can watch real footage from that morning in Kobe. It only lasts a few seconds, but it is enough to understand something you cannot learn from a book, what it actually feels like when the solid, dependable ground turns wobbly beneath your feet.

So why does the Earth do this? Here is the secret. The ground you are standing on is not one solid ball of rock. The Earth's hard outer shell is cracked into enormous pieces, like a cracked eggshell, and these pieces, called plates, are floating very slowly on hotter, softer rock below. They are always moving, about as fast as your fingernails grow, far too slow to feel. But sometimes two plates get stuck against each other, pushing and pushing for years, and the pressure builds and builds. Then, suddenly, they slip. All that stored-up energy is let go at once, and the ground shakes. That is an earthquake.

The very same cracking and pushing also makes volcanoes, which is why this gallery keeps them together. Melted rock from deep inside the Earth forces its way up through gaps between the plates and bursts out as a volcano. Earthquakes and volcanoes are twins, both of them made by the restless, moving skin of our planet.

So the shaking floor is not just a fairground ride. It is a tiny, safe taste of one of the most powerful forces on Earth. And when it stops, and the supermarket goes still again, you can remember that somewhere under your feet, right now, those giant plates of rock are still slowly grinding along, building up the pressure for the next one, somewhere, someday. The Earth is never really holding still.
