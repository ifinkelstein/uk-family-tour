# The Fastest, Flattest Bit and a Falling Apple
Have you noticed how smooth and fast the train feels in this early part of the journey? There's a reason for that. Right now you're crossing some of the flattest land in all of Britain, a huge, wide, pancake-flat area called the Fens. There are almost no hills to climb and almost no bends to slow down for, just mile after mile of dead-straight track running across enormous flat fields. And that is exactly why this is the fastest part of the whole trip. A train can really let rip on ground like this, and this is the very stretch where those record-breaking steam engines used to build up their tremendous speed. So sit back and feel it. You are on the fastest, flattest, straightest railway in the country, doing what it was built to do.

Now here's a wonderful thing to look out for as you zoom across it. Somewhere out here you flash past a town called Grantham, and just near it is an old farmhouse with an apple orchard. And that orchard might be the most important orchard in the history of the world. Why? Because a boy named Isaac Newton grew up right there, and he went to school in Grantham, the very town you're speeding through.

The story goes that one day, young Isaac was sitting under an apple tree in that garden, thinking, when an apple dropped off a branch and fell to the ground. Bonk. Now, everybody had seen apples fall before. Millions of apples had fallen for millions of years and nobody thought anything of it. But Isaac Newton asked a question nobody had properly asked. Why does the apple fall down? Why doesn't it float up, or drift sideways? What is pulling it down toward the ground?

And thinking about that one falling apple, he worked out one of the biggest secrets in the entire universe. He figured out gravity, the invisible pulling force that holds you on the ground, that keeps your feet from floating off, that makes a dropped toy fall and a thrown ball come back down. The very same force that keeps the Moon circling the Earth and the Earth circling the Sun. All of it, from one apple, near a town you're rushing past right now. So next time an apple, or anything at all, falls off your table, you can say, aha, gravity, just like Isaac Newton. And you'll know it was more or less invented right here, out of the window, in the flattest, fastest part of your ride to Scotland.
