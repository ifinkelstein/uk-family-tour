# Reading the Tudors in the Brick
Now that you know who they were, here is how to see them as you walk. Hampton Court is unusual because it is really two palaces in one. The front half, all warm red brick, twisting chimneys and battlemented gatehouses, is the Tudor palace, begun by Cardinal Wolsey and seized by Henry. The elegant, symmetrical stone half at the back was added by later kings more than a century afterward. The join runs right through the middle, and crossing it is like stepping over a hundred and fifty years in a single stride.

Stay with the Tudor half for a moment and look for the family everywhere. Find the Tudor rose worked into stone and glass. Look for the initials H and A entwined, for Henry and Anne Boleyn, which whoever scrubbed Anne's memory from the palace after her execution somehow missed in one or two places. Step into the Great Hall, the last great medieval hall built for an English king, and up into the Chapel Royal with its dazzling blue and gold ceiling. Both are Henry's work.

This palace is also the great survivor. Most Tudor palaces are simply gone, burned, demolished or crumbled into the ground. Whitehall, where much of Henry's court lived, largely burned. Hampton Court endured, which is why standing here is the closest you can come to walking through the actual, physical world of these people.

One last thread to carry with you. The Tudor line ended in sixteen oh three, when the childless Elizabeth died and the crown passed to her cousin up in Scotland, James the Sixth of Scots, who became James the First of England, the first of the Stuart family. If you are travelling on to Stirling and Edinburgh later in this trip, that is the family whose halls you will walk. The story does not end at Hampton Court. It simply moves north.
