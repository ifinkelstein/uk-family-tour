# The Cafe Where the Magic Got Written

Picture this. A young mum in a chilly Edinburgh winter, pushing a pram around the streets until her baby finally nods off. The moment those little eyes close, she hurries into a cafe, finds a corner table, orders a single coffee, and starts scribbling like mad. She has maybe an hour before the baby wakes. Every sleeping minute counts, because in her notebook, Harry Potter is learning to fly.

The cafe she went to most was called Nicolson's, up a flight of stairs on a busy corner not far from here. She picked it for a sneaky reason. It was partly owned by her brother-in-law, so nobody hassled her for sitting there for hours over one cold cup of coffee. Big chunks of the very first Harry Potter book were written at those tables. The cafe is gone now, it's been other restaurants since, but there's a plaque on the corner of the building telling you what happened upstairs.

Now for a little detective work. A few streets away sits another cafe, The Elephant House, and for years it had a big sign in the window saying Birthplace of Harry Potter. Sounds impressive. But is it true? Time to look at the evidence. The idea for Harry Potter popped into the author's head in nineteen ninety, on a delayed train in England, years before she ever came to Edinburgh. The first chapters were written in London. And Rowling herself finally said it straight out on the internet, she'd been writing Harry Potter for several years before she ever set foot in that cafe. So it can't be the birthplace. But, she added, she really did write there, so we'll let them off. Case closed. Not the birthplace. Still a real Harry Potter cafe.

Then the poor Elephant House had its own disaster. In August of twenty twenty-one a fire tore through the building, and the cafe had to close for more than four years. But firefighters and builders saved something special, the actual table where Rowling used to sit and write. It was fixed up, and when the cafe finally reopened just after Christmas in twenty twenty-five, the table went back in. Today her portrait hangs in that room next to other Edinburgh writers.

The bit I love most is this. Rowling was broke while she wrote that first book. She was living on money the government gives people who have almost nothing. Twelve publishers said no thanks to Harry Potter before one small one finally said yes. Think about that next time you're in a cafe. The person at the corner table, nursing one cold coffee and scribbling in a notebook, might be writing the most famous story of the next hundred years. And nobody around them has any idea.
