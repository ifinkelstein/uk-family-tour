# The Sneaky Innocent Railway
Now here's a secret hiding right at the edge of this park. There is a tunnel, a long dark tunnel, running underneath the ground near Arthur's Seat, and nearly two hundred years ago trains used to rumble through it. And the whole thing had the funniest, gentlest name. People called it the Innocent Railway.

Innocent? A railway? Why would a train be called innocent, like it hadn't done anything naughty? Here's the answer, and it's my favourite part. When railways were brand new, the very first steam trains scared people half to death. They were huge, they hissed and roared, they belched smoke, and they went terrifyingly fast, so fast that folk genuinely worried you might not be able to breathe. But this little railway didn't use a scary steam engine to pull its wagons along. It used horses. Actual horses, clip-clopping along, tugging the wagons on the rails. Because it was pulled by calm, friendly horses instead of a snorting steam monster, people decided it was safe and gentle and, well, innocent. And the name stuck.

The railway opened in the year eighteen thirty-one, which makes it one of the very first railways in all of Scotland, and its tunnel was the first railway tunnel the country ever had. It was built to carry coal into the city to keep everyone's fires burning, but then a clever man had an idea. He stuck a coach on the rails and started giving people rides, and it was such a hit that fourteen thousand passengers piled on in the very first month. Fourteen thousand. Everybody wanted a go on the gentle horse-train.

To get the wagons through that long tunnel under the park, they used a huge rope and a machine that hauled them along, because horses don't much like dark tunnels. Imagine being a wagon full of coal, creaking through the black tunnel on the end of a giant rope, with the whole hill sitting on top of you.

The trains are long gone now, but here's the brilliant thing. The tunnel is still there, and so is the old railway track, and today people walk and cycle right through it. You can stroll along the very path where the horses once pulled their wagons, and step into that same tunnel the coal trains rumbled through, with Arthur's Seat rising up above your head. A sneaky old horse-railway, hidden under a volcano, with the friendliest name a train ever had.
