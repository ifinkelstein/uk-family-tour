# The Real Diagon Alley?

Stand at the top of Victoria Street and look down. The street curves away below you like a slide, and every shopfront is painted a different color. Red, blue, yellow, green, all leaning together down the slope. There's even a walkway running along the rooftops on one side, so the street has two levels, like shops stacked on shops. Half the people around you are probably taking photos, and lots of them are thinking exactly the same thing. Diagon Alley.

Play the game yourself. Can you spot a shop that could sell wands? At number forty there really is a wizard shop, stuffed with scarves and robes and chocolate frogs, usually with a queue of fans outside. For years, people say, there was a joke shop near the bottom of the street, and fans loved the idea that it was the model for the Weasley twins' shop. A curved, colorful, magical-looking street with a joke shop on it. It has to be Diagon Alley, right?

Time for the truth, and it's a shocker. A few years ago Rowling answered this question herself, straight out. No real street inspired Diagon Alley. It came out of her head. She said she'd never even seen Victoria Street when she invented Diagon Alley, and that she's never visited most of the places around the world that claim to be the inspiration. Fans were flabbergasted. Some argued with her. But she wrote the books, so she gets the final word.

Then why does everyone keep saying it? Because people love a treasure map. When a story is that famous, everybody wants the magic to be real, and findable, and photographable. So cities grow legends around beloved books the way trees grow moss. Shops put up signs, tour guides point, and the story gets bigger every year. It's not lying exactly. It's more like wishing out loud.

And here's a secret that makes Victoria Street better than any legend. It once had a real wizard. Long ago, this slope was a steep zigzag lane called the West Bow, and in the sixteen hundreds a man named Major Weir lived here. He seemed to be the holiest man in Edinburgh, until one day he confessed to being a wizard, and in sixteen seventy the city executed him for witchcraft. For many years afterward, people were too scared to live in his house, swearing they saw strange lights and heard his ghost. So forget Diagon Alley for a second. This street had its own wizard story three hundred years before Harry Potter was born. Look down the curve one more time. Real magic rumors, painted shops, and a mystery. Not bad for one little street.
