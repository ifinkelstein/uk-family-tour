# How to Read a Pub
The word pub is short for public house, and that is the whole idea in two words. Historically it was literally somebody's house, with the front room opened to the public, the keeper living upstairs, and the beer often brewed out the back. That is why a good pub still feels like a home rather than a shop, with its fireplace and its dog and its regulars in their usual corner, and why the person in charge is called the landlord or landlady.

The signs came from illiteracy. In the thirteen nineties, English law required alehouses to hang out a sign so the authorities could identify them, and since most customers couldn't read, the signs were pictures. A red lion, a white hart, a coach and horses, a king's head. Centuries later the pictures remain, swinging over the street, and once you start decoding them you can't stop. A Red Lion is usually old royal or noble heraldry. A Bishop's or King's anything points to an old landowner. A name like the Volunteer Arms remembers a local regiment.

The etiquette is simple but real. You order at the bar, you pay when you order, and nobody will ever come to your table, which baffles visitors who sit politely waiting for service that will never arrive. If you're in company, you buy rounds, each person taking a turn to fetch drinks for the table, and keeping honest track of whose turn it is matters more than money in Britain. And pubs are far more family-friendly than their reputation abroad suggests. At lunchtime and early evening, a decent pub is full of families eating fish and chips or a Sunday roast, kids with a lemonade or a ginger beer, dogs asleep by the fire. It is closer to a living room with a licence than to a bar.

Edinburgh has some of the finest pub interiors in Britain, and three are worth crossing town for. The Cafe Royal, tucked behind Princes Street, is a Victorian jewel box, with a great oval island bar, a compartmented ceiling, and six large Doulton tile murals of famous inventors, Watt, Faraday, Franklin, Caxton, Stephenson and Peel, made for an exhibition in 1886. Its oyster bar next door glows with stained-glass sportsmen. Around the corner, the Guildford Arms of 1896 hides one of the most extravagant painted ceilings you'll ever see in a room where people are drinking beer, run by the same family since it opened. The Bow Bar in Victoria Street you've already heard about in the whisky story, and its row of hand pumps is as good as its gantry.

But the one to plan around is the Sheep Heid Inn, in Duddingston village on the far side of Arthur's Seat, which you're climbing in a few days. The story goes there's been an inn on the spot since 1360, which would make it one of the oldest pubs in Scotland, though the building you'll actually sit in is mostly eighteenth century, so treat the date as legend with a good pedigree. Legend also says King James the Sixth stopped in often and once gifted the landlord a snuff box made from a ram's head. What is certainly real is the skittle alley out the back, built in the eighteen eighties and said to be the oldest surviving in Scotland, two ancient wooden lanes where you can bowl at pins while your lunch is cooked. Come down off the summit, walk into Duddingston, and you'll have earned it.
