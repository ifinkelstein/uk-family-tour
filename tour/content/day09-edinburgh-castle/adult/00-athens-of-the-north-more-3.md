# What to Notice in Edinburgh

Before you plunge into the castle, arm yourself with a few things to watch for, because Edinburgh rewards the visitor who reads the city as well as the sights.

First, the two towns. This castle esplanade is one of the best places in the whole city to see the split with your own eyes. Look north, across the valley where the railway and gardens now run, and you can pick out the New Town, its long straight streets and matching stone terraces marching in tidy rows. Then look at the ground falling away below the castle toward the palace at the far end, and you are looking down the spine of the Old Town, one long ridge with a maze of lanes tumbling off either side. Two ideas of how to build a city, side by side.

Second, the stone. Almost everything here, grand and humble alike, is built from the same local sandstone, so the whole city shares a family resemblance, a warm honey grey that turns near black where centuries of soot have settled. That shared material is part of why Edinburgh feels so unified even though it is really several different places stitched together.

Third, the vertical drama. Keep glancing up and down. Bridges here cross over streets rather than rivers, so a road at your feet may have another road passing far below it. Staircases plunge between levels. It is a city best understood in three dimensions.

And finally, keep an eye out for the thinkers and the storytellers. Over the coming days you will meet statues, plaques, and museums honoring the philosophers, scientists, and novelists this city produced, from the Enlightenment giants to the writers of Sherlock Holmes, Treasure Island, and a certain boy wizard. Edinburgh wears its brains and its imagination openly.

Keep all of this in the corner of your eye. The castle comes first, but everything you see today, the Royal Mile, the writers' haunts, the great green hill of Arthur's Seat, is a chapter in the same remarkable city.
