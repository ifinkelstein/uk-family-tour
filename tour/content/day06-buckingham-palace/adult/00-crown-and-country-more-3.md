# Crown and Country, and What to Watch For

Now that you hold the whole arrangement in your head, the crown that reigns and the Parliament that rules, the rest of the day arranges itself into a single story, and it is worth knowing what to watch for as we go.

Start right here, with the language of the crown. Everything about this palace speaks the vocabulary of symbol rather than power. The famous central balcony is where the royal family gathers at the great national moments, a coronation, a wedding, a jubilee, to be seen by the crowd, and being seen is the whole point. The guardsmen in scarlet are real soldiers of regiments with genuine battle honours, but their duty here is ceremonial, a piece of living heritage performed for the nation and for you. Watch how much of it is about continuity, the sense that this has always been so and will go on.

Then, as we move toward Westminster, feel the register change. The great clock tower most people call Big Ben rises over the Houses of Parliament, and that building, not this one, is where the country's decisions are genuinely fought out, in debate and vote and argument. A short walk away, off Whitehall, is a plain black door at number ten Downing Street, the working home of the prime minister, the person who actually runs the government. There is no gold coach there, no balcony, no bearskin caps. That contrast, between the splendour of the palace and the businesslike doors of Westminster and Whitehall, is the constitution made visible in stone.

And then we go underground, to the Churchill War Rooms, and the stakes of the whole arrangement become suddenly, breathtakingly real. Because in nineteen forty, this entire settlement, crown and Parliament and the freedom to argue at all, came within a hair of being extinguished, and a handful of people in a cramped cellar helped hold it together. Keep the balance in mind as we walk. The pomp you see this morning and the quiet corridors of power this afternoon are two faces of one very old, very deliberate answer to a hard question, who should be allowed to rule, and how do you stop them ruling too much.
