# The City That Smelled of Toast
Sniff the air. Nothing unusual today, probably. But if you had stood on this street a hundred years ago, the whole city would have smelled of warm toast and porridge. A sweet, cosy, biscuity smell drifting over the rooftops. That was the smell of malt being cooked, because Edinburgh, believe it or not, was one of the biggest beer-making cities in the whole world. Everyone remembers the whisky. Almost everyone has forgotten the beer.

It started nearly nine hundred years ago with monks. A Scottish king built a big abbey called Holyrood at the bottom of the Royal Mile, and the monks there brewed beer, the way monks did all over Europe. They had picked a lucky spot without knowing it. Deep under Edinburgh, water trickles through old stone and comes up through wells, and that water has minerals in it that happen to be perfect for brewing. Brewers later worked out exactly where the magic water ran, in a curving band right under the city, and they gave it a wonderful name. The charmed circle. If your brewery stood on the charmed circle, your beer came out better, so breweries squeezed onto that line like players fighting for the best spot in a game.

By Victorian times, Edinburgh had about forty breweries. Forty! Steam puffing, barrels rolling and rumbling over the cobbles on horse-drawn carts, and that toasty smell everywhere, all the time. The beers even had funny names. Scottish brewers named their beers after prices. A barrel of ordinary beer cost sixty shillings, which was old Scottish and English money, so the beer was called Sixty Shilling. Stronger beer cost more, so it was called Seventy Shilling, or Eighty Shilling. It's as if you invented a lemonade and just named it Three Pounds Fifty. And people still order it. You can walk into a pub here today and hear a grown-up ask for "an eighty bob," a beer named after its Victorian price tag. The strongest, darkest kind of all got the best name, though. They called it a wee heavy, which is Scots for a small mighty thing.

Bit by bit, the breweries closed down. Companies gobbled up other companies, buildings were knocked down, and the toast smell faded from the air. The very last of the old giants was called the Caledonian Brewery, and it kept brewing the old way until just a few years ago, boiling its beer in huge open copper kettles with real flames underneath, the last brewery in all of Britain still doing that. Then, in 2022, the company that owned it shut it down, after a hundred and fifty three years, and its buildings are being turned into houses. The people who worked there were heartbroken, and lots of the city was too.

But don't be too sad, because the story has a twist. All over Edinburgh, new little breweries have been popping up, small ones, started by people who love the old beer, filling barrels just like the monks and the Victorians did. The city that smelled of toast is quietly warming up the toaster again.
