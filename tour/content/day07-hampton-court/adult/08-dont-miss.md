# Don't Miss These: A Checklist Walk
Hampton Court punishes the unprepared with its sheer size, so here is the connoisseur's checklist, one hook per stop. Inside first. The Great Hall, for the last great medieval hammer-beam roof built for an English king, and the Abraham tapestries beneath it, woven with real gold that still glints on a bright day. The Chapel Royal, for one long look up at the blue and gold vault, the finest surviving Tudor ceiling in existence. The Haunted Gallery, walked slowly, as the processional route it actually was. The Tudor kitchens, best if the fire is lit and the costumed cooks are in. Then three rooms most visitors miss entirely. The Wolsey Closet, a single small chamber that is the closest anyone can now get to the cardinal's vanished palace. The Council Chamber, an unassuming room where the Privy Council took decisions that broke England from Rome and destroyed two queens. And, most days from Wednesday to Saturday, the re-created crown of Henry the Eighth in the Royal Pew above the chapel, the ghost of regalia Parliament melted down in 1649. From there, climb the King's Staircase, Verrio's baroque propaganda machine, into William the Third's apartments, where the Guard Chamber's walls are dressed with more than three thousand muskets, pistols and swords arranged into fans and sunbursts, an armory turned into wallpaper. Give Mantegna's Triumphs of Caesar ten unhurried minutes, nine canvases that are arguably the greatest Renaissance paintings in Britain, and if energy allows, the Cumberland Art Gallery beyond, where the Royal Collection rotates genuinely first-rank pictures through a quiet suite. Outdoors, the obligatory pair, the Maze and, for the children, the Magic Garden on Henry's old tiltyard. Then the quieter set pieces. Fountain Court, Wren's cloistered square at the heart of the baroque palace, best seen by simply walking its full circuit. The Privy Garden, the king's private parterre reconstructed exactly to its 1702 design, the finest baroque garden resurrection in the country. The little Pond Gardens next door, sunken and impossibly pretty, which began as Tudor fish ponds holding the freshwater course of Henry's dinners. The Great Vine, planted in 1768 and now the largest grapevine in the world, still cropping heavily every year in its private glasshouse. And the royal tennis court, where real tennis, the medieval ancestor of the modern game, is still played on a court built for Charles the First; if a match is on, stop and watch a few baffling points. Nobody collects the whole list, and the palace rewards surrender rather than completism. Pick the rooms that pull you, take the gardens at strolling pace, and let at least one thing, the roof, the crown, the vine, stop you completely.
