# Why They Smell So Different
Here is a puzzle. Whisky is made from just grain and water. So why does one kind smell like apples and honey, and another smells like a smoky bonfire on a beach? The answer is that whisky tastes of the place it comes from, and Scotland has five different whisky lands, each with its own flavour.

Most whisky comes from a green valley in the northeast called Speyside, and those tend to smell soft and fruity, like apples and pears and honey. Way down south, near England, the whiskies are gentle and grassy. Up and across the huge Highlands they can be almost anything. But the most exciting land of all is a small, windy island in the west called Islay, and you say it "eye-la." Islay is made of that squishy peat we talked about, and its whiskies smell of smoke and sea and seaweed, so strong that grown-ups either love them like crazy or pull a hilarious scrunched-up face on their very first sniff.

Now, you are not going to taste any of this, because whisky is only for grown-ups. But you can do the fun part, the smelling, and here comes the cool science bit. Most of what we call "taste" is really smell. It is true. Your tongue only knows a few things on its own, like sweet and salty and sour. Everything else, all the apple and smoke and vanilla, your nose is doing that, sniffing tiny invisible bits of smell floating up in the air. That is why, when you have a stuffed-up nose from a cold, your food tastes of almost nothing. Try it sometime. Hold your nose and eat something, and it goes flat and dull. Then let go, and whoosh, all the flavour comes rushing back.

So when grown-ups sniff a whisky, swirling it and sticking their nose right into the glass, they are really being smell detectives, hunting for apples, smoke, honey, orange, even chocolate, all hiding in one golden glass. And in a place like the Scotch Whisky Experience, you can be a smell detective too, sniffing little pots of aromas and shouting out what you find. No tasting needed. Your nose does the whole adventure.
