# The City Where an Emperor Was Made
Try to picture Roman York not as a ruin but as a working city, because that is what it was for more than three centuries. A fortress the size of a small town sat on the high ground, home to roughly five thousand soldiers of a single legion, with stone barracks, bath houses, granaries and a headquarters building whose foundations still lie directly beneath the Minster. Across the river a proper civilian city grew up, with merchants, temples, a marketplace and the villas of the wealthy, important enough that Rome eventually named it the capital of the northern half of Roman Britain and granted it the rank of colonia, the highest status a Roman city could hold.

The name, by the way, may be the oldest thing here. Eboracum seems to come from an ancient British word for a place of yew trees, older than Rome itself, and it has quietly survived every conquest since, worn down over the centuries from Eboracum to Eoforwic to Jorvik to the single blunt syllable we use now, York.

This far-northern city kept pulling the whole empire toward it. Emperors came in person to run their wars against the tribes of what is now Scotland, and two of them died here. The second death is the one that mattered. In the year three hundred and six, the emperor Constantius died in York, and his soldiers, standing in this city, immediately proclaimed his son emperor on the spot. That son was Constantine, later called the Great, and within a few years he had legalized Christianity across the Roman world and founded a new capital, Constantinople, that would outlast Rome itself by a thousand years. It is not an exaggeration to say that one of the hinge moments of Western history happened in a garrison town in northern England. There is a statue of him now, lounging with his broken sword outside the Minster, a few steps from where the acclamation is thought to have taken place. Give him a nod. Very few provincial cities can say they manufactured an emperor who changed the world.
