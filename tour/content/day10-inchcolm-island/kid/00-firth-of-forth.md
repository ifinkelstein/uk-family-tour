# Where the River Turns Into the Sea
Look out at the water all around your boat. It looks like the sea, doesn't it? Big and grey and salty, with waves and seabirds. But here is a secret. This isn't quite the sea, and it isn't quite a river either. It's something in between, and it even has its own special name. It's called a firth.

Here is what that means. A river is born high up in the hills, small enough to jump across. As it flows it grows and grows, until near the very end it gets so enormous, and so wide, and so full of salty water from the ocean sneaking in, that it stops being a river at all and turns into this. A firth. A firth is the giant open mouth of a river, wide enough to swallow the sea. And this one is called the Firth of Forth.

The funny thing is, if you could stand here two thousand years ago, the water would look almost exactly the same. This firth is old, older than any castle, older than any king. And for all that time it has been like a giant watery road. Kings sailed across it. Armies were scared of it. Monks rowed little boats out to hidden islands. Ships carried wool and coal and wine up and down it. The whole city of Edinburgh grew up right beside it, because this was the best road in Scotland, and it was made entirely of water.

So today you are not just going to an island. You are sailing on one of the most important roads in the whole country. Feel the boat rock under you. Taste the salt in the air. And keep looking, because this watery road is absolutely stuffed with secrets, and I am going to show them to you one by one.
