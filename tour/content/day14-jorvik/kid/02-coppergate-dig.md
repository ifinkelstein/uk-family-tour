# The Muddy Miracle Under Your Feet
Why does York know so much about the Vikings? Because of magic mud. In 1976, before a shopping center was built at Coppergate — "street of the cup-makers" in Old Norse — archaeologists got five years to dig. What they found amazed the world. The ground here is wet, oxygen-poor clay, and things that normally rot away — wood, leather, cloth, plants, even insects — had survived for a thousand years. The archaeologists found entire Viking house walls still standing waist-high, fences, workshops, forty thousand objects in all: shoes, socks, combs carved from antler, ice skates made of horse bones, board games, padlocks, pans — the complete everyday life of ordinary Viking families, not kings. They even found thousand-year-old fleas, and eggs of the stomach worms that lived inside actual Vikings, which tells scientists the Vikings had... pretty rumbly tummies. The dig became famous; thousands of people queued to watch. When it ended, York built the JORVIK Centre right on top, and in the glass-floored gallery you can look down at some of the real preserved timbers, exactly where they were found. Archaeologists say a single cubic meter of Coppergate mud holds more history than a treasure chest of gold. Though they would probably also accept the gold.
