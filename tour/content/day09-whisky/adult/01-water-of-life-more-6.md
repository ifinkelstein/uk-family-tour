# The Science of Whisky
If the making of whisky sounds like magic, it is really chemistry, and rather beautiful chemistry at that. Follow what is actually happening and every step makes plain sense.

Start with fermentation. Yeast is alive, a microbe, and like any living thing it eats and gives off waste. Feed it sugar and it produces two things, alcohol and carbon dioxide, the very gas that fizzes in a fizzy drink. That is the trick behind every alcoholic drink on earth. The sugars from the barley go in, the yeast gets busy, and out come alcohol and a great roaring head of bubbles. Nothing more mysterious than that.

Then distillation, which is only a way of separating things with heat. Alcohol boils at seventy-eight degrees, water at a hundred. So if you warm the fermented liquid gently, the alcohol turns to vapour and rises before most of the water does. Catch that vapour, cool it back to a liquid, and you have pulled the alcohol away from the water, stronger than before. That is all a still is. A copper kettle for catching the right steam.

If it were only alcohol and water, though, it would taste of nothing. The flavour lives in tiny trace compounds that ride along with the alcohol, and distillers call them congeners. Two families matter most. Esters are the fruity, flowery smells, pear and apple and cut grass, made mostly in the still. Phenols are the smoky, medicinal ones, and they come from the peat smoke driven into the barley right at the start. More peat, more phenols, more bonfire. So when you catch fruit or smoke in a glass, you are smelling real molecules with real names.

The great change, though, happens in the barrel, and the hero is oak. Fresh off the still the spirit is clear and harsh. Years in a cask turn it into something else entirely, and the wood does three jobs at once. It gives colour and sweetness, above all where the barrel has been charred, burnt black inside, because that toasted layer breaks down a substance in the wood called lignin and frees vanillin, the same molecule that gives vanilla its scent. It cleans, because that char works like the charcoal in a water filter, quietly pulling out the young spirit's harsh, sulphury notes. And it breathes, because oak is porous, so a little air seeps in year upon year and softens the whisky, while a little whisky seeps out and drifts off into the air. That escaping share, about two percent a year, is the famous angels' share.

One last piece of science, the trick every enthusiast swears by. Add a drop of water. It sounds mad, to water down something you have waited twenty years for, but there is hard chemistry behind it. Researchers ran the simulations and found that in strong whisky the flavour molecules, one of them named guaiacol, hide down in the liquid, pinned there by the high alcohol. Add a splash of water, drop the alcohol a touch, and those molecules rise to the surface and leap into the air, straight up your nose. The water does not weaken the whisky. It sets the smells loose.
