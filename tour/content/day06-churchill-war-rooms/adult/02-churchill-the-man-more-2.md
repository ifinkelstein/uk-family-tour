# Cigars, Siren Suits, and the Long Goodbye
The private Churchill was a gift to caricaturists and a trial to his staff. He was rarely without a cigar, kept eccentric hours, working late into the night and napping in the afternoon, and conducted a startling amount of business from his bed, propped against pillows amid a blizzard of papers. He designed his own one-piece zip-up garment, the siren suit, which he wore without a flicker of embarrassment to meetings and even, on occasion, to dine with kings. He drank steadily, wept easily, and could be monstrously demanding of those around him. Genius and self-indulgence were tangled together in him inseparably.

The greatest surprise of his career came at the very moment of triumph. In July 1945, with Germany defeated, the British electorate turned him out of office in a landslide, voting for Labour's promise of social reform. It remains one of democracy's most bracing lessons, that gratitude and votes are different currencies. Churchill was wounded but undaunted. He led the Opposition, wrote his monumental history of the war, and in 1953 was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature, an almost unheard-of honour for a serving politician. That same period saw him back in Downing Street for a second premiership, from 1951 to 1955, by then ageing and increasingly frail.

When he died in January 1965, at the age of ninety, Britain gave him a state funeral of a scale normally reserved for monarchs, watched by millions on television and along the streets, the cranes on the Thames dipping in salute as his coffin passed. It was a farewell to more than a man. It was a farewell to Britain's imperial century. Keep all of this in mind down here, where the legend was still being forged, and where the ending was very far from certain.
