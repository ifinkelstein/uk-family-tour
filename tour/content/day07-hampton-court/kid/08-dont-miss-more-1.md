# The Tiny Golden Room That Survived
Remember how this whole palace once belonged to Cardinal Wolsey, before the king took it? Here is a strange question. If Wolsey built such an amazing palace, where did it all go? The answer is, it mostly vanished. Henry rebuilt it, later kings rebuilt it again, and Wolsey's famous golden rooms, the ones that made visitors gasp five hundred years ago, were pulled apart, painted over, and lost. All except, more or less, one. It is called the Wolsey Closet, and closet back then just meant a small private room, not a cupboard for coats. It is tiny, about the size of a garden shed, and most visitors walk straight past the door without ever knowing it is there. Do not be most visitors. Step inside and look up first, because the ceiling is the treasure, a whole sky of golden squares and circles locked together like a puzzle, decorated with roses and feathers, the badges of the Tudor family. It looks like it must be solid carved gold. Now for the secret, because scientists actually tested it. That gorgeous ceiling is not gold at all underneath. It is made from a paste of fine sawdust and crushed-up brick dust, mixed with glue boiled from animal skins, squished into moulds like cookie dough, stuck up there, and then painted gold. Tudor craftsmen were brilliant fakers, and honestly, isn't that cleverer than real gold? Around the top of the walls runs Wolsey's own motto, written in Latin, which means, the Lord is my helper. And on the walls hang old paintings telling the Easter story. Those paintings hide a mystery too. About sixty years ago, experts cleaning them discovered completely different, even older paintings hiding underneath, pictures that had been painted over about five hundred years ago and forgotten. Imagine being the person who found that. One warning before you go hunting. This little room is a bit of a jigsaw. Bits of it were gathered up and pieced back together long after Wolsey died, so nobody is completely sure it looked exactly like this. But every piece is genuinely Tudor, and standing in there is the closest anyone alive can get to Wolsey's lost palace. Here is the game. Stand in the middle, turn slowly all the way around, and imagine a thousand rooms decorated like this one. That is roughly what Wolsey built. And when Henry saw it, he wanted it so badly he took the whole thing. One tiny golden room is all that is left to show you why.
