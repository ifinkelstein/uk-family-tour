# The Palace Was a Whole Town
Try this. Stand in one of the big courtyards, close your eyes for three seconds, and instead of tourists, fill it with workers. Because this palace was never just a home for a king. It was a whole working town, with hundreds and hundreds of people living their entire lives around one family. You already know about the cooks. But think about everything else a town needs. Somebody had to wash the clothes. The laundresses were some of the only women workers in the whole palace, and washing the king's own shirts was a real official job held by the king's own laundress, a position of serious trust, because you did not let just anybody near the royal underwear. Somebody had to mow, dig and prune, so there were teams of gardeners keeping the orchards, the flower beds and the fish ponds. Somebody had to drive the taxis, and the taxis were boats, rowed by watermen who ferried people and messages up and down the Thames all day, timing everything by the tide. There were porters guarding the gates like security guards, deciding who got in. There were brewers, bakers, candle keepers, mole catchers, rat catchers, and boys whose whole job was running messages from one end of the enormous building to the other. Now, who paid them all? The palace had its own bank and head office, called the Board of Green Cloth, and yes, it was named after an actual table covered in green cloth. At that green table, officials counted everything coming in, the meat, the ale, the firewood, the candles, and everything going out, including everybody's wages. Working here came with a deal called bouche of court, which means mouth at court, your daily allowance of food, drink, candles and firewood. The fancier your job, the better your allowance. A gentleman might get wine and bright beeswax candles. A kitchen boy got weak ale and smoky cheap candles made of animal fat. Even light had a rank. And where did everyone sleep? The important people got proper rooms, and the better your room, the more everyone respected you. The rest squeezed in wherever they could, in attics, in shared beds, or curled up on the warm kitchen floor right where they worked. So here is your mission for the rest of the visit. Every time you walk through a grand empty room, add the missing people back in. A boy with a message. A laundress with a basket. A porter yawning at his gate. The king got the glory, but the town did the work, and today, you are walking down its streets.
