# Robert Burns, Scotland's Voice

Start with a test. Have you ever, at midnight on New Year's Eve, crossed your arms, joined hands with strangers and sung a song about old acquaintance being forgot, without having the faintest idea what the words mean? Then you have sung Robert Burns. Auld Lang Syne is his, or at least his making, and it is sung every New Year in more countries than almost any song on earth. So is the line, tender and battered by a thousand greetings cards, my love is like a red, red rose. Burns is that rare thing, a poet whose words have escaped the page entirely and become something people simply do, without knowing whose they are.

He is Scotland's national poet, its Bard, and the reason is not only that he was brilliant, which he was, but that he wrote in Scots, the everyday tongue of ordinary Scottish people, at a moment when polite society was busy trying to sound properly English. Burns took the language of the farmyard and the tavern and the kitchen and made great poetry out of it, and in doing so he told Scotland that its own voice was worth hearing. He was born in seventeen fifty-nine, the son of a struggling tenant farmer in Ayrshire, and he did much of his best work with mud on his boots.

In the winter of seventeen eighty-six he arrived in Edinburgh, twenty-seven years old, with one slim book of poems behind him, and the city lost its head over him. For a single dazzling season the ploughman poet was the toast of Edinburgh's grand drawing rooms, dined and feted by dukes and professors, and a new expanded edition of his poems was published right here in seventeen eighty-seven. It made his name across the whole country, though it never made him rich.

One of his songs you will hear echoing on another day of this trip. Burns wrote Scots Wha Hae, an imagined battle speech in the mouth of Robert the Bruce, rousing his men before they fought the English at Bannockburn. When you stand near Stirling and look out over that ground, remember that the most famous version of Bruce's speech was written not in the fourteenth century but by a farmer poet four hundred years later. That is the power of Burns. He gave Scotland the words for its own history.

Every year on the twenty-fifth of January, the day he was born, Scots the world over sit down to a Burns Supper. A haggis is carried in to the skirl of the bagpipes, someone recites Burns's mock-heroic Address to a Haggis to the steaming plate, and there are toasts and songs and rather a lot of whisky. No other poet on earth gets a global dinner party thrown in his honour every single year. Burns died young, at thirty-seven, worn out by hard living. But his voice never has.
