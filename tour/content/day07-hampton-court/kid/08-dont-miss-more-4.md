# The Crown That Was Turned into Coins
Up in the royal balcony of the chapel, you can stand right next to Henry the Eighth's crown, close enough to count the jewels. But wait. If that is Henry's crown, why isn't it locked up with the Crown Jewels in the Tower of London, behind thick glass and guards? Here is the twist. It cannot be, because the real one no longer exists, and the story of how it vanished is one of the wildest in English history. The real crown was a monster of a thing, made of gold weighing about as much as a big bag of sugar, blazing with sapphires, rubies, emeralds and pearls, well over three hundred stones in all. Henry wore it on the grandest days of the year. Every sixth of January, the feast of the three kings who brought gifts to baby Jesus, Henry would put on the crown and process through the palace to this very chapel, carrying gold, frankincense and myrrh, as if he were a fourth king joining the story. Look closely at the crown and you will spot tiny golden statues standing in it, small as toy soldiers. They hide a secret. Originally some of those figures were images of Christ. But after Henry quarrelled with the Pope and made himself head of his own church, he had them swapped for three English kings who were saints, as if to say, in this country, kings answer to nobody. Even his hat was an argument. Then, about a hundred years after Henry died, England had a revolution. The country fought a civil war, Parliament won, King Charles the First had his head cut off, and the winners decided to get rid of kings altogether, including their treasure. In sixteen forty-nine, the order went out. The royal crowns were broken up and melted down, and Henry's magnificent crown went into the furnace. The gold was made into ordinary coins, the jewels were sold off one by one. Think about that. Somebody may have bought bread with a melted piece of the crown of England. The one you are looking at is a copy, but a serious one, made in twenty twelve by the Queen's own crown jeweller, who used old palace lists and paintings to put all three hundred and forty-four stones and pearls back in their right places. And one last treat. A few years ago, a man with a metal detector dug up a tiny golden king in a field, and some experts wonder if it once stood on the real crown, one little survivor of the fire. Nobody knows for sure. Keep an eye on your feet in muddy fields, just in case.
