# The Council Chamber: Government at the Table
It is easy to leave Hampton Court thinking of it purely as a pleasure palace, all feasting, tennis and tapestries, and to forget that wherever Henry went, the state went with him. The Council Chamber is the corrective, and it may be the most consequential ordinary-looking room in the building. Here the Privy Council met, the inner committee of the king's most trusted councillors, the executive government of Tudor England, and when the court was in residence it met almost daily, usually around midday, at a table in a room like this. The palace has dressed the chamber in blue and gold hangings patterned with Tudor roses and fleurs-de-lis, the lilies a standing reminder that English kings still formally claimed the throne of France, and the effect is deliberately theatrical, because these meetings were theatre of the most serious kind. Consider what was likely debated and agreed in council here during the fifteen thirties and forties. The break with Rome and the construction of a new Church of England with Henry at its head. The destruction of Anne Boleyn in fifteen thirty-six. The unpicking of the Anne of Cleves marriage in fifteen forty, a diplomatic reversal that also destroyed Thomas Cromwell, the minister who had arranged it. And in November fifteen forty-one, with the court in residence here, the catastrophe of Catherine Howard, whose alleged past and present indiscretions were investigated and prosecuted by exactly these councillors while the frightened queen was held in her rooms a few corridors away. What should strike you is the medium of all this violence. No battlefield, no mob, no drawn sword, just men around a table, depositions read aloud, memoranda drafted, warrants prepared for a signature. The Tudor revolution, the executions, the seizures of monastic land, the redefinition of what England believed, was processed as paperwork, and much of that paperwork moved through rooms like this one. There is a modern flavor to it that the grand state apartments lack. The presence chamber and the throne canopy dramatize majesty, but the Council Chamber shows you administration, the quiet machinery that turned a king's rage or desire into legal instruments with fatal consequences. Stand at the table and note how small the room feels for the weight it carried. A dozen or so men, ambitious, frightened of their master and of each other, arguing an agenda at midday. That was the government of England, and for the people whose names came up in the minutes, this modest chamber was the most dangerous room in the kingdom.
