# Why Every Street Ends in Gate

Here is a game you can play for the rest of the day, and once you know the secret you will spot it everywhere in York. Look at the street signs. Really look at them. Coppergate. Stonegate. Micklegate. Fossgate. Goodramgate. Notice anything? Almost all of them end in the same little word. Gate.

Now, you might think gate means a gate, like a gate in a fence that you swing open. But it does not, and this is the fun part. That word gate is actually a Viking word. In the language the Vikings spoke, the word for a street was gata. So when you say Coppergate, you are really saying Copper Street, and when the Vikings named these streets more than a thousand years ago, that name stuck and never, ever left. Every time you read a street sign ending in gate, you are reading a little bit of Viking language that has survived for a thousand years. Micklegate just means the big street, because mickle was their word for big.

And now the joke that catches everyone out, so see if you can remember it. In York, the streets are called gates. But the actual big stone gateways, the giant doorways in the city walls that you walk through to get into the old city, those are not called gates at all. They are called bars. Bootham Bar. Monk Bar. Micklegate Bar. So in this crazy, wonderful city, the gates are streets, and the bars are gates. Try saying that to a grown-up and watch their face. There is even a real street here with the silliest name in all of England. It is called Whip-Ma-Whop-Ma-Gate, it is the shortest street in the city, and nobody knows for certain what the name means. See if you can find it.

So why did all these Viking words stick around? Because after the Vikings took over, this whole half of England, the whole north and east, was actually run by the Vikings, under Viking rules and Viking law. People even had a name for it. They called it the Danelaw, which just means the law of the Danes, because a lot of the Vikings came from Denmark. For a while it really looked like England might turn into a Viking country for good. It took the English kings almost a hundred years to win it all back, and even after they did, the Viking words were stuck fast, in the streets, in the names, in the way people talked.

So the Vikings never really left York at all. They are hiding in plain sight, on every street sign, in a little word you will now notice everywhere. Gate. Gata. Street. Go on, start counting them.
