# The Wilderness and the Word
It is worth dwelling on Churchill's wilderness years, because they explain everything that followed. Through most of the 1930s he held no ministerial office and little influence. He sat on the back benches, warning in speech after speech that Nazi Germany was rearming for conquest and that Britain was dangerously unprepared. He was dismissed as a reckless old imperialist crying wolf. Had he died in 1939, history might remember him as a brilliant, erratic failure. It was only when his warnings came true that the wilderness became, in hindsight, a vindication.

His true weapon was rhetoric, and 1940 was its supreme test. Consider the sequence. On the thirteenth of May, in his first speech as Prime Minister, he offered the Commons nothing but blood, toil, tears and sweat, a bracing honesty that, paradoxically, inspired confidence. On the fourth of June, after the deliverance of Dunkirk, came we shall fight on the beaches, a cascade of defiance that turned a military disaster into a promise never to surrender. And on the eighteenth of June, facing the fall of France, he told the nation that if the Empire lasted a thousand years, people would still say this was their finest hour.

These were not off-the-cuff remarks. He laboured over them for hours, dictating and redrafting, testing the cadences aloud. Delivered in his growling, deliberate baritone and carried into every home by radio, they did real strategic work, holding a frightened population steady when there was little else to offer. The American broadcaster Ed Murrow said Churchill mobilised the English language and sent it into battle. Standing in the bunker from which much of that war was directed, you can appreciate how much of Britain's survival in 1940 rested on nothing more solid, and nothing more powerful, than words.
