# The Secret Highlands
While the cities blacked out and the firths filled with warships, the emptiest parts of Scotland were given the strangest jobs of the war. In nineteen forty-two the army took over Achnacarry Castle, seat of Cameron of Lochiel in Lochaber, and turned its grounds into the Commando Basic Training Centre, the toughest school in the British forces. Over twenty-five thousand men passed through it by the war's end, British commandos plus French, Dutch, Norwegian, Polish and American volunteers, and the course was deliberately brutal: speed marches through the hills with full kit, river crossings in winter, assault landings on the loch practised with live ammunition fired over the boats. It's often called the birthplace of modern special forces training, and the idea that elite soldiers should be forged somewhere cold, wet and mountainous has never really gone away. On the road north at Spean Bridge, on a hillside every recruit marched past from the railway station, the Commando Memorial now looks out over the training ground, three bronze soldiers gazing toward Ben Nevis. It's one of Scotland's most visited monuments, and one of its most moving.

The secret agents had their own hidden Scotland. The Special Operations Executive, Churchill's sabotage service, ran paramilitary schools in requisitioned shooting lodges around Arisaig on the west coast, where agents bound for occupied Europe learned demolition, silent killing and survival. And in the Cairngorms, near Aviemore, three lodges collectively known as Station Twenty-six housed the Norwegians of Kompani Linge, chosen precisely because the terrain matched home, mountains, high passes and deep winter snow. The men who trained there pulled off what many rate the single finest sabotage operation of the war. In February nineteen forty-three, a small team of Norwegian skiers who had prepared in Scotland dropped onto a frozen plateau, climbed down and up a gorge the Germans considered impassable, broke into the heavily guarded Vemork plant in Telemark, and blew up the equipment producing heavy water for Germany's atomic research, then skied away. Not one shot was fired, not one saboteur was lost, and the German nuclear programme never recovered its footing.

And then there's the night Scotland's war turned surreal. On the tenth of May, nineteen forty-one, a lone Messerschmitt crossed the Scottish coast at dusk, and out of it, when the fuel ran low, parachuted Rudolf Hess, Deputy Fuehrer of Nazi Germany, third man in the Reich. He had flown solo from Augsburg on a self-appointed mission to negotiate peace, aiming for the estate of the Duke of Hamilton, whom he believed, on almost no evidence, would broker a deal. He bailed out over the village of Eaglesham south of Glasgow, broke his ankle landing in a field, and was found by David McLean, the ploughman at Floors Farm, who marched him to the farmhouse cottage and sat him by the fire while the family offered the bewildered Deputy Fuehrer a cup of tea. The story goes that McLean confronted him armed with a pitchfork, a detail Scotland has never wanted to fact-check too hard. Hitler declared Hess insane. Churchill, told of the news at the cinema, reportedly finished watching the Marx Brothers first. Hess spent the rest of his life in prison, and historians still argue about what exactly he thought he was doing. The field at Eaglesham is still just a field, which somehow makes the story better.
