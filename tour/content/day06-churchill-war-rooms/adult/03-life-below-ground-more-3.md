# The People Who Ran It
Let us give the supporting cast their due, because the popular memory of this place collapses everything into one cigar-chewing figure, and the reality was a small underground community of clerks and typists and telephonists, most of them young, most of them women, sworn to a secrecy they carried for the rest of their lives.

Take Elizabeth Layton, one of Churchill's personal secretaries, who joined in May 1941 at the age of twenty-three. She had spent much of her childhood in Canada, and she answered what looked like an ordinary advertisement, only to find herself typing for the Prime Minister at the height of the Blitz. Working for Churchill was a peculiar ordeal, because he dictated directly and continuously, pacing or propped up in bed, expecting the words to appear finished, flawless and instantly, no drafts, no hesitation. And because he could not abide the clatter of a normal machine while he was composing, his typists used a silent typewriter, a noiseless Remington, so that the only sound in the room was his own rolling voice. Layton's very first encounter with him involved a small mistake and an immediate, withering displeasure. She learned fast, stayed for the rest of the war, and later wrote a candid, affectionate memoir that is one of our best windows into what daily life beside the great man actually felt like, the terror and the tedium and the flashes of unexpected kindness.

Around her worked the telephonists at the switchboard, connecting calls by hand, plug and socket, plug and socket, a physical relay for every urgent conversation passing in and out of the bunker at any hour. There were cipher officers turning gibberish into orders, clerks, cooks, orderlies, and the Royal Marines who stood guard. They endured conditions that were genuinely wretched. The ventilation was poor and the air foul with smoke and the smell of too many people in too little space. Rats were a persistent and unpleasant reality. Rations were plain and monotonous, the same institutional food that fed the rest of rationed Britain, taken in a cramped canteen with no daylight to tell you whether it was breakfast or supper.

And yet the work that flowed out of this drab, airless warren was of the highest consequence, and one small daily ritual captures that better than anything. Every single morning, the Map Room staff distilled the whole night's events across every theatre of the war into a single concise summary, completed before nine o'clock. That report went not only to the Chiefs of Staff and to Churchill, but to the King himself, so that George the Sixth began each day with an accurate picture of exactly how his war was going. Think of the discipline that demands, night after night, year after year, no matter how catastrophic the news, an unbroken thread of clear, honest information running from these anonymous young officers straight to the monarch's breakfast table.

So as you move through these rooms, calibrate your attention. The furniture that matters most is not Churchill's chair but the typists' desks, the switchboard, the bunks. The people who sat at them won no medals and gave no speeches, kept their secrets to the grave, and formed the quiet human machinery without which none of the famous words would ever have reached the world.
