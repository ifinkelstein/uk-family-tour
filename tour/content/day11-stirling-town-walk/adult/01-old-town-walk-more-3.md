# Two Great Houses: Mar's Wark and Argyll's Lodging
Near the top of the town, two grand houses tell you how Scotland's nobles lived when they were not inside the castle. The great and the powerful needed to be close to the king, but a royal fortress is a cramped and drafty place to stay. So the richest built themselves town palaces just outside the gates, and two of them survive here.

The first, and you cannot possibly miss it, is Mar's Wark, that enormous, ornate stone facade straddling the top of the street. Wark simply means work, or building. It was raised around 1570 by John Erskine, Earl of Mar, who held one of the great hereditary jobs in all of Scotland, keeper of Stirling Castle. His family guarded the fortress and helped raise the royal children inside it for generations. So Mar built himself a showpiece right at the castle gate, dripping with carved figures and coats of arms, deliberately echoing the Renaissance palace up the hill. The story goes that he pinched much of the stone from a nearby abbey that had fallen out of use, and you can still spot a carved cross in the masonry that seems to back the tale up. It was never quite finished, war and politics got in the way, and today only that magnificent front wall remains, like a stage set with almost nothing behind it.

Just a little way off is Argyll's Lodging, and this one is complete, the finest surviving town house of its era anywhere in Scotland. Walk into its courtyard and you step straight into the seventeenth century. Its grandest owner was Sir William Alexander, a poet and courtier who was made Earl of Stirling, and who lavished a fortune turning it into a private palace fit to receive the king, Charles the First. It very nearly ruined him, and he died deep in debt. Later the house passed to the powerful Campbell Earls of Argyll, whose name it still carries today.

Between them, these two houses make the point beautifully. Up the hill, the crown lived in stone and splendour. Down here, the nobles built their own smaller splendours as close to the throne as they could possibly get, because in that world, nearness to the king was everything.
