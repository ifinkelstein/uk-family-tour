# The Cafes, the Legend, and What Rowling Actually Said

The most contested piece of real estate in Potter tourism is a cafe window. For years, The Elephant House on George IV Bridge displayed a sign reading Birthplace of Harry Potter, and for years, thousands of fans photographed it, queued for it, and wrote their devotions on the walls of its toilets, which became a shrine of graffiti in a dozen languages. It's a lovely cafe with a knockout view of the castle from the back room. It is also, on the birthplace claim, wrong, and we know because Rowling finally said so herself.

Her correction, posted on Twitter in twenty twenty, was gentler than it might have been. She'd been writing Potter for several years before she ever set foot in the place, she said, so it's not the birthplace, but she did write there, so, in her words, we'll let them off. The actual conception is well documented. The idea arrived fully formed in nineteen ninety, on a delayed Manchester to London train, and the earliest chapters were written in London, in a flat in Clapham. By the time she reached Edinburgh in late nineteen ninety-three, Harry, Ron and Hermione already existed.

The cafe that has the strongest genuine claim barely gets tourists at all, because it no longer exists. Nicolson's Cafe occupied a first-floor room at the corner of Nicolson Street and Drummond Street, and it was co-owned by Rowling's brother-in-law, a man named Roger Moore, no relation to James Bond. That family connection mattered. A woman nursing a single espresso for two hours while filling notebooks is a poor customer anywhere else. Here, nobody minded. Large portions of Philosopher's Stone were written at those tables, baby asleep alongside. The premises have since cycled through several restaurants, most recently Chinese, but a plaque on the Drummond Street corner marks the spot. When Nicolson's later became a bistro called Spoon, Rowling fans kept coming anyway, which tells you something about the gravitational pull of this story.

As for the Elephant House, fate handed it a dramatic third act. In August twenty twenty-one, a fire that started in a neighboring building gutted the cafe, and it stayed shut for more than four years while the block was rebuilt. The table Rowling wrote at survived, was restored, and when the cafe finally reopened in late December twenty twenty-five, it went back into the room where she used to sit, beneath a portrait of her that now hangs alongside Ian Rankin, Alexander McCall Smith and Irvine Welsh, three more writers who worked in that same room over the years. Which is the honest version of the cafe's claim, and frankly the better one. Not the birthplace of Harry Potter, but a genuine working cafe of the Edinburgh book trade, where at least four internationally bestselling authors have sat scribbling over coffee. The marketing sign was a stretch. The room itself never needed it.
