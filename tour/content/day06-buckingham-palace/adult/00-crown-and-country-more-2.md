# How the Kings Lost Their Kingdom

For most of human history, and for a long stretch of England's, a king simply ruled. His word was law, he raised the taxes, he made the wars, and if you objected, that was your misfortune. The story of how that raw royal power drained away, slowly, over some eight hundred years, into an elected Parliament, is really the story of modern Britain, and it has three great turning points worth carrying in your head today.

The first is a meadow by the Thames called Runnymede, in the year twelve fifteen. A thoroughly unpopular king named John had lost wars in France and taxed his barons to the bone, and the barons, in armour, finally cornered him and forced him to seal a document we now call Magna Carta, the Great Charter. Its most explosive idea was simple. The king himself was not above the law. He could not seize a free man's property or lock him up on a whim, and he could not raise certain taxes without consulting his leading subjects. It was a deal between a king and his aristocrats, not democracy in any modern sense, but the principle it planted, that even the crown must obey the law, never quite died.

The second turning point was far bloodier. In the sixteen forties, a long quarrel between King Charles the First and Parliament over money and power boiled into outright civil war. Parliament won. In sixteen forty nine it did the once unthinkable, putting its own king on trial and cutting off his head, and for about eleven years England had no monarch at all. The monarchy was eventually restored, but the lesson had been carved in blood, a king who defied Parliament could lose everything. A few decades later, in sixteen eighty eight, Parliament calmly swapped one king for another and made him sign a Bill of Rights, settling for good that the crown ruled only with Parliament's consent.

The third change was the quietest and took the longest. Across the eighteen hundreds and into the twentieth century, the right to vote spread, reform by reform, from a tiny sliver of wealthy men outward to almost every adult in the land. As power flowed to the voters and their Parliament, the monarch's real authority quietly ebbed to almost nothing, until the crown became what you see today, immensely dignified, deeply loved by many, and politically all but powerless. That is the strange, hard won balance we will spend the day walking through.
