# Smugglers in the Hills
Do you want a story with a real villain, secret hideouts, and a famous poet who was sneakily on the wrong side? Then let me tell you about the whisky smugglers.

First, how old is whisky? Very, very old. The oldest writing we have about it comes from the year fourteen ninety-four, more than five hundred years ago, when a king's helper wrote down that a monk named Friar John Cor was given a big pile of grain to make "the water of life." A monk! And he was handed enough grain to make loads of it, which means people already knew exactly how to do it, so it must be even older than that. Nobody knows who made the very first drop.

For hundreds of years, ordinary Scottish farmers made whisky in their own cottages, and that was fine. But then the government did something people hated. They put a tax on it, which meant every time you made whisky you had to hand some money to the king. So what did the Scots do? They started making it in secret. All over the hills and glens, people hid little whisky machines in caves, in barns, under the heather, and worked at night so nobody would spot the smoke. Then they smuggled the whisky out, sometimes in special barrels shaped to hide under a lady's coat so she looked a bit round. Sneaky.

But the king sent men to catch them. These taxmen were called excisemen, or "gaugers," and they were the most hated men in all of Scotland. Everybody tried to trick them. And now for the twist. Scotland's most famous poet ever, the man who wrote the song people sing on New Year's Eve, guess what his job was? He was a gauger. A taxman! Robert Burns rode around on his horse measuring people's whisky barrels. But secretly, I think, he liked the smugglers, because he wrote a funny poem about the Devil coming to carry the taxman away, and everyone cheered.

In the end, in the year eighteen twenty-three, the king made a new rule. It made the tax much smaller and let people make whisky properly, out in the open. So the smugglers stopped hiding, built real whisky factories called distilleries, and started selling Scottish whisky all over the world, shipped out from the harbours right here near Edinburgh. That is how a secret drink from the hills became one of the most famous drinks on the planet. Not bad for the water of life.
