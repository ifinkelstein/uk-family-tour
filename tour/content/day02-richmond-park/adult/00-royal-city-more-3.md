# What to Notice Before We Set Off
Before we walk, let me tell you what to notice, because it is easy to treat a park as just a green blur between the interesting bits. First, the sheer improbability of where you are. You are only about eight or nine miles from the center of one of the world's great cities, close enough that on a clear day you can actually see it, and yet it feels like open countryside, all rough grass and bracken and huge, low, ancient oak trees. Some of those oaks are older than the park itself, older than the United States, standing here quietly while whole empires came and went.

Second, notice the greenness, and understand where it came from. London is one of the greenest major cities on the planet, and that is not a happy accident of clever town planning. It is largely an accident of monarchy. Kings and queens seized great sweeps of land for hunting and for pleasure, and centuries later, when the city swelled up all around them, those royal grounds became the public parks that let London breathe. The crown's old playgrounds are now everyone's back garden. You will feel that same pattern again and again in this city.

Third, use your senses. Listen for skylarks somewhere overhead, tiny birds that pour out song while hovering in place, and for the wind moving through the bracken. Look at the way the land rolls in long open lawns, kept that way by centuries of grazing. And yes, keep half an eye out for the deer, always at a respectful distance, because they are the living reason all of this survived at all.

And here is one thing to look forward to. A little later we will climb a small rise where, if the day is clear, you can peer ten miles clear across the whole of London and catch a famous domed cathedral floating above the treetops, a place you will stand right beneath tomorrow. For now, though, just breathe in. This is your gentle, green front door into two thousand years of history. Let us begin.
