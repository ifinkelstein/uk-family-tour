# Reading the Street: What to Notice Today
Once you know the layers are there, the City turns into a treasure hunt, and you can read two thousand years off its street signs and stones. Start with the wall. The Romans built it around the year 200, and although most is buried or gone, real chunks survive above ground, most dramatically near the Tower, where a battered mass of Roman and medieval stonework stands beside the busy road at a spot called, helpfully, Tower Hill. There is even a street called simply London Wall, running along where the wall once ran. When you pass it, you are tracing a boundary two thousand years old.

Next, listen to the names. The City's street names are a medieval map that never got erased. Cheapside comes from the old word for a market. Bread Street, Milk Street, and Poultry are where those things were sold. Threadneedle Street, home of the Bank of England, may remember the needle makers. And the churches, so many churches, most of them dedicated to saints and tucked between the office blocks, mark parishes a thousand years old even when the building on top is new.

Then look up and look down at the same time. Above you are the glass giants the City loves to nickname, the Gherkin, the Cheesegrater, the Walkie Talkie. Below your feet, whenever builders dig a new foundation here, they hit Roman mosaics, medieval wells, plague pits, and the charred line left by the Great Fire. In 1988, digging for an office block, workers found a whole Roman amphitheatre under the Guildhall, and you can visit it in the basement, its outline painted in black on the modern square above.

So spend the day looking twice. The City wears all its ages at once, the Roman under the medieval under the Victorian under the glass. And it all slopes down toward the river, and toward the oldest, heaviest statement of power in the whole square mile, the fortress you will finish at, the Tower of London.
