# High and Low: A Castle Full of Lives
So far this castle has given you kings and queens, murders and coronations, alchemists and cannon. Let me now do something different. Let me stop the parade of famous events for a moment and ask a quieter question. What was daily life actually like for the people who filled this rock, all of them, from the very top of society to the very bottom?

Because a castle like Stirling was more than a stage for a handful of monarchs. It was the top of a ladder, and every rung of that ladder was a kind of person, each one leaning on the rung above and the rung below. At the very top stood the king or queen, whose single day we will follow first. Just beneath the throne stood the advisers and the great nobles, the few powerful families who ran the country and, if they were clever or lucky, grew rich by royal favour, and who could fall just as fast. Below them came the skilled craftsmen, the masons and carvers and painters whose clever hands actually built and decorated everything you have been admiring. And at the broad base of the ladder, holding the whole thing up, stood the ordinary people, the farmers in the fields below the walls, who were the vast majority of Scots and who paid for all the rest.

Every one of these lives touched this one castle. The farmer's grain became the king's bread. The mason's chisel cut the king's window. The noble's ambition decided who lived and who died. Over the next few short chapters I am going to walk you up and down that ladder, from a king's day to a peasant's supper, and introduce you to one more real person at the end. This is the human machinery underneath all the gold.
