# What Happened to Cardinal Wolsey
So Henry took this beautiful palace from Cardinal Wolsey. But what actually happened to Wolsey himself? His story is a proper rise and fall, and it is worth knowing, because it tells you exactly how dangerous it was to be the king's favourite.

Start with the rise. Wolsey was not born rich or royal. His father was a butcher in a town called Ipswich, an ordinary man who sold meat. And yet clever, hard-working Wolsey climbed all the way up to become a cardinal, a top churchman, and the most powerful person in England after the king. He ran the whole country for Henry. He was so grand that a visitor from Italy said you had to speak to Wolsey almost as though he were the king. Imagine, a butcher's son, building a palace finer than the king's own. For years, it worked beautifully.

Then came the fall, and it came from one problem Wolsey could not solve. Henry desperately wanted to end his first marriage, to Catherine of Aragon, so he could marry Anne Boleyn instead. Only the Pope in Rome could allow it, and Wolsey was told, sort it out, make it happen. But the Pope kept saying no, and no matter how hard Wolsey tried, he simply could not deliver what the king wanted. And a king who does not get what he wants stops being your friend very quickly.

In the year fifteen twenty-nine, Henry stripped Wolsey of his power and his palaces, including this one. Then Wolsey was accused of treason, the most serious crime of all, and ordered to travel to London to face the king's anger, probably to be locked in the Tower and executed. He never got there. On the journey south, old and exhausted and ill, he stopped at an abbey in Leicester, and there he died, in November fifteen thirty. And people remembered his sad last words, that if he had only served God as carefully as he had served the king, God would not have abandoned him now, in his old age. The man who built all this splendour died worn out and disgraced on a road, and his glittering palace simply carried on without him.
