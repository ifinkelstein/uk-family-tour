# Two Thousand Years, One Morning's Walk
Let me lay out the whole story fast, because you will be walking through all of it. It begins with the Romans. Around the year 47, a few years after the emperor Claudius invaded Britain, they laid out a town where the Thames was narrow enough to bridge and deep enough for ships. Londinium. It grew quickly, and then, in the year 60 or 61, a British queen named Boudica led a revolt, sacked the town, and burned it to the ground, killing everyone who had not fled. Archaeologists still find the scorched red layer she left in the soil. The Romans rebuilt, and around the year 200 they threw a great stone wall around the city, two and a half miles of it. That wall fixed the shape of London for the next thousand years and more, and you can still touch pieces of it.

When Rome pulled out, around the year 410, the town emptied and fell quiet. The Saxons who came after mostly settled just to the west, in a place they called Lundenwic. Then came the Vikings, raiding up the river in their longships, until King Alfred the Great pushed them out and moved his people back inside the safety of the old Roman walls, around the year 886. So the walled city filled up again.

Medieval London grew rich and stubborn behind those walls, a city of merchants that bargained with kings for its own rights and its own mayor, privileges it guards to this day. Then the Great Fire of 1666, which you will hear plenty about, wiped out four fifths of it in four days. They rebuilt in brick and stone, and Christopher Wren raised a new St Paul's over the ashes. The Blitz burned it a third time in 1940 and 1941. And out of that last ruin grew the modern City, a global banking capital of glass and steel where fewer than nine thousand people actually live, but half a million arrive to work each morning.

Romans, Saxons, Vikings, medieval merchants, Wren, the Blitz, the bankers. Six ages of the same square mile, and this morning you get to walk straight through all of them.
