# The Man Who Held the Bridge

This is one of the greatest true adventure stories in all of English history, and it happened just outside York, so listen closely.

In the year ten sixty-six, three different men all wanted to be King of England, and they were ready to fight for it. There was Harold, the English king, waiting nervously down south, watching the sea. There was William, a duke from France, building a fleet of ships to invade. And there was Harald Hardrada, the King of Norway, an absolute giant of a man, said to be nearly seven feet tall, who had adventured all the way to the great city of Constantinople and back, and who now sailed up the river to York with three hundred longships to grab England for himself.

At first it went brilliantly for the Vikings. They beat the local soldiers just outside York and marched in like conquerors. But King Harold was cleverer and faster than anyone expected. Way down south, he gathered his best warriors and marched them north at incredible speed, day and night, day and night, all the way up the country, and burst upon the Vikings so suddenly that many of them had left their heavy armour back on the ships, because it was a hot day and they thought they were safe.

And now the famous bit. As the English charged, the Vikings had to get across a narrow wooden bridge, and one single Viking warrior decided to save his friends. He was huge, and he had a great axe, and he planted himself right in the middle of that bridge and would not move. One man, against an entire army. The English came at him and he swung his axe and knocked them down, one after another after another, holding the whole bridge by himself while his friends got ready behind him. The old stories say he cut down as many as forty men before he fell. And how did they finally beat him? A sneaky English soldier floated underneath the bridge in a little barrel, poked his spear up through the gaps in the planks, and got him from below. Not exactly fair, but it worked.

After that the battle was over quickly, and King Harald Hardrada of Norway was killed by an arrow through the throat. The last great Viking army was finished. Of the three hundred ships that had come, so few Vikings were left alive that they only needed about twenty-four ships to sail home.

But here is the sad and astonishing ending. King Harold, the winner, barely had time to celebrate before terrible news arrived. William and his French army had landed in the south. So poor Harold had to turn his tired, wounded soldiers straight around and march all the way back down England again, and just three weeks later he fought William at the Battle of Hastings, and this time Harold lost, and was killed. And William became King of England.

And now the very best secret of all, that ties your whole holiday together. Remember the Tower of London, right at the start of your trip? William built that. And guess where William's family came from, long ago? They were Vikings too. Northmen who had settled in France a few generations earlier and become the Normans. So the Vikings did not really lose in ten sixty-six at all. One set of Vikings was beaten in a field outside York, and another set of Vikings, gone a bit French, won the whole of England and built the Tower where your adventure began. It was Vikings, all the way, from the very first day of your trip to this very last one.
