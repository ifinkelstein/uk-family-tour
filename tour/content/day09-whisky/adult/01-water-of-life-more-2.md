# A Map in a Glass
Here is a thing that delights whisky lovers. You can taste geography. Scotch is divided into five whisky regions, part official line, part old tradition, and each has a character of its own. Learn them, and a glass becomes a map.

The biggest by far is Speyside, gathered around the river Spey in the northeast, where more than half of Scotland's distilleries are crammed together. Speyside malts are the crowd-pleasers, elegant and fruity, apple and pear and honey, sometimes deepened with dried fruit from old sherry casks. Glenfiddich, Glenlivet, Macallan, all Speyside. Next comes the vast sprawling Highland region, which covers most of the map and so covers most of the moods too, from light and floral to big and muscular. The Lowlands, down toward England, tend to be the gentlest, soft and grassy, once nicknamed the breakfast whiskies, which tells you how easy they go down. Campbeltown is the curiosity, a single small town on a far western peninsula that once held over thirty distilleries and now keeps just a precious handful, making a salty, characterful dram all its own.

And then there is Islay. Say it "eye-la." A small windblown island in the west, made largely of peat, and its whiskies are the wild ones, drenched in smoke and sea salt and something almost medicinal, tasting of bonfires and seaweed and, some swear, hospital antiseptic, in the best possible way. People who love Islay love it fiercely. People meeting it for the first time sometimes wonder if their drink is on fire. Both reactions are correct.

One more distinction is worth knowing, because you will see it on every bottle. A single malt is the whisky of one single distillery, made only from malted barley. The proud individual. A blend, like Johnnie Walker or Famous Grouse, is a marriage of many whiskies from many distilleries, married for a smooth and steady taste, and it was blends that conquered the world. Neither is better than the other. One is a soloist. The other is a choir.

So how does a grown-up actually taste the stuff? Slowly, and with a bit of respect. You pour a small measure, a dram, into a glass that narrows at the top to gather the aromas. First you just smell it, nosing it gently, because most of what we call taste is really smell. Then a small sip, rolled around the mouth. And here is the trick the experts swear by. Add a drop or two of water. It sounds like heresy, but a little water opens the whisky up, releases hidden aromas, and softens the sting of the alcohol. Smell it again and it is a different drink. That is the whole pleasure of it. Not to drink much, but to notice much.
