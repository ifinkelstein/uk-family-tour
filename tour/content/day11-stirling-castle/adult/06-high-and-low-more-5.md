# Mary's Darling Nurse
To finish, let me introduce you to one more real person from this castle's golden age, someone you will not find in the usual roll of kings and villains, a working woman who spent her life closer to Scotland's royal children than almost anyone. Her name was Jean Sinclair, though the records also call her Janet, and she was the nurse of Mary, Queen of Scots.

In a royal household a nurse was a figure of real trust. Great ladies of the sixteenth century did not usually feed and mind their own babies, so that intimate, round-the-clock work was handed to chosen women, and the woman who did it could become one of the quietly most important people in a child's whole life. Jean Sinclair had already served the royal family before Mary was even born. She had been nurse to Mary's little half-brother, a Prince James who died young, so when the baby girl who would be queen arrived, Sinclair was the safe, experienced pair of hands she was placed into.

There is one small scene that brings her vividly to life. In March of fifteen forty three, when Mary was only a few months old, the English ambassador Ralph Sadler came to inspect the infant queen, because there were rumours she was sickly and might not live, and a sickly heir changed everything. It was Jean Sinclair who unwrapped the baby from her tight swaddling bands and showed the child off so that the ambassador could see for himself that she was, as he then reported home, a healthy and well-formed little girl. A nurse's steady hands, unwrapping a baby, quietly reassuring a watching kingdom.

That summer the household moved here to Stirling, and Sinclair helped raise the little queen behind these safe walls. Then, when the English wars grew too dangerous and it was decided to send Mary to France for safety, Jean Sinclair went too. She was part of the small royal household, only around thirty people, smuggled out of Scotland by ship and delivered to the French royal nursery, and she stayed at the child's side in a foreign country, far from everything she knew.

Her letters, which survive, show her to be no fairy-tale servant but a real, anxious, proud woman. She fretted about money, complained when she felt she was being left off the household's list of allowances, and insisted, with some dignity, that she came of honest folk and would not simply abandon her charge. For her long service she was rewarded with pensions and with grants of land, including ground at Cornton, close by this very castle. She died before Mary grew to be a woman, remembered warmly as her darling nurse.

I like ending on Jean Sinclair, because she is the whole ladder in a single life. She was born ordinary, and she rose, not by battle and not by scheming, but by patience, skill, and plain love, cradling the most important baby in Scotland. History gives the kings the statues. But it was women like her who actually kept the future alive.
