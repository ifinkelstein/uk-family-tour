# Tell Me More: The Grassmarket and Half-Hangit Maggie

Since you'll be having lunch in the Grassmarket, it's worth knowing what you're eating over. This long, handsome square below the castle rock is one of the most convivial spots in the city now, all pubs and terraces and Sunday crowds, but for the best part of two centuries it was Edinburgh's gallows ground. Public executions were held here until seventeen eighty-four, watched by crowds that could swell to twenty thousand, packing the square and hanging out of every window above it.

Look for a small memorial near the east end, set into the cobbles, marking the site of the gibbet, and a stone that remembers more than a hundred Covenanters hanged here between sixteen sixty-one and sixteen eighty-eight, in the years of religious persecution the Scots still call the Killing Time. Those were the men and women who wouldn't accept the king's bishops, the same movement whose National Covenant was signed up the hill, and many of them made their last speech from a ladder in this square. There's a pub at the corner cheerfully named The Last Drop, and yes, the name means exactly what you fear.

But the Grassmarket's best story is one where the gallows lost. In seventeen twenty-four a woman named Margaret Dickson, Maggie to everyone, was sentenced to hang here for concealing the death of her baby. She was duly hanged, cut down, pronounced dead, boxed in a coffin and loaded onto a cart to be carried home for burial. On the road, the story goes, the cart drivers stopped for a drink, and knocking noises began to come from inside the coffin. They prised the lid, and there was Maggie, alive, groggy, and extremely surprised. Now came the legal puzzle. Her sentence had been to hang, and she had hanged. The court decided it could not very well hang her twice for the one crime, especially as, being officially dead in the eyes of the law, she was arguably now a new woman entirely. She was set free, lived another forty years, and went about ever after as Half-Hangit Maggie.

There's a pub named for her in the square today, so you may well eat your lunch under her sign. Raise a chip to her. She is the one person in Edinburgh history who beat the Grassmarket gallows and lived to tell everyone about it, at length, for decades.
