# Meet Sophie and the Real T. rex
The museum has a roaring, moving T. rex that lunges and growls, and it is brilliantly scary. But it is a robot. So let me tell you what the real animal was actually like, and then introduce you to a real dinosaur skeleton you can meet here too.

Start with Tyrannosaurus rex. It was one of the biggest meat-eating animals ever to walk on land, up to about thirteen metres long, as long as a bus and a bit more. Its head alone was longer than you are tall, and its mouth was full of teeth the size of bananas, some as long as your forearm, with rough edges like steak knives. Scientists think T. rex had one of the most powerful bites of any land animal that has ever lived, strong enough to crunch straight through solid bone. Its little arms look silly, it is true, but they were not weak. They were short and thick and could probably curl a heavy weight. Scientists still argue about what they were for.

And here is something that would surprise the people who made that roaring robot. Many of T. rex's close cousins were covered in feathers, fluffy ones, more like a giant terrifying chicken than a scaly lizard. Some scientists think baby T. rex may have been fluffy too. Nobody knows for certain, which is exactly what makes it fun to think about.

Now come and meet Sophie. Sophie is a Stegosaurus, one of those dinosaurs with the row of tall bony plates down its back and the spikes on its tail. And Sophie is special, because she is the most complete Stegosaurus skeleton ever found anywhere in the world. When scientists dug her up, more than four out of every five of her bones were still there, over three hundred of them, which almost never happens. Most dinosaur skeletons in museums are missing lots of pieces, filled in with copies, but with Sophie, most of what you see is the real thing.

She went on show here in twenty fourteen, and she was the first brand-new, nearly complete dinosaur to go on display in this museum in almost a hundred years. Nobody even knows if Sophie was a boy or a girl. Those plates on her back may have helped her show off to other stegosaurs, or soak up the sun's warmth, or scare off attackers. So the museum gives you both, a robot T. rex to make you jump, and Sophie, real bones, standing quietly, a hundred and fifty million years old.
