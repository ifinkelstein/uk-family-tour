# The Duck That Broke the World Record
Let's talk about that record-breaking engine again, because the story gets better. The fastest steam locomotive in the history of the world was named after a duck. It's true. The engine was called Mallard, which is a kind of wild duck, the one with the shiny green head that you see on ponds. And Mallard wasn't the only bird in the family. The engineer who built these engines, Sir Nigel Gresley, gave a whole flock of them bird names. There was Bittern, and Kingfisher, and Wild Swan, and Seagull, and Golden Plover. A whole stable full of birds, all built for speed.

Why a duck for the fastest one? Because these engines were shaped to slip through the air like a bird in flight. Most steam engines back then were big and boxy and flat at the front, which is terrible for going fast, because the air pushes straight back against a flat wall. But Gresley shaped the front of Mallard into a smooth, rounded, sloping nose, like the front of a modern bullet train, so the air slid up and over it instead of shoving against it. They even tested the shape by blowing wind at little models, the way you'd test a paper airplane. And they painted Mallard a beautiful deep blue, so it looked as fast as it truly was.

On the third of July, in 1938, on a long gentle downhill slope not far south of where you started today, Mallard went thundering down the hill and hit one hundred twenty-six miles per hour. That beat the old record, which had belonged to a German engine, by about a mile and a half an hour. Here's the amazing part. That was almost ninety years ago, and no steam engine anywhere on Earth has ever gone faster since. Not one. The record has stood longer than your grandparents have been alive.

Think about how fast that really is. Going down the hill, Mallard's tall wheels were spinning round about five hundred times every minute, the fire was roaring, and the whole heavy iron machine was shaking and screaming. The men on board could actually smell hot metal, because a part deep inside the engine was beginning to melt from the sheer effort. They had pushed it right to the very edge, snatched the record, and eased off just in time before anything broke.

Mallard is still around today. It doesn't run anymore, but it sits, polished and blue and beautiful, in the National Railway Museum in the city of York, which your train passes through. If you ever go inside, you can stand right next to the actual duck that beat the whole world. Give a little quack as you pass York. Mallard has earned it.
