# Paint, Brick, and the Black Dog
There is a private Churchill worth knowing, the one who found ways to survive himself, and understanding him deepens everything you see in these rooms. Behind the cigar and the growl was a man who wrestled all his life with spells of crushing depression, which he called, with grim affection, his black dog. When the black dog was on him, the energy drained away and the world went grey, and a lesser man might have been swallowed by it. Churchill's answer was not to talk about it much. His answer was to work with his hands and his eyes until the darkness loosened its grip.

His great rescue was painting, and it arrived at his lowest ebb. In 1915, in the depths of the Gallipoli disgrace, out of office and consumed by failure, he picked up a paintbrush for the first time in middle age, almost by accident, and it seized him completely. He painted for the rest of his life, hundreds of canvases, mostly luminous landscapes drenched in the strong sunlight he loved. He wrote a wonderful little essay about it, arguing that painting was the perfect antidote to worry because it demanded total concentration and left no room in the mind for anything else. For a man whose mind was his greatest asset and his greatest tormentor, that was salvation. He even exhibited under a false name once, to be judged on the work rather than the fame.

He built things, too, literally. At Chartwell, his country home, he laid brick walls with his own hands, tens of thousands of bricks, and enjoyed it so much that he joined the bricklayers' union. Picture that, one of the most powerful men in the world, cigar clamped in his jaw, methodically buttering mortar and setting brick upon brick, because the rhythm of it quieted the storm in his head. And of course he wrote, prodigiously, millions of words of history and memoir, which paid his considerable bills and, not incidentally, ensured that he would get to tell his own story first. Writing, painting, bricklaying, these were not idle hobbies. They were survival tools, ballast against the black dog, the disciplines that kept a volatile, oversized personality from tearing itself apart.

Which brings us to the greatest test of his equilibrium, and one of the noblest moments of his life. In July 1945, weeks after leading his country to victory, the British people voted him out of office in a landslide. When his wife suggested the defeat might be a blessing in disguise, Churchill replied, with perfect rueful wit, that at the moment it seemed quite effectively disguised. And yet he did not rage against the voters who had rejected him. Asked about them, he said simply that they had had a very hard time. He had long declared his own motto for life, in victory, magnanimity, and here, at the bitterest possible moment, he actually lived it. There is something profound in that. The man who would not surrender to Hitler also would not sulk at the verdict of a free electorate, because he understood, better than almost anyone, that the whole point of the war he had just won was that ordinary people got to make choices, including the choice to send him home. That grace in defeat may be the truest measure of the man who once prowled these corridors.
