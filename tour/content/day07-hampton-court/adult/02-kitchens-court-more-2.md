# Plumbing, Waste, and the Great House of Easement
The kitchens tell you how a Tudor court was fed. The plumbing tells you how it survived itself, and it is one of the more impressive, and more revolting, engineering stories on the site. Consider the arithmetic. Six hundred people, resident for weeks at a time, all of them eating and drinking heavily, all of them producing the inevitable result. A palace on this scale was as much a sanitation problem as a domestic one, and the Tudors, to their credit, took it seriously.

Start at the clean end. Hampton Court enjoyed running water long before almost anywhere else in England. Fresh spring water was piped to the palace from Coombe Hill, across the river near Kingston, a distance of around three miles, through lead pipes that in places ran beneath the bed of the Thames itself. It was a genuinely ambitious piece of hydraulic engineering, traditionally credited to Wolsey and extended under Henry, and it fed the kitchens, the fountains, and a small number of privileged private bathrooms, the king's own among them.

Now the other end. For the six hundred, Henry built a communal lavatory grandly titled the Great House of Easement. Do not picture anything private. It was a two-storey block of wooden bench seats pierced with holes set a couple of feet apart, seating something like fourteen people at once, side by side, discharging into brick shafts that drained toward the moat and the river. There was no plumbing as we would understand it, and certainly no privacy. This was communal in the fullest sense, and its very grandeur, a whole named building dedicated to the purpose, tells you how large the problem loomed.

Somebody had to empty it, and that fell to a crew of boys and men known as gong scourers, whose job was to climb down into the brick chambers and dig out the accumulated filth by hand. It was dangerous, disgusting, poorly paid, and utterly essential. And it explains one of the great rhythms of Tudor life, the way the entire court would periodically decamp and go on progress, partly for politics and pleasure, but partly for the brute practical reason that a palace housing hundreds became, after a few weeks, close to unbearable. Only once everyone had gone could the servants move in to scour, air, and, in the period's own polite phrase, sweeten the house, scrubbing it back to habitability before the whole machine returned to dirty it all over again.
