# The Toilet That Wasn't
Of all the stories these rooms hold, the finest is also the funniest. Somewhere along the corridor you'll pass a door marked engaged, the universal signal of an occupied lavatory. For most of the war, the staff believed it concealed a rare luxury, Churchill's own private toilet, the one perk of rank in a place with barely enough plumbing to go round. It was nothing of the kind. Behind that door was the most secret room in the entire complex.

It was the Transatlantic Telephone Room, a converted broom cupboard barely three feet by five, and it housed the terminal for a top-secret encrypted telephone link between Churchill and the President of the United States. The scrambling system, code-named SIGSALY, was a technological marvel the size of a room in its own right, far too large to fit here. It lived in the basement of the American Embassy, then in Grosvenor Square. Through it, Churchill and Roosevelt could speak in real time across the Atlantic, confident that German eavesdroppers would hear only a wash of meaningless noise.

The genius of the cover story lies in its psychology. A locked door marked engaged invites no curiosity whatsoever, whereas a sentry would arouse far more suspicion than a lavatory ever could. It was hidden in plain sight by the sheer banality of its disguise. When you find the little room today, pause on the thought that some of the most consequential conversations of the war, between the two men holding the Western alliance together, took place in a space everyone had been politely trained to ignore.
