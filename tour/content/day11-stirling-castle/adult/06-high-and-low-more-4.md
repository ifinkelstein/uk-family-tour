# The Farmer in the Carse
Now come all the way down the ladder, past the king and the lords and the craftsmen, to the wide base that held everything else up, the ordinary farming families who were the overwhelming majority of Scots. Look out from the walls at the flat, rich, green land spreading below the castle. This is the Carse, the fertile floor of the valley of the Forth, and for centuries it was worked by peasant farmers whose lives could hardly have been more different from the golden rooms above them.

Start with the house, because it would genuinely shock you. A farming family lived in a low building of stone, turf, and timber, roofed with thatch, often just a single long room with a fire burning in the middle of the floor and no chimney at all, so the smoke found its own way out through the roof and everything inside smelt of peat smoke. Very often the family shared that one room with their animals, cattle at one end for warmth, people at the other. The floor was bare earth. It was dark, smoky, crowded, and cold, and it was home.

Now the food, which was astonishingly plain and barely changed for hundreds of years. The heart of it was oats and a hardy barley called bere. Oatmeal became porridge, or brose, which was little more than oatmeal and boiling water, or it was baked hard into oatcakes. Alongside it came kale, a tough green cabbage so central to Scottish life that the word kail came to mean the whole meal, and the vegetable patch by the house was the kailyard. There was milk and cheese when the cow gave it, fish if you lived near water, and meat almost never, because your animals were far too valuable to eat while they were still alive and working. And the everyday drink, for grown-ups and children alike, was weak ale brewed from barley, because it was safer than much of the water people could get.

These families did not own their land. They were tenants, and they paid their rent not mostly in coin, which was scarce, but in kind, a fixed share of their grain and animals handed to the landlord, and often in labour too, days of unpaid work owed on the lord's own fields at the busiest times of the year. A whole cluster of families would work the land together in what was called a fermtoun, sharing the ploughing and dividing the strips of soil between them.

And here is the cruel part, the part that ties this humble life straight back to all the grand history you have been hearing today. When armies marched, and around Stirling they marched again and again, it was these people who paid first and paid worst. Soldiers trampled the growing crops, drove off the cattle, burned the thatch, and ate or destroyed the winter store. During the Rough Wooing, the English wars of the fifteen forties, the fields of the Forth were deliberately laid waste. The kings fought for the castle. The farmers lost the harvest. And a lost harvest, for a family living this close to the edge, could mean a whole winter of real, aching hunger.
