# Two Countries That Share a King
Let's talk about the weirdest part. If England and Scotland are two different countries, why are they both on the same island with the same king? How did that ever happen?

Here is the story, and it is a good one. Hundreds of years ago, England and Scotland each had their own king, and the two of them did not get along at all. Then, in a year we say as sixteen oh three, the Queen of England died without any children to take the crown after her. The person next in line to be king turned out to be, of all people, the King of Scotland. So this one man, named James, packed up, rode all the way south to London, and became the king of both countries at the very same time. One king wearing two crowns. Imagine being the captain of two rival football teams at once.

But even then, the two countries still had their own separate governments, arguing away in their own separate buildings, for another hundred years. Then, in seventeen oh seven, they finally joined those governments together into one. That is the moment they truly became one big country called Great Britain.

Now here is the clever bit, and it is the reason Scotland still feels so different today. When Scotland agreed to join up, the Scots said, fine, but we are keeping the things that make us us. And they did. They kept their own laws, so some rules are actually different in Scotland than in England. They kept their own schools. They kept their own churches. And they even kept their own money, which is why in a day or two you might get a coin or a banknote that looks nothing like the ones from London, but works exactly the same. Scotland joined the club, but it absolutely refused to stop being Scotland. Pretty cool, when you think about it, for a whole country to be that stubborn about being itself.
