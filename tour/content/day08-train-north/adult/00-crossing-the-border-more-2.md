# The Iron Road That Shrank the Island
Think for a moment about what this journey used to cost, not in money but in days. Before the railway came, getting from London to Edinburgh meant something close to a small expedition. The fastest mail coaches, changing horses every few miles and rattling on through the nights, took the better part of two days and left you thoroughly battered. For most people the two ends of this island were, in any practical sense, different worlds that rarely met. Then, in the space of a single decade in the eighteen forties, the railway arrived and quietly performed a kind of magic. It did not move the cities, but it moved them closer, folding a two-day ordeal down into a single comfortable day, and it did the same trick everywhere at once. An island that had always been a loose collection of regions, each with its own accent and its own horizon, suddenly became a place you could actually cross.

The trains changed something even stranger, something invisible. Before them, every town kept its own local time, set by its own noon sun, so a clock in the west might read several minutes behind a clock in the east, and nobody much minded, because nobody could travel fast enough for it to matter. The railways could not run on that. A timetable needs one time, the same time, everywhere along the line, so the companies imposed a single standard clock across the country, and ordinary Britain slowly gave up its patchwork of local noons for one national time. The railway did not just carry the country together. It synchronized it.

And so it is worth looking up from your book now and then and simply watching the land change, because the journey tells its own story. The soft, green, hedged lowlands of the south give way, mile by mile, to something barer and harder and older, more stone than hedge, more moor than meadow, the ground quietly rising toward the north. By the time the sea appears on your right and the hills gather on your left, the very look of the country is telling you what the border only confirms. You are somewhere new, and the soft south is well behind you.
