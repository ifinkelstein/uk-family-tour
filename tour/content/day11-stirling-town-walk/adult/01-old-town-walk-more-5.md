# The Old Bridge and the Town Walls
The last stretch of the walk takes you down toward the River Forth and the Old Bridge, and this is where the whole day comes full circle. Up in the castle you heard the story of the Battle of Stirling Bridge in 1297, when Wallace and Andrew Murray let the English army file halfway across a narrow timber bridge, then destroyed the half that had crossed. That wooden bridge is long gone. But its stone successor still stands, close to where the battle was fought, and it is the handsome four-arched bridge you see before you.

It is around five hundred years old, built in the late fourteen hundreds or the early fifteen hundreds, and for centuries it was the great gateway to the north, the lowest bridge over the Forth, the very chokepoint that made Stirling so important in the first place. Every drover, merchant and army heading into the Highlands crossed here, pausing at the toll gates that once stood at each end. And it very nearly had a violent moment of its own. In 1745, when Bonnie Prince Charlie and his Jacobite army came marching, the governor of the castle ordered the southern arch of this bridge to be blown up to stop them crossing. It worked. The Jacobites had to wade the river elsewhere, and the broken arch was patched up again a few years later.

As you walk, look out for the old town wall, the best preserved burgh wall in all of Scotland. It connects straight back to your castle visit. Those walls went up in a tremendous hurry around 1547, and the reason was the very same war you heard about up above, the Rough Wooing, when Henry the Eighth's armies were trying to force the infant Mary, Queen of Scots into a marriage with his son. Stirling threw up stone walls to keep the English out and to keep their little queen safe. The path that follows the wall is called the Back Walk, one of the oldest town walkways in Britain, and it gives you a walker's view of just how well defended this town once was.

A town needed walls for the obvious reason, to keep enemies out. But walls also controlled who came in, and where, and whether they paid their tolls at the gate. So as you cross that old bridge and trace that old wall, remember you are walking the town's front door and its locked gate, the same stones that once helped decide who got into Scotland, and who did not.
