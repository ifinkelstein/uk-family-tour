# Three Lochs and the Swans
This hill has three little lakes hiding around it, and in Scotland a lake is called a loch. Each one is different, and two of them have a surprising secret: they aren't really wild at all. People made them on purpose.

The first one you might pass is called Saint Margaret's Loch, down near that ruined old chapel. It's the swan loch. Look for big white swans gliding about like they own the place, with ducks and hungry geese paddling around them. Swans are much bigger up close than you expect, and they can be a bit bossy, so it's fine to watch them but don't get too near, because a grumpy swan is not something you want to argue with. Here's the surprising bit. This whole loch was made by people less than two hundred years ago. The ground here used to be a soggy, muddy bog, and a man named Prince Albert, who was married to Queen Victoria, had the idea to turn the whole park into something beautiful. So workers dammed up the water and made this pretty loch where the swans now live. Every swan out there is swimming on a lake that somebody imagined and built.

It's named after a real queen, Saint Margaret, who lived about a thousand years ago and was so kind and so holy that people later called her a saint. There's a story that a tiny chapel to her once stood right by the water.

Higher up the hill, if your walk takes you that way, there's a second little loch called Dunsapie, tucked up near the top with a smashing view. That one was made by people too, part of the very same plan. And then, right down at the bottom on the far side, there's the third one, Duddingston Loch, and this one is the wild one, the real natural lake that was here all along. It's so full of birds that it's been turned into a special bird sanctuary, a safe place where the birds are protected and people aren't allowed to disturb them. It has the biggest bed of tall reeds for miles, a whispery green jungle where all sorts of birds hide and nest.

So you've got three lochs, two of them built by people to make the park pretty, and one truly wild one packed with birds. As you walk, see how many you can spot, and count the swans while you're at it. Just remember the golden rule of swans. Admire them all you like, but let them be the bosses.
