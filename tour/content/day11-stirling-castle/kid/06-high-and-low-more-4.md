# Dinner at the Bottom of the Ladder
Now let us climb all the way down to the very bottom of the ladder, past the king, past the lords, past the builders, right down to the widest step of all, where almost everybody actually lived. These were the farmers, ordinary families who grew the food, and there were far more of them than everyone else put together. Look out from the castle at the flat green fields down below. That rich land is called the Carse, and this is where they worked from morning to night.

Now, imagine walking into a farmer's house for dinner. First thing you notice, it is dark, and it is smoky. Really smoky. Because the fire burns right in the middle of the floor and there is no chimney at all. The smoke just drifts up and leaks out through the straw roof, so your eyes sting and everything smells of woodsmoke. The floor is not wood or stone. It is bare, cold earth. And here is the part that always makes people go eeew. The family very often shares that one single room with their animals. Yes, the cows live indoors with you, right at the other end of the room. Why? Because the cows help keep the house warm, like big furry radiators that moo in the middle of the night.

So what is for dinner? Not much choice, I'm afraid. Almost every single day you eat oats. Oats boiled up into gloopy porridge, oats mixed with hot water into a lumpy grey paste called brose, or oats baked into hard, dry oatcakes. With it you get kale, a tough green cabbage, boiled again and again. And that is more or less it. Meat? Almost never, because the animals are far too precious to eat. And what do you drink, even if you are a child? Weak, watery beer, believe it or not, because the plain water often was not clean or safe.

And here is the hardest part of all. These families did not even own their own fields. They had to hand over a big chunk of everything they grew to the local lord, as rent, plus days and days of unpaid work on his land. They lived very close to the edge, with only just enough to get by from one harvest to the next.

Now remember all those exciting battles you heard about, fought around this castle. Who do you think suffered the most? Not the king, safe behind his thick walls. It was these poor farming families. When armies came marching past, the soldiers stomped all over the crops, stole the cows, and set fire to the roofs. And if the food you needed for the whole long winter got trampled or burned in a single afternoon, your family might go very, very hungry. The kings fought over the castle. The farmers lost their dinner.
