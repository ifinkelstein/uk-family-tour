# A Day in the Life of a Blue Whale
Imagine you could shrink down and follow a living blue whale for one whole day. What would it be like? First, you would need good ears, because a blue whale is one of the loudest animals on the entire planet. Its call can reach a hundred and eighty-eight decibels, which is louder than a jet plane taking off. But you would barely hear it, because most of that sound is so low and deep that human ears cannot pick it up. Other whales can. A blue whale's rumble travels through the ocean for hundreds of miles, which means two whales on opposite sides of a whole sea can slowly call back and forth to each other, like the world's biggest, slowest telephone.

Now hold your breath, because we are going down. A feeding blue whale dives deep and can hold its breath for many minutes at a time, its enormous heart slowing right down to save oxygen. Down in the dark it hunts clouds of krill, tiny shrimp smaller than your finger. And here is the amazing part. The whale does not nibble. It speeds up, opens its mouth impossibly wide, and gulps a swimming pool of water and krill all at once. Its throat has deep pleats like an accordion that balloon out to hold it all. Then it squeezes the water back out through bristly plates and swallows what is left. One giant gulp can hold more water than the whale's whole body weighs.

Now, how long does our whale live? A blue whale can live to eighty or ninety years old, about the same as a person, and a few may reach a hundred and ten. Think about that for a moment. Somewhere in the ocean right now there may be an old blue whale that was born before humans agreed to stop hunting them, an animal that has lived through that entire rescue.

And blue whales begin life in a tremendous hurry. A baby blue whale is the fastest-growing animal on Earth. It drinks hundreds of litres of its mother's thick milk every day and puts on weight faster than almost anything alive, growing by roughly the weight of a grown person each and every day in its first months. From tiny shrimp, the biggest animal that has ever lived just keeps getting bigger. Not a bad day's work.
