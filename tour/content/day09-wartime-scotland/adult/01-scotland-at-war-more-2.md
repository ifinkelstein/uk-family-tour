# Operation ZZ and the Great Scuttling
The First World War at sea ended in the Firth of Forth, in the most extraordinary naval pageant ever staged. Ten days after the Armistice, on the twenty-first of November, nineteen eighteen, the German High Seas Fleet, nine battleships, five battlecruisers, seven light cruisers and fifty destroyers, steamed across the North Sea to give itself up. The Royal Navy called the plan Operation ZZ, and it took no chances. Before dawn the Grand Fleet put to sea, around one hundred and ninety Allied warships, British squadrons joined by American battleships and French ships, and formed two enormous parallel columns miles apart. The light cruiser Cardiff led the German line into the gap between them, and the columns then turned inward and escorted their old enemy into the firth, guns unloaded by agreement on the German side, but every Allied crew at action stations in case of a last act of defiance. There was none. That afternoon Admiral Beatty made the signal the fleet had waited four years for: the German flag is to be hauled down at sunset today, and is not to be hoisted again without permission. Ships' companies cheered him from end to end of the anchorage. Some of the older officers found it hard to watch. A fleet built to fight the greatest sea battle in history had ended without one, swinging at anchor off Inchcolm.

The Germans were moved north to Scapa Flow in Orkney, and there they sat for seven months, skeleton crews aboard rusting ships, while the politicians argued at Versailles over who would get them. Their commander, Rear Admiral Ludwig von Reuter, decided nobody would. On the twenty-first of June, nineteen nineteen, with the British guard squadron conveniently away on exercise, he hoisted a prearranged signal and every German crew opened the seacocks. Over the next five hours fifty-two of the seventy-four interned ships went to the bottom, the greatest single day's loss of shipping in history, battleships rolling over with steam roaring from their vents. By strange chance a boatload of Orkney schoolchildren on a sightseeing trip sailed right through it, and watched dreadnoughts capsize around them. Nine German sailors were shot in the confusion, often called the last dead of the First World War. Most of the wrecks were later raised for scrap, but seven still lie on the seabed, and divers travel from around the world to swim over them.

Scapa Flow's second war began with tragedy. On the night of the fourteenth of October, nineteen thirty-nine, two days before the Forth raid you've just heard about, the German submarine U-forty-seven, commanded by Guenther Prien, threaded through the blockships of a supposedly impassable channel and torpedoed the battleship Royal Oak at her anchorage. She rolled over in minutes. Eight hundred and thirty-five men and boys died, and boys is the accurate word, for the crew included over one hundred and sixty rated boy sailors between fourteen and eighteen, most of whom were lost. Churchill ordered the eastern channels sealed for good, and the great concrete causeways built to do it, the Churchill Barriers, still carry Orkney's roads today. The labor came largely from Italian prisoners of war, and they left behind the war's most unexpected monument. Given two Nissen huts and whatever scrap they could find, prisoner Domenico Chiocchetti and his comrades built a complete Italian chapel, with a painted vault, a wrought-iron screen made from salvage, and concrete dressed up as carved stone. Chiocchetti stayed on after his fellow prisoners went home, to finish the sanctuary. The Italian Chapel is lovingly preserved, and it may be the gentlest thing either world war left behind in Scotland.
