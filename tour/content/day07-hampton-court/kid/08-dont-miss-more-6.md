# The Secret Sewing School in the Palace
Here is a secret almost nobody in today's crowd knows. People still work inside this palace, behind some of these very windows, and one group of them has one of the most unusual jobs in Britain. They are the Royal School of Needlework, a whole school for embroidery, which is the art of sewing pictures and patterns with a needle and thread. The school is over one hundred and fifty years old, and since nineteen eighty-seven its home has been right here, in apartments in the newer, Wren-built half of Hampton Court. While you are out here eating an ice cream, someone in there is bent over a wooden frame, stitching away at something that might end up in a museum, or on television, or on a queen. Because this is not hobby sewing. When Catherine Middleton married Prince William in twenty eleven and became a princess, the lace on her famous wedding dress was worked by the embroiderers of this school, and the job was so secret that most of the stitchers did not even know what the pieces they were sewing were for. The story goes that they had to wash their hands every thirty minutes and keep swapping to fresh needles, so that not one fingerprint or tiny smudge would ever touch that snow-white lace. And when King Charles was crowned in twenty twenty-three, the school led the embroidery of a special golden screen that stood around him during the most sacred, private moment of the whole coronation. They stitched it as a great tree with fifty-six leaves, one leaf for each country in the Commonwealth, the family of nations connected to Britain. Think about how perfectly this fits the palace. Five hundred years ago, in Henry's day, needlework was one of the most precious arts in England. The tapestries in the Great Hall, woven with real gold, were basically the giant version of it, and a single set cost as much as a warship. Kings showed off with thread the way they showed off with castles. And here, in the same building, people are still doing it, stitch by stitch by stitch, training new students so the skill never dies out. You cannot usually go inside, it is a working school, not a museum, so this is a secret to collect rather than a door to open. But as you walk past the East Front, look up at the windows and remember, this is not just a palace full of old things. Somewhere up there, a needle is moving right now, sewing the next piece of history.
