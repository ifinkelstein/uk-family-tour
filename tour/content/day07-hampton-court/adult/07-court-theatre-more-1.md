# The Groom of the Stool, or Power at Its Most Intimate
If proximity to the king was the currency of this world, then follow that logic all the way to its startling conclusion, and you arrive at the strangest great office in English history. The single person granted the most intimate access of all to the royal body was the servant who attended the king in the most private act imaginable, and his title was exactly as literal as it sounds. He was the Groom of the Stool, the stool being the close stool, the padded, velvet-covered, gold-studded commode on which the king relieved himself.

Our instinct is to read this as the ultimate humiliation, a courtier reduced to a lavatory attendant. The Tudors saw it precisely the other way around. The Groom of the Stool was, for much of the sixteenth century, one of the most powerful men at court, because he possessed the thing everyone else was clawing toward, unguarded, daily, private time with the monarch. He dressed the king, he slept near him, he heard him think aloud, and he was trusted with the royal secrets and, increasingly, the royal money, since the office came to control significant parts of the privy purse. Ambitious noblemen who would not have dreamed of emptying a chamber pot competed fiercely for a role defined by doing more or less exactly that, because in a system where intimacy was power, the most intimate servant was, quietly, among the most powerful.

The danger came bundled with the closeness. Consider Sir Henry Norris, Henry's Groom of the Stool through the early fifteen thirties, a genial and trusted favourite who knew the king as few others did. When Anne Boleyn was brought down in fifteen thirty-six, Norris was swept up in the charges, accused of adultery with the queen on what almost every historian regards as no real evidence at all, and executed. The man who had stood closest of all to the king was among the first to die when the king's mood turned. It is the entire logic of this palace compressed into a single career. The nearer you stood to the source of power, the warmer you were, and the more certainly you might be burned. At Hampton Court, even the humblest-sounding job could make you a great man, and being a great man near Henry the Eighth was one of the more reliable ways to lose your head.
