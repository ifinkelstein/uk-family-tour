# The Palace, the Cathedral, and the Few
Two buildings you've encountered on this trip will mean rather more to you now. This morning you stood before Buckingham Palace. During the Blitz it was bombed on several occasions, once while the King and Queen were in residence. They pointedly refused to decamp to safety, and after the palace was struck Queen Elizabeth, later the Queen Mother, remarked that she was almost glad of it, because now she could look the bombed-out East End in the face. That single sentence did an enormous amount to bind a frightened city to its monarchy.

On Monday you saw Saint Paul's Cathedral. On the night of the twenty-ninth of December 1940, one of the fiercest incendiary raids of the war set the City ablaze in what became known as the Second Great Fire of London. Wren's cathedral stood ringed by flames, and a volunteer fire watch fought through the night to save it. The photograph taken that night, the dome rising serene and unbroken above a sea of smoke, became perhaps the single most famous image of British defiance, printed everywhere as proof that London still stood.

And the reason any of it survived at all traces back to the skies of the previous summer. In the Battle of Britain, from July to October 1940, the outnumbered pilots of the Royal Air Force fought off the Luftwaffe and made a German invasion impossible, prompting Churchill's immortal tribute that never in the field of human conflict was so much owed by so many to so few. So the palace you admired, the cathedral that awed you, and the very possibility of this peaceful holiday all rest on events decided, in part, from rooms like these.
