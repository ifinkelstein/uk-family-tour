# Churchill the Man
To understand these rooms you have to understand the man they were built to serve, and Winston Churchill defies every easy summary. He was, by turns and often at once, a soldier, a war correspondent, a prisoner, a painter, a bricklayer, a Nobel laureate, a spectacular failure, and the indispensable man of the twentieth century. Born in 1874 into the aristocratic Marlborough family, at Blenheim Palace, he was an indifferent schoolboy with a slight speech impediment, written off by his own father as a disappointment. He spent the rest of his life outrunning that verdict.

As a young man he sought out wars the way others seek out holidays, seeing action in India, Sudan and South Africa while filing vivid, well-paid dispatches for the newspapers. In the Boer War he was captured, escaped from a prison camp in Pretoria, and became a national celebrity before he was twenty-six. He entered Parliament, held one great office after another, and then, in the 1930s, fell into the political wilderness, a lonely and ignored figure warning about German rearmament to an audience that thought him a warmonger and a has-been.

That decade of exile is the hinge of his story, because when the catastrophe he had predicted finally arrived, he was the one senior man untainted by the failed policy of appeasement. On the tenth of May 1940, the day Germany invaded the Low Countries, he became Prime Minister at the age of sixty-five, an age when most men retire. His great gift, it turned out, was not military genius but moral clarity and language, an almost superhuman refusal to contemplate defeat, communicated in speeches that stiffened an entire nation's spine.

He was also difficult, self-centred, frequently wrong, and capable of real callousness, and any honest account keeps those flaws in view. But in the specific crisis of 1940, his stubbornness was precisely what Britain needed. The Churchill Museum next door will let you trace the whole arc. For now, simply remember that the rumpled, cigar-chewing figure who prowled these corridors had already lived three or four lifetimes before he ever got here.
