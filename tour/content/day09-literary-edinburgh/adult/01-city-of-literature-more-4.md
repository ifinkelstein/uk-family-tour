# Sherlock Holmes Was Born in a Classroom Here

The greatest detective in the history of fiction was, in a sense, born in an Edinburgh medical school. Arthur Conan Doyle, the man who created Sherlock Holmes, was born in this city in eighteen fifty-nine, and he trained here as a doctor. And the famous method that makes Holmes who he is, that trick of looking at a total stranger and reading their whole life from tiny clues, Conan Doyle did not invent. He watched a real man do it, right in front of him, in a lecture theatre at the University of Edinburgh.

That man was Doctor Joseph Bell, a brilliant surgeon and teacher, and Conan Doyle was his student and later his clerk. Bell had a party piece that astonished everyone who saw it. A stranger would walk into the room, a patient he had never met, and before the person had said a word about their illness, Bell would tell them their trade, where they had recently travelled, what they had done that morning, all from the smallest of details. The callus on a hand, the mud on a boot, a line of sunburn, a way of standing, a scrap of an accent. To onlookers it seemed like magic. Bell insisted it was nothing of the kind. It was simply observation, close and trained, of things sitting there in plain sight for anyone who bothered to look.

Conan Doyle never forgot it. When he sat down years later to write a detective story, he built his hero around exactly that method. Sherlock Holmes greets Doctor Watson at their very first meeting by deducing, correctly and instantly, that Watson has just come back from military service in Afghanistan, and the reader is hooked for the next hundred and thirty years. Holmes first appeared in eighteen eighty-seven, in a story called A Study in Scarlet, and went on to become perhaps the most famous fictional character on earth, with his deerstalker hat and his pipe. Conan Doyle later wrote to Bell and told him plainly, it is most certainly to you that I owe Sherlock Holmes.

What is easy to miss is how genuinely new this was. Before Holmes, mystery stories tended to solve themselves through luck, or confession, or coincidence. Conan Doyle gave the world a hero who solved crimes by reason, by evidence, by paying attention, treating a crime scene the way Bell treated a patient. In doing so he did not merely write some popular stories, he essentially built the modern detective story, the whole vast genre of crime fiction and courtroom drama and forensic television that we swim in today. Real detectives began studying his methods, and some early forensic scientists cited Holmes as an inspiration.

So the next time you watch a detective on television dazzle everyone by noticing the one tiny thing nobody else saw, remember where the trick was learned. In an Edinburgh hospital, from a real doctor with a genius for looking, watched by a medical student who was quietly taking notes that would change storytelling forever.
