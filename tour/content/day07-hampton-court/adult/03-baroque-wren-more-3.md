# The Fire of 1986 and What It Revealed
On the thirty-first of March 1986, fire broke out in the south wing of Hampton Court, in the very State Apartments that Wren had built for William the Third. It began, poignantly, in a grace-and-favour apartment on an upper floor, where a candle left burning by an elderly resident set light to the room overnight. That resident, Lady Daphne Gale, the widow of a distinguished general, died in the blaze. It remains the palace's worst disaster of modern times, and by the standards of historic-building fires it was severe. The flames tore through the roof of the King's Apartments and sent burning timbers crashing down through the floors below, gutting some of the finest baroque interiors in the country.

What happened next is the part worth carrying with you as you walk these rooms. Staff and firefighters mounted an extraordinary salvage operation even as the building burned, forming human chains to pass paintings, furniture, and carved fragments out through the smoke, and a great deal was saved. The King's Staircase and its Verrio murals came through. Then began one of the most ambitious restoration projects Britain had undertaken, six years of painstaking work, completed in 1992, using traditional materials and craft techniques wherever possible, hand carving, lime plaster, seasoned oak, so that the repaired rooms would age like the originals rather than sit within them as obvious modern patches.

There is a strange gift buried in the catastrophe. When conservators stripped the fire-damaged rooms back to their bones, they could read the building's history in a way an undamaged palace never allows, layers of earlier decoration, hidden Tudor brickwork, the tool marks of Wren's carpenters, all briefly exposed to view. The disaster became, for the historians, an accidental excavation. So when you admire the immaculate baroque of the King's Apartments today, hold two thoughts at once. Almost everything you see is genuinely of William's time, saved by luck and courage. And some of it is a superb, knowing reconstruction, proof that this palace has spent five centuries being damaged, patched, and loved back into being, and that the version standing in front of you is simply the latest act of that very long devotion.
