# The Loch Where a Sport Was Born
Remember that wild loch at the bottom of the hill, Duddingston Loch, the one packed with birds? Well, in winter, something magical used to happen to it, and it may just be the birthplace of a whole sport.

Long ago, Scotland had proper freezing winters, so cold that this loch would freeze right over into a sheet of thick, solid ice. And when that happened, the whole city seemed to rush down to play on it. People strapped blades to their boots and went skating, whizzing and gliding across the frozen water. But they also invented something cleverer, a game called curling, and lots of people think curling was born right here on this very loch.

Curling is a brilliant, slightly bonkers game. You slide a big heavy polished stone across the ice, trying to make it stop in exactly the right spot, while your teammates run alongside it frantically sweeping the ice with brooms to help it slide further. Yes, really, sweeping the ice with brooms, in the middle of a game, like the tidiest sport ever invented. It looks a bit silly and it is enormously good fun, and Scotland is so proud of it that people now play curling at the Winter Olympics. And it may all have started on this little loch below Arthur's Seat. There was even a special wee building on the shore, a Curling House, just for the players and their heavy stones.

There's a famous painting of this frozen loch too, and it's one of the best-loved pictures in all of Scotland. It shows a man in a smart black coat and a tall black hat, gliding across the ice on one leg, arms folded, looking extremely pleased with himself. He was a real person, a minister, which is a kind of church leader, and everyone calls the painting the Skating Minister. There he is, calm as anything, skating on this exact loch more than two hundred years ago. You can go and see the real painting in a gallery in the city, and once you've seen it you never forget it.

So picture it. A freezing winter's day, the loch turned to glass, the whole city out on the ice, skaters gliding, stones sliding, brooms sweeping, and one very dignified minister balanced on a single skate. All of it, right here, at the foot of your volcano. And coming up next, there's a sneaky old train that used to creep right underneath this park, and you'd never even know it was there.
