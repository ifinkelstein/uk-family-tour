# The Beer That Sailed to India
Two hundred and fifty years ago, if you wanted to get from Britain to India, there was only one way. A wooden sailing ship, creaking and rolling for four, five, even six months, all the way down around the bottom of Africa and across the Indian Ocean. No fridges. No freezers. Food and drink just had to survive, and lots of it didn't. Water in barrels went green and slimy. Milk was hopeless. And this is where beer enters the story of the sea.

People love to tell it like this. British beer kept spoiling on the long voyage, until one clever London brewer invented a special new beer, extra strong and stuffed with extra hops, because hops work like a preservative, the way vinegar keeps pickles safe. His beer arrived in India tasting wonderful, everyone cheered, and it got the name India Pale Ale. Great story. But beer detectives, real historians who dig through dusty old papers, checked, and the truth is sneakier. That strong, hoppy beer already existed in Britain, and it just happened to be brilliant at sea voyages. The famous brewer mostly won because his brewery sat right next to the docks where the India ships loaded up. Nobody invented it for India. India just gave it its name. So when you hear the legend, you can smile and say, the story goes.

Here's another ocean-sized surprise. In the olden days, ordinary weak beer, called small beer, was something almost everybody drank, even children, even at breakfast. Ew, right? But think about it. Their water often came from dirty rivers and wells that could make you really sick, while small beer had been boiled while it was being made, so it was often the safer choice. Kids in this city, hundreds of years ago, drinking small beer with their morning porridge. Aren't you glad someone invented taps?

Beer kept travelling. In Bavaria, in Germany, brewers stored their beer in freezing cold caves in the mountainsides, and down in that cold dark a different kind of yeast slowly took over, one that likes the cold and sinks to the bottom. It made a cleaner, crisper, lighter beer, and its name comes from the German word for a storeroom. Lager. Then a town called Pilsen made the first golden, sparkling, sunshine-coloured lager, and the world went bananas for it. Today, if you picture a glass of beer, yellow and fizzy, you're picturing lager. Nearly every beer on the planet is now its grandchild.

Britain, though, stubborn as ever, kept its old living cask ale, with the barrels in the cellar and the levers on the bar. And guess what happened. Brewers in America got bored of boring beer, discovered the old British styles, and made India Pale Ale famous all over again, so the beer that once sailed away to India ended up sailing home. Scotland is full of new brewers making it too.

So this week, when you're in a pub having your chips and your ginger beer, look over at the bar and count the tall handles. Every fizzy yellow lager in the world came from a cave in Germany. But the beer coming up through those levers was alive in the cellar right under your shoes a minute ago. Of all the beers on Earth, that one travelled the shortest way, and that's exactly what makes it special.
