# The Secret Bunker
You've come down here at the end of a long, hot day, and you'll have noticed the temperature drop the moment you left the Clive Steps and descended below King Charles Street. That coolness is your first clue to what this place really is, a bunker, a subterranean nerve centre, tucked into the basement of what was then the New Public Offices and is today the Treasury, right on the edge of Saint James's Park. For six years it was one of the most closely guarded secrets in Britain.

The idea was born of hard-headed realism. Through the 1930s, as Hitler rearmed Germany, British planners grew certain that any future war would open with a knockout blow from the air, bombers over London on the very first day. The prevailing dread was captured in a phrase of the time, the bomber will always get through. So in 1938, around the Munich crisis, work quietly began to convert this reinforced basement into an emergency seat of government, somewhere the war could be run even as the capital burned.

The Cabinet War Rooms became operational on the twenty-seventh of August, 1939, a week before Britain declared war. And from that moment one room never closed. The Map Room was staffed around the clock by officers of the Navy, Army and Air Force, every hour of every day, for the entire war. The lights, quite literally, were never switched off, until the sixteenth of August, 1945, when they were extinguished for the first time in six years.

Then the place was simply abandoned. The staff walked out and left it, maps on the walls, pins in place, as though everyone had merely stepped out for lunch. It slumbered, more or less untouched, for nearly four decades, preserved by a curious mixture of secrecy and neglect, until the Imperial War Museum opened it to the public in 1984. What makes this site extraordinary among London's attractions is its authenticity. Very little here is reconstruction. You are looking at the actual room where the war was tracked, frozen at the instant peace arrived.
