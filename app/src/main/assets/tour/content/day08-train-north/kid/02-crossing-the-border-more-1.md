# Holy Island and the Vikings
As your train runs along the seaside in the far north of England, watch the window for the North Sea, and keep an eye out for a low island just off the coast with a little castle on it. That's Holy Island, also called Lindisfarne, and it has one of the strangest features of any place in Britain. Twice a day, it isn't an island at all.

Here's how it works. A narrow road runs across the sand from the mainland out to the island. But when the tide comes in, the sea rises and floods right over that road, and the whole thing vanishes under the water. For hours, nobody can drive on or off. Then the tide goes out again, the road reappears dripping with seaweed, and the island joins back onto the land. People who forget to check the tide times sometimes have to scramble up onto a little wooden rescue tower and wait there, with their car sitting underwater below them, feeling very silly indeed.

More than a thousand years ago, this tiny island was one of the most important and most peaceful places in the whole country. Monks lived there in a monastery, quietly making the most beautiful books you have ever seen, hand-painted with gold and swirling patterns and tiny creatures hidden inside the letters, one page at a time. One of those books, called the Lindisfarne Gospels, has actually survived to this day, more than thirteen hundred years old, and it is now so precious that it lives inside a special case in a great library down in London. And then, in the year 793, something terrifying happened. Longships appeared on the water, filled with warriors from across the sea. The Vikings had arrived. They stormed the island, smashed the church, stole the treasure and the golden books, and sailed away. People all across the country were horrified. Nobody had ever dared attack a holy place like that before. Many historians say that awful day was the true beginning of the age of the Vikings in Britain.

So as you glide past Holy Island in your warm, comfy train, picture those longships out on the cold grey water, and the frightened monks, and be very glad you're arriving in a rather friendlier century than they did.
