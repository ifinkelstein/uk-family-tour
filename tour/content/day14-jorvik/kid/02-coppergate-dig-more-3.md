# The Dig Everyone Came to Watch

Imagine a giant muddy hole in the middle of a busy city, with archaeologists down inside it on their knees, scraping at the ground with little trowels. Now imagine a queue of people, hundreds of them, stretching all the way down the street, waiting patiently in the rain, just to stand and watch those archaeologists dig. That really happened, right here, and it is one of the best parts of this whole story.

The man in charge of the dig was called Richard Hall. Back in the nineteen seventies, when archaeologists found something exciting, they usually hid it away behind big fences so nobody could see, and only clever professors were allowed near. Richard Hall thought that was silly. He said something brave. He said the Vikings of York do not belong only to professors, they belong to everybody, especially to the ordinary people of York. So instead of hiding his dig, he built special platforms where anyone could come and look right down into the hole and watch the Viking city being uncovered, bucket by bucket, day by day.

And people came. Oh, they came. Over the five years of digging, more than one million visitors queued up to watch. One million. The newspapers gave it a nickname, the Viking Dig, with capital letters, like the name of a film, and it became famous all over the country. Children came on school trips and pressed their faces to the railings. Grown-ups came on their lunch breaks. Everybody wanted to see a real Viking shoe or a real Viking comb come out of the mud for the very first time in a thousand years.

Here is why that matters. When Richard Hall saw how much people loved watching, he had an idea. What if, when the digging finished, they did not just fill the hole back in? What if they built something on top so that visitors could keep coming, forever, and travel back to the Viking street whenever they liked? That idea became the JORVIK Centre. The ride you are about to go on exists because a million people once queued in the rain to watch some archaeologists dig a hole. So really, you are here today because those people could not look away. And honestly, neither could anyone. Who would?
