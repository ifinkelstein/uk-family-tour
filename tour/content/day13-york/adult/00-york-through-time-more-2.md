# Reading the City in Layers
When the Roman legions finally left Britain in the early fifth century, York did not vanish; it changed hands. The Angles, a Germanic people, moved into the old Roman shell and renamed it Eoforwic, and in doing so they gave it a second golden age, this one Christian and scholarly. In the year six hundred and twenty seven, the Northumbrian king Edwin was baptized here on Easter Day, in a hastily built wooden church that stood on the very spot the great stone Minster occupies now. Every stone of that cathedral, in a sense, grows out of that single wet morning.

The Anglo-Saxon city became famous for its learning. Its cathedral school held what was probably the finest library in England, and it produced a scholar named Alcuin, who in the year seven hundred and eighty two was recruited by no less a figure than Charlemagne to run the schools of his empire. A boy educated in York ended up shaping the education of half of Europe. Then, in the eight hundred and sixties, a great Danish army swept in and took the city, and York entered its Viking chapter as the capital of a northern kingdom they called Jorvik. But that is tomorrow's story, and it deserves its own day, so we will leave the Vikings knocking at the gate for now.

Here is the useful part for a walker. All of these ages are still legible if you know where to look. The walls you can climb largely follow the line the Romans first laid down. Any street whose name ends in gate, and there are many, from Stonegate to Coppergate to Petergate, carries a Viking word, gata, meaning street, so the very act of asking directions puts a little Old Norse in your mouth. The word bar, confusingly, means a gateway in the wall, medieval this time. And beneath the Minster itself, in the Undercroft, you can walk down through the actual strata, Roman wall, then Norman, then Gothic, one age resting on the bones of the last. York does not tuck its history away in a museum case. It leaves it lying around in the street, and hands you the trick of reading it.
