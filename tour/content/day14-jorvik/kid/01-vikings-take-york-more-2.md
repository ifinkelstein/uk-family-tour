# A Day on a Viking Street
Close your eyes for a second and put yourself on the real Coppergate, one thousand years ago. You wake up in a house made of woven wooden sticks packed with mud and moss, with a roof of straw or wooden shingles, and a fire smoking in the middle of the floor. There is no chimney, so the smoke just drifts up and out through the roof, and everything, including you, smells of woodsmoke all the time. It is dark inside, and a bit crowded, because your whole family sleeps on benches along the walls, maybe with a dog and some chickens for company.

Step outside and the street is buzzing. Coppergate is narrow and muddy, lined with fences and workshops, and the neighbours are noisy. Next door someone is hammering hot metal. Across the way a woman is carving combs out of deer antler, and further along a man is spinning wooden cups on a foot-powered machine, which is exactly how the street got its name. You can hear geese honking, pigs snuffling, people haggling, and a language called Old Norse, the great-great-grandparent of modern Icelandic, being shouted and laughed all around you.

Down by the river Ouse, boats are unloading. Some carry fish and firewood from nearby, but others have come a very long way, and this is the amazing part. Traders in Jorvik got their hands on silk from thousands of miles east, golden amber from the cold Baltic Sea, and once even a little cowrie shell that lives only in warm seas near Arabia. A Viking child in muddy York might hold a shell that had travelled further than anyone they would ever meet.

What did you do all day? If you were a Viking kid, you worked. You fetched water, gathered firewood, fed the animals, helped spin wool, and learned a trade from your parents. But there was fun too. Children played with carved wooden toys and little boats, they had ice skates made from horse leg bones for the frozen river in winter, and grownups and children alike loved a board game a bit like chess, where one side defends a king and the other side tries to trap him. And everyone told stories by the fire, long tales of gods and monsters and heroes, because there were no books for most people, only voices and memory.

It was smoky, smelly, freezing in winter and packed with people and animals. But it was also busy, clever, and connected to half the world. That is the street the JORVIK ride is about to carry you through.
