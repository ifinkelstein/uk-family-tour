# How Kings Stopped Being the Boss

Long, long ago, a king really was the boss of everything. His word was the law. He grabbed the money, he started the wars, and if you complained, well, too bad for you. So how did we get from that all powerful king to the king across these railings, who is famous and grand but doesn't really rule at all? It took about eight hundred years and three enormous, dramatic moments. Here they are.

The first happened in a grassy meadow by a river, way back in the year twelve fifteen. There was a king named John, and honestly, almost nobody liked him. He kept losing wars and demanding more and more money. Finally his most powerful lords had had enough. They put on their armour, cornered him, and made him press his seal onto a document we now call Magna Carta, which means the Great Charter. And it said something jaw dropping for the time. Even the king has to obey the law. He couldn't just steal your stuff or throw you in a dungeon whenever he felt like it. That one idea never went away.

The second moment was much scarier, so brace yourself. About four hundred years later, a king named Charles the First had a gigantic argument with Parliament about who was really in charge. The argument grew and grew until it turned into an actual war, king against Parliament. And Parliament won. Then, in sixteen forty nine, they did something almost nobody had ever dared do to a king. They put him on trial, found him guilty, and chopped off his head. For about eleven years, Britain had no king at all. Later they did bring a king back, but every king after that knew the terrifying lesson. Do not push Parliament too far.

The third change was the slowest and the quietest. Bit by bit, over hundreds of years, more and more ordinary people were allowed to vote, until nearly every grown up in the country got a say. And as the people got more power, the king got less and less, until the crown became what you see today. Loved by lots of people, dazzling to look at, and in charge of almost nothing. Pretty wild for a story that started with an angry king in a field, don't you think?
