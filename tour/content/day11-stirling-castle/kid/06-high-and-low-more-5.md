# The Woman Who Held the Baby Queen
For our very last story, let me introduce you to one more real person, someone you have definitely never heard of before, even though she once held the most important baby in all of Scotland in her own two arms. Her name was Jean Sinclair, and she was the nurse of Mary, Queen of Scots.

Back in those days, a royal mum did not look after her own baby the way mums do today. Instead, a specially chosen woman did nearly everything, the feeding, the rocking, the cuddling, and staying awake all through the night when the baby cried. That woman was the nurse, and if she did her job well, she became one of the most trusted and best-loved people in the whole palace. Jean Sinclair was so good at it that she had already looked after a royal baby once before Mary was even born. So when the little baby who would be queen came along, everyone agreed. Give her to Jean.

And my favourite moment in her whole story is this. When Mary was just a few months old, a nosy ambassador all the way from England came to check up on her, because there was a rumour going round that the baby queen was weak and sickly. If that rumour turned out to be true, it could change everything. So who sorted it all out? Jean Sinclair. She calmly unwrapped the baby from her tight little blanket and held her up, wriggling, so the man could see with his own two eyes that Mary was a strong, healthy, perfect little girl. And off he trotted to tell everyone the good news. One steady nurse, holding up one baby, and a whole worried country breathed out again.

Soon after that, Jean brought baby Mary here to Stirling, to grow up safe behind these thick castle walls. And later, when Scotland became too dangerous and it was decided to whisk Mary far away to France, guess who went with her, sailing across the whole sea to a strange new country where nobody even spoke the same language? Jean Sinclair. She would not leave her little queen behind, not for anything in the world.

We even still have some of the letters Jean wrote, and they show she was a real person with real worries, especially about money. But she stayed loyal right to the very end, and Mary always remembered her fondly as her darling nurse.

I love finishing with Jean, because think about what she shows us. You did not have to be a king or a lord or a mighty warrior to matter. An ordinary woman, with kind and patient hands, helped keep the future queen of Scotland alive. The kings all get the statues. But people like Jean were the ones who really held the future, sometimes literally, fast asleep in their arms.
