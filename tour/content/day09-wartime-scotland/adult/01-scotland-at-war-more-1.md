# The First Raid: Spitfires Over the Forth
The Second World War's air battle over Britain began here, six weeks into the war and ten months before the Battle of Britain. On the sixteenth of October, nineteen thirty-nine, twelve Junkers Ju eighty-eight bombers of the Luftwaffe's Kampfgeschwader thirty crossed the North Sea to attack Royal Navy warships near the Forth Bridge and the Rosyth dockyard. Their leader was Hauptmann Helmut Pohle, and by his own later account his orders had a strange scruple to them: ships in dock were off limits, because bombs falling on a dockyard might kill civilians, and in October nineteen thirty-nine both sides were still pretending this would be a tidy war. The battlecruiser Hood, the biggest prize in the firth, was safely inside the dock, so the bombers went for the cruisers Southampton and Edinburgh and the destroyer Mohawk, lying in the open water your boat crosses on day ten.

No air raid siren sounded. The warning system simply failed, and so Edinburgh got its first air battle as a public spectacle. People stood in Princes Street and on the Fife shore watching aircraft wheel and dive over the bridge, many assuming it was an exercise, while golfers played on and spent machine-gun rounds pattered onto rooftops. Up from their airfields came the Spitfires of two auxiliary squadrons, six-oh-two, City of Glasgow, and six-oh-three, City of Edinburgh, weekend flyers, lawyers and farmers and businessmen, defending their own home towns. They shot two of the bombers into the sea. Fighter Command credited the first to Flight Lieutenant Pat Gifford of six-oh-three, a solicitor in civilian life, and these are counted as the first German aircraft destroyed over Britain in the Second World War. Pohle himself was pulled from the water off Crail, the only survivor of his crew, and is often described as the war's first German prisoner taken in Britain. The raid was no comedy for the navy, though. Sixteen sailors died and forty-four were wounded, most aboard Mohawk, whose captain, Commander Richard Jolly, was mortally wounded yet refused to leave his bridge, brought his ship safely in, and died hours later. Twelve days after that, a Heinkel came down on a hillside at Humbie in East Lothian, the first German aircraft brought down on British soil, and the auxiliaries had made their point. The Luftwaffe never got a free run at the Forth.

Edinburgh had actually been bombed from the air once before, and that story is stranger still. On the night of the second of April, nineteen sixteen, the Zeppelin L-fourteen droned over the blacked-out city, drawn by the lights of Leith after failing to find Rosyth and the bridge. For over an hour it wandered above Edinburgh dropping some forty-four bombs on a city with essentially no defence. A whisky warehouse in Leith went up in flames, a bomb killed six people in Marshall Street, and another exploded in the Grassmarket outside the White Hart Inn, directly below the castle. Thirteen or fourteen people died that night, depending which count you follow. Look down at the pavement by the White Hart when you're in the Grassmarket. There's a flagstone marking where the bomb fell, one small scar from the night the First World War reached Edinburgh's Old Town.
