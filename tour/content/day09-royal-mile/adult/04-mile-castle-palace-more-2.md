# Tell Me More: Holyrood, Palace, Abbey and a Parliament

At the very foot of the Mile, where the crown gives way to the cross, stands the Palace of Holyroodhouse, the official Scottish residence of the British monarch, and beside it the roofless shell of the abbey that gave the place its name. Start with the ruin. Holyrood Abbey was founded by David the First in the eleven twenties, and legend says he built it in thanks after a miraculous escape, when a stag he was hunting turned on him and a holy cross appeared between its antlers, or in his hands, depending who's telling it. Believe as much of that as you like. The abbey was real, magnificent, and is now a beautiful skeleton open to the sky, stripped by reformation, war and a collapsed roof.

The palace grew up beside it as a royal home, and its most famous room is a small one. Up a tight turnpike stair in the oldest tower are the chambers of Mary, Queen of Scots, and it was here, on the ninth of March fifteen sixty-six, that the ugliest scene in Scottish royal history played out. Mary, six months pregnant with the future James the Sixth, was at supper when her jealous husband Lord Darnley and a gang of armed nobles forced their way in, dragged out her Italian secretary David Rizzio, and stabbed him more than fifty times, some say in front of her, some say in the next room. They left his body at the top of the stairs. To this day the guides will show you a patch of floor said to be stained with his blood, and I'll be honest with you, that stain is refreshed now and then with a little red to keep the tourists happy. The murder was real enough. The stain is theatre.

Nearly two centuries later the palace had its last great fling as a royal court, when Bonnie Prince Charlie swept into Edinburgh in the seventeen forty-five rising and held court here for about six weeks, giving receptions and at least one ball in the long gallery to celebrate his victory at Prestonpans, while his father was proclaimed king at the Mercat Cross up the hill. It was a brief, glittering, doomed romance, and by the next spring it ended in slaughter at Culloden. The monarch still comes back, though. Each summer the King or Queen spends a week in residence here, holding garden parties and investitures, and the flag over the palace tells you whether they're at home.

Then turn and look across the road, at the strangest building on the whole Mile. That is the Scottish Parliament, opened in two thousand and four, the first home of a Scottish parliament to sit since the old one voted itself out of existence at the Union with England in seventeen hundred and seven. Its Catalan architect, Enric Miralles, filled it with odd organic shapes, upturned boats, leaf forms, abstract motifs bristling off the walls, and he died of a brain tumour before it was finished. It came in three years late and roughly eleven times over budget, at more than four hundred million pounds, and Scotland argued about it furiously, and then it won the country's top architecture prize and everyone went rather quiet. Crown at the top of the Mile, cross and palace at the foot, and now, right beside them, the modern seat of the nation. The old street still knows how to surprise you at the bottom.
