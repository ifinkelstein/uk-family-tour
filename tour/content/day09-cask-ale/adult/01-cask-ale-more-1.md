# How Beer Is Made
Beer is made from four things, and you already know all of them. Barley, water, hops, and yeast. Three of those you met in the whisky story. The fourth, the hop, is beer's own signature, and we'll get to it.

Think of beer as bread's liquid cousin. Same grain, same yeast, same warm smell while it's being made. A baker and a brewer are running the same experiment in different directions, and for most of history they worked side by side, often in the same household, often the same woman doing both.

It starts with malt, which is barley that has been soaked, allowed to begin sprouting, and then dried, so the grain unlocks its own sugars. The brewer grinds this malt and steeps it in hot water in a big vessel called a mash tun, essentially making an enormous pot of sweet barley tea. What runs off is called the wort, and it is warm, cloudy, and sweet enough to drink like a dessert. Brewers do taste it, and visitors on brewery tours are often handed a cup. It tastes like the milk at the bottom of a bowl of breakfast cereal.

Then comes the boil, and the hops go in. A hop is the flower of a climbing vine, a soft green cone that looks like a tiny pinecone made of paper. On its own it is shockingly bitter, and that is the point. The bitterness balances all that malt sweetness, the way lemon balances sugar, and the resins in the hop also help keep the beer from spoiling. Hops came late to Britain, only catching on in the fourteen and fifteen hundreds, and for a while the unhopped old drink was called ale and the newfangled hopped stuff was called beer.

After the boil, the wort is cooled and the yeast is pitched in, and the real magic starts. Yeast is a living single-celled fungus that eats sugar and gives out two things, alcohol and carbon dioxide. In a bakery you keep the gas and drive off the alcohol, and you get risen bread. In a brewery you keep both, and you get beer. For a few days the vats seethe with a thick cap of foam, and a working brewhouse smells warm and yeasty and faintly of toast.

And here is the family secret worth exactly one sentence. If you take that fermented liquid before any hops have touched it and run it twice through a copper still, you are on the road to whisky, which is why brewers like to say, with a straight face, that whisky is simply beer that went on to further education.

What comes out of the fermenting vessel is, in most of the world, then filtered bright, pasteurised so nothing lives in it, carbonated with injected gas, and sealed up. Stable, consistent, and finished. But in Britain there has always been another path out of the brewery, the one where the beer leaves home still alive. That path leads down into a pub cellar, and it's where we're going next.
