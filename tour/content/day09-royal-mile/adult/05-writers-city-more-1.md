# Tell Me More: Scott, and the Invention of Scotland

Of the three writers in that little museum, one did something stranger than merely write great books. Walter Scott more or less redesigned how the world pictures Scotland, and not always for the better. Start with the books. In eighteen fourteen he published Waverley, anonymously, a novel set during the Jacobite rising of the seventeen forty-five. It sold out in two days, and it did something genuinely new, taking real history and peopling it with invented characters caught up in it. Scott is widely credited with inventing the historical novel, the whole enormous genre, and for a decade he was the most famous author alive, turning out Ivanhoe, Rob Roy and the rest at a furious pace.

One of those books connects straight to a spot you saw earlier today. In eighteen eighteen Scott published The Heart of Midlothian, a novel named for the Old Tolbooth prison whose outline is marked by that granite heart in the cobbles by St Giles, the one the locals spit on. The prison is long demolished, but the name survives largely because Scott made it the title of a bestseller. The heart in the street and the heart on the spine of the book are the same heart.

Then, in eighteen twenty-two, Scott pulled off his greatest piece of theatre. King George the Fourth announced a visit to Edinburgh, the first by a reigning monarch in nearly two centuries, and Scott stage-managed the entire thing as a tartan spectacular. He dressed the city, and the portly king, in Highland plaid, and staged a pageant of clans and kilts at a time when Highland dress had until recently been banned outright and the Highlands themselves half-emptied by clearance. It was gloriously inauthentic, a Lowland lawyer inventing a costume drama, and yet it stuck. The shortbread-tin image of Scotland, all tartan and romance and misty glens, was to a remarkable degree the creation of this one busy novelist. He even helped rediscover the ancient Scottish crown jewels, forgotten in a locked chest in the castle, and had them put back on display, where you can see them still.

You cannot miss his monument. On Princes Street stands the Scott Monument, a soaring, sooty Gothic rocket more than sixty metres high, with the great man seated in marble beneath it, his dog at his feet. It is the largest monument to a writer anywhere in the world. Two hundred years on, opinions divide. Some love Scott's romances, some find them heavy going, and many wish he'd left Scotland's self-image a little less swathed in tartan. But no writer ever shaped a nation's picture of itself more completely, and no city ever built its storytellers a taller stone thank-you.
