# Deer, Fountains, and the Secret of the Magic Garden
When you tumble out of the maze, do not rush straight for the exit, because the gardens around you are enormous, and they are full of things worth finding.

First, the deer. Beyond the neat flower gardens stretches a huge wild park called Home Park, and living in it right now, today, is a herd of about three hundred fallow deer. Here is the amazing part. Those deer are the great-great-great, and keep going for a very long time, grandchildren of the deer that Henry the Eighth kept here to hunt five hundred years ago. Henry walled in this whole park specially so he could gallop after deer whenever he liked. The palace changed, the kings died, but the deer just kept being deer, generation after generation, and they are still here. Look for them out across the grass, especially the males with their branching antlers.

Look too for the Long Water, a long straight canal like an arrow pointing away from the palace toward the horizon, and for the great fountain gardens with their cone-shaped yew trees standing in rows like dark green soldiers. Everything here was designed to look powerful and perfectly tidy, to show that the king could boss even nature around.

And now the best secret of all, saved for the Magic Garden. You already know it has towers and battlements to climb on. But those towers are not just any castle shapes. They are copies of real towers that once stood on this exact spot. Right here, in Henry the Eighth's time, there was a tiltyard, a special arena where knights in armour thundered at each other on horseback with long lances. Around that arena stood five tall towers, where the king and his guests sat to watch and to feast, high up with the best view in the palace. Over the centuries, four of those towers vanished completely. Then, when workers were digging to build your playground back in the year twenty sixteen, archaeologists found the buried floor of one of the lost towers, hidden underground all that time. So the Magic Garden is built right on top of Henry's jousting ground, and its climbing towers are echoes of the real ones, where a king once cheered and knights once crashed. You are playing on a five hundred year old field of games. Climb it like you mean it.
