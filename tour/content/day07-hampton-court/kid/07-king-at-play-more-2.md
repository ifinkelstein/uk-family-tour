# The Music King, and the Song He Did Not Write
Here is a side of Henry the Eighth you might not expect. The big, fierce king was also a real musician, and a good one. He played several instruments, the lute, which is a bit like a round-backed guitar, the organ, the recorder, and more, and he was very proud of it. He kept a whole crowd of musicians at court, and he collected instruments the way some people collect toys, owning dozens and dozens of them.

Better still, Henry actually wrote his own music. Real songs, written down, that we can still play and sing today. His most famous one is called Pastime with Good Company, and it is basically a cheerful tune about how much he loves hanging out with his friends, hunting, singing, and having a good time. It is almost five hundred years old, and it still sounds fun. If you ever hear it played, that is a king's own music reaching your ears across five whole centuries.

Now, you may have heard a beautiful, sad, old English song called Greensleeves, and there is a very popular story that Henry wrote it himself for Anne Boleyn, while he was trying to win her heart. It is a lovely story. It is also almost certainly not true, and here is how we can tell. Greensleeves is built on a special Italian style of music that had not even arrived in England yet while Henry was alive. And the song only turns up written down for the very first time in the year fifteen eighty, more than thirty years after Henry had died. So unless he wrote a hit song from beyond the grave, in a style that did not exist yet, it was not him. Sorry.

This is a handy thing to remember whenever somebody tells you a great story about the past. Sometimes the best-loved tales are exactly the ones that never happened. A good detective, and a good historian, always ask the very same question you can ask, does this story actually fit the facts? When it comes to Henry and Greensleeves, the answer is a polite but firm no. He gave us plenty of real music instead. That is more than enough.
