# What a King Actually Does All Day

If the King does not govern, you might reasonably ask what on earth he does with his time, and the honest answer is that his job is enormous, relentless, and almost entirely ceremonial and symbolic rather than political. Think of him less as a chief executive and more as the living embodiment of the state itself, a person whose whole role is to stand for the country rather than to steer it.

Some of the work is constitutional and quietly essential. Every law passed by Parliament requires the monarch's formal approval, called Royal Assent, before it takes effect. In theory the King could refuse. In practice no monarch has refused a bill in more than three hundred years, since the reign of Queen Anne, because to do so would shatter the whole delicate understanding that the crown does not overrule the elected will of the people. So the assent is real, and it is also, by iron custom, automatic. Once a year the King performs the State Opening of Parliament, arriving in a gilded coach, wearing a crown that weighs more than a bag of sugar, to read aloud a speech setting out the coming year's laws. Every word of that speech is written by the government, not by him.

Then there is the softer, endless work that fills the calendar. He receives the prime minister in a private weekly audience, where by tradition he may, in the old formulation, be consulted, encourage, and warn, but never command. He hosts visiting presidents and monarchs, hands out honours to nurses and scientists and pop stars, opens hospitals, plants trees, and represents Britain abroad in a way no politician quite can, precisely because he is above the political fray.

And a great deal of it is pure theatre, and gloriously so. The sentries in their red tunics and towering bearskin caps, the Changing of the Guard, the balcony appearances before oceans of people, the horses and the gold and the flags. None of it decides a single law. All of it says, in a language older than any statute, this is the country, and it endures.
