# The Fall of Wolsey
The base of this story leaves Wolsey handing over Hampton Court and dying on the road to a treason trial. It is worth slowing down on that fall, because it is one of the great tragic arcs of Tudor politics, and this palace is its most eloquent monument.

Consider first how far he had climbed. Thomas Wolsey was the son of an Ipswich butcher, a man of genuinely humble origin who rose, on brains and appetite for work alone, to become Cardinal, Archbishop of York, Lord Chancellor, and papal legate, effectively the chief executive of Henry's England for the better part of two decades. He gathered offices, revenues, and palaces on a scale no English subject had ever managed, and Hampton Court was the supreme statement of that ascent. The problem, as the fall would reveal, is that everything he held he held at the king's pleasure, and pleasure is a thin foundation.

His undoing was a single intractable task, the annulment of Henry's marriage to Catherine of Aragon, the so-called King's Great Matter. Only Rome could grant it, and Rome, with Catherine's nephew the Emperor Charles the Fifth effectively controlling the Pope, would not. Wolsey, for all his cunning, could not conjure a divorce the papacy was determined to withhold, and Anne Boleyn's rising faction was only too happy to whisper that his failure was really foot-dragging. In fifteen twenty-nine he was stripped of the chancellorship and his vast properties, Hampton Court and York Place among them. Reduced to his archbishopric in the north, he lingered a while, then in fifteen thirty was arrested for treason at Cawood and ordered south to face the king.

He never arrived. Broken in health, he got as far as Leicester Abbey, and there he died, on the twenty-ninth of November fifteen thirty, spared the axe only by his own body. His gentleman-usher George Cavendish recorded the line that has clung to him ever since, that had he but served God as diligently as he had served the king, God would not have given him over in his grey hairs. It is the perfect Hampton Court epigraph. The man who poured his genius into serving an earthly master built the most magnificent house in England, and lived just long enough to watch that master take it, his liberty, and very nearly his life. Every brick of the Tudor courts you are standing in was laid by a servant whose brilliance could not save him.
