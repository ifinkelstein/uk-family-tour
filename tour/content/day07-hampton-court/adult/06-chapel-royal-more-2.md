# The Georgian Sunset, and Why No Monarch Has Slept Here Since
Hampton Court holds one quiet distinction that surprises people. No reigning monarch has actually lived here for nearly three hundred years, which is precisely why so much of it survives. The story of how a working royal palace became a museum is worth knowing, because it explains the whole atmosphere of the place.

After William the Third, the palace enjoyed a final flourish under the early Georgians. George the First and George the Second both kept court here, and it was for them that the last great suites of rooms were fitted out and the Chocolate Kitchen kept busy. George the Second brought his family and his quarrels with him, this was a household riven by the famous Hanoverian loathing between reigning kings and their heirs, and the palace saw its share of royal sulking behind gilded doors. And then, in seventeen thirty-seven, George the Second simply stopped coming. His grandson George the Third, when his own turn came, never cared for the place and never lived here, and no monarch since has made it a home.

What do you do with an enormous, empty, expensive palace that the royal family no longer wants? The Georgian and Victorian answer was ingenious. The apartments were carved up and handed out, rent free, as grace-and-favour lodgings, granted by the Crown to people it wished to reward, the widows of generals and admirals, retired courtiers, distressed gentlefolk with the right connections and the wrong bank balance. For two centuries the old state rooms and Tudor lodgings filled with a genteel, gossipy, slightly threadbare community of pensioners, living among the ghosts and the tapestries. It is no accident, as you will hear elsewhere, that the palace's reputation for hauntings dates from exactly this era.

The final turn came in eighteen thirty-eight, when the young Queen Victoria, newly on the throne, took the decision that made Hampton Court what it is today. Rather than keep it as a private royal preserve, she ordered it opened, free of charge, to the public. Ordinary people poured in by railway and river steamer, and the palace began its long second life as one of the nation's great days out. So the reason you can wander these rooms at all is that a succession of monarchs found the place unfashionable, and one young queen decided her subjects might enjoy what she herself did not want. Hampton Court survived, in the end, by being abandoned at exactly the right moment, into the hands of everyone.
