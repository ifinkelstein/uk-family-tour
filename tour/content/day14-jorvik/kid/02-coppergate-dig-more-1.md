# The Helmet at the Bottom of the Pit
Of all the forty thousand things dug up around here, one is so special it lives in a glass case of its own in a museum across the city. It is a helmet, and the story of finding it is pure luck and pure drama.

It was the twelfth of May, nineteen eighty-two, in the afternoon. The archaeologists had finished part of the dig, and a big mechanical digger was scooping out soil to make room for the new building. Suddenly the metal bucket clanged against something hard, deep down in what had once been a well or a pit. The digger driver stopped. Down in the mud was a lump of rusty metal, and when the archaeologists rushed over and cleaned it, their jaws dropped. It was a helmet, and not a Viking one. It was even older, made by the Anglo-Saxon people who lived in York before the Vikings arrived, more than twelve hundred years ago.

This helmet is a masterpiece. It is made of iron, with strips of shiny brass, and a curtain of tiny iron rings, called mail, to protect the neck. It has guards for the cheeks, a bar to guard the nose, and two beautiful brass eyebrows that meet in a little animal head. Best of all, running over the top is a strip of brass with words scratched into it, in Latin, a prayer that names a man called Oshere. Nobody knows exactly who Oshere was, but this was almost certainly his helmet, worn or owned by a real Anglo-Saxon warrior with a real name. That is incredibly rare. In more than a thousand years of digging all over England, only a tiny handful of helmets this old have ever been found. You could count them on one hand.

Why was such a treasure at the bottom of a pit? That is a mystery. Maybe it was hidden on purpose to keep it safe, and the owner never came back. Maybe it was thrown away, or lost, or dropped in as a gift to the gods. The mud is not telling. But because the ground here is so wet and airless, the same magic that saved the Viking shoes and combs also saved this helmet for us, so that you, today, can look a twelve-hundred-year-old warrior's own helmet right in the eyebrows.

When you have time in York, the helmet is worth hunting down in the Yorkshire Museum. It is one of the greatest things ever pulled out of the ground in this whole country, and a mechanical digger very nearly scooped it up by accident.
