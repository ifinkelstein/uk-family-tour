# The Graveyard of Names

Greyfriars Kirkyard has been collecting stories since the fifteen hundreds. Covenanters were imprisoned here, grave robbers prowled here, and a small dog allegedly kept vigil here for fourteen years. But the graves that draw the longest lines today belong to people nobody had heard of twenty-five years ago, because fans decided they were the secret ingredients of Harry Potter.

The star attraction is against the far wall, along the path toward the gate into Heriot's grounds. A worn stone commemorates Thomas Riddell Esquire, who died in eighteen oh six at the age of seventy-two, and below him his son, also Thomas Riddell, a young captain of the fourteenth regiment who died at Trinidad in the West Indies in eighteen oh two, aged twenty-six. Riddell, two Ls, but say it aloud and you've said the name of Tom Riddle, the boy who becomes Lord Voldemort. The stone is now one of the most visited graves in Scotland. You'll spot the worn grass leading to it, and fans occasionally leave notes, some of them apologizing to the real Riddells for the association.

Nearby, the supporting cast. William McGonagall, widely celebrated as the worst poet in the history of the English language, a Dundee weaver whose recitals were so gloriously awful that audiences pelted him with vegetables. He died poor in Edinburgh in nineteen oh two and lies here. Professor McGonagall's surname is genuinely his; Rowling has written that she borrowed the name of the terrible poet deliberately, enjoying the thought of the formidably brilliant professor sharing it. And there's a stone for one Elizabeth Moodie, which fans have adopted on behalf of Mad-Eye Moody.

So did Rowling harvest names here on her writing breaks? The Elephant House is barely two minutes away, and the theory writes itself, which is precisely why you should distrust it. In twenty twenty-two she addressed it directly, and her answer was deflating. She'd never taken the tour. One of her own children went on a Potter walking tour of Edinburgh for a laugh, and came home, in Rowling's telling, with a ton of information that was news to her. What she has conceded, in interviews going back to the nineties, is that she was a magpie for unusual names, collecting them from war memorials, phone books, maps and, yes, gravestones. So the honest version is this. The Riddell connection is possible, unconfirmed, and unknowable, perhaps even to Rowling. The McGonagall name is real but came from the poet's fame, not his headstone. The rest is legend, and the tour guides' certainty is theater.

Before you leave, look southwest over the wall at the fairy-tale pile of turrets and chimneys rising beyond. That's George Heriot's School, built through the sixteen hundreds with the fortune of George Heriot, goldsmith to King James the Sixth, as a school for orphaned boys. It still gives free places to children who've lost parents. Its pupils are sorted into four houses with color identities, and it looks more like Hogwarts than anything the films built. The claim that it inspired Hogwarts is everywhere in Edinburgh, and Rowling has never endorsed it. Her standing line about such claims is that she's never even seen most of the places said to have inspired her. Still, as you stand in a graveyard beside a turreted school for children, with Voldemort's name on a stone behind you, you can see why this city stopped asking her permission.
