# A Tale of Two Towns

To understand why Edinburgh looks the way it does, you have to picture it before the split. For centuries the whole city was squeezed onto the single ridge that runs down from this castle, hemmed in by walls, water, and the steep sides of the rock. With nowhere to spread out, Edinburgh grew upward instead, stacking families ten and twelve stories high in smoky stone tenements. It was overcrowded, it was filthy, and the coal haze hanging over it earned the city an affectionate insult, Auld Reekie, which is broad Scots for Old Smoky.

By the middle of the seventeen hundreds the respectable classes had had enough. The town leaders decided to build, from scratch, an entirely new district on the empty farmland across the northern valley, and in seventeen sixty-six they held a competition for the design. It was won by an almost unknown young architect named James Craig, and his plan was approved the following year, in seventeen sixty-seven. What Craig drew was the opposite of the medieval muddle behind you, a calm, rational grid of wide straight streets and dignified squares, with the main avenues named to flatter the royal family, George Street, Queen Street, Princes Street.

The houses went up in what we call the Georgian style, meaning the neat, balanced, classical look that was fashionable during the reigns of the several kings named George in the seventeen and early eighteen hundreds. Rows of matching stone fronts, orderly windows, restraint everywhere. The wealthy moved across the valley almost as one, abandoning the Old Town to the poor, which is precisely why the medieval quarter survived unmodernized for you to walk through today.

So the city froze into two halves that have faced each other ever since, the cramped, romantic, shadowy Old Town on the ridge, and the graceful, spacious, planned New Town on the plain. Together they are so remarkable that the whole ensemble was made a UNESCO World Heritage Site in nineteen ninety-five. Over your days here you will cross between them again and again, and each time it will feel like stepping between two centuries.
