# The Great Vine and the Oldest Tennis Court
Two of the grounds' quieter superlatives reward a detour. The first is the Great Vine, and the numbers are genuinely astonishing. It was planted in 1768, during the tenure of Lancelot "Capability" Brown as the king's gardener here, grown from a cutting taken at Valentines Mansion in Essex. From that single cutting has grown what Guinness World Records recognizes as the largest grapevine on Earth: a stem now around four meters in girth at the base, with its longest rod trained to more than thirty-six meters under glass. It still crops heavily every year, averaging something like two hundred and seventy kilograms of Black Hamburg dessert grapes, which the palace sells to visitors each September. Queen Victoria had its grapes sent to the royal table. A single plant, more than two and a half centuries old, still fruiting, tended by a full-time vine keeper who looks after essentially nothing else. It is one of the most extraordinary living objects in Britain, and it sits in a modest glasshouse most people walk straight past.

The second is the Royal Tennis Court, home to real tennis, the intricate medieval ancestor of the lawn game, played indoors off sloping penthouse roofs and into hazard openings, with heavy handmade balls and asymmetrical racquets. Henry the Eighth was an addict and, in his athletic youth, a formidable player; Wolsey's original court stood here, and fragments of his walls survive. The court you can look into today was built for Charles the First in 1625, and remarkably it is still in constant use, the oldest real tennis court in Britain where the game is actively played. If a match is on, pause and watch a few points. The scoring is baffling, the angles are geometry made physical, and you are watching a sport played more or less unchanged since the Tudor kings sweated on this very spot.
