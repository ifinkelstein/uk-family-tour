# Turner's Temeraire and Van Gogh's Absent Sunflowers
The Fighting Temeraire, 1839, voted the nation's favourite painting in a 2005 BBC poll, is Turner editorialising shamelessly and magnificently. The Temeraire, second ship of the line at Trafalgar, was sold for scrap in 1838 and towed to Rotherhithe. Turner reengineers the facts: the tow happened over two days, mostly unrigged, with masts already sold off; he restores her masts, paints her spectral white and gold, invents the elegiac sunset, and puts the sun setting in the east if you check the geography. He knew exactly what he was doing: "my darling," he called it, refusing all offers and leaving it to the nation.

Van Gogh's Sunflowers would normally make a useful pairing, but today the gallery lists it as on loan to Philadelphia. Keep it as context rather than destination. Painted in Arles in 1888 as Van Gogh prepared the Yellow House for Gauguin's arrival, the Sunflowers turned new chrome yellows into emblems of friendship, heat, optimism, and fragility. Some of those pigments are now slowly darkening, so conservators monitor the painting's chemistry with unusual care. If you cannot see it today, use Van Gogh's Chair instead: a quieter object, but one that carries the same emotional charge. Turner and Van Gogh both turn simple subjects into pressure vessels for feeling, and both make modern colour feel almost alive.
