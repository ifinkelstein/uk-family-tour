# Edinburgh, Brewing City
Everyone knows Edinburgh and whisky. Almost nobody remembers that for two centuries this was one of the great brewing cities of the world, and that the whole town used to smell of it, a warm, sweet, toasty fog of malt drifting over the rooftops that older residents still recall with real affection.

It began with monks. When King David the First founded Holyrood Abbey in the eleven hundreds, the canons did what monastic orders did everywhere in Europe and set up a brewhouse, helped by something they may not have fully understood. Edinburgh sits on a seam of underground water, wells fed through old sandstone, and that water comes up hard, loaded with minerals, which happens to be superb for brewing pale, clean-tasting ales. Brewers later mapped where the good wells ran, a band curving from Holyrood out through Fountainbridge, and called it the charmed circle. Breweries crowded onto that line like houses along a good street.

By the mid eighteen hundreds Edinburgh had around forty breweries, and only London and Burton upon Trent rivalled it in Britain. Two names towered over the rest. William Younger's, founded in 1749 and eventually brewing in the shadow of Holyrood Abbey itself, and William McEwan's, founded in 1856 at Fountainbridge. Both became export empires, shipping strong Scotch ale to Canada, the Caribbean, India and Australia. The two firms merged in 1931, and through later mergers became the core of Scottish and Newcastle, for a time one of the biggest brewers on the planet.

Scottish beer even had its own private language. From the mid eighteen hundreds, beers here were named after the wholesale price of a cask, in shillings. A modest everyday beer might cost sixty shillings a hogshead, a stronger one seventy, a stronger one still eighty, and the names stuck to the beers themselves. In an Edinburgh pub you can still order an eighty shilling, pronounced eighty bob, and get a smooth, malty dark amber ale, essentially a beer named after a Victorian invoice. At the top of the scale sat the strong, treacly Scotch ale that export markets loved, affectionately called a wee heavy. And Scottish ales as a family lean maltier and less bitter than English ones, partly plain geography, since hops never grew happily this far north and every sack of them had to be carted up from England at real expense, so brewers used them sparingly and let the malt do the talking.

The twentieth century took it all apart. Takeovers, closures, demolitions, until by the two thousands a city of forty breweries was down to one grand old survivor, the Caledonian on Slateford Road, founded in 1869. The Caledonian was a working Victorian time capsule, brewing in open-topped coppers heated by direct flame, the last brewery in Britain doing it that way. It should have had a happy ending, but it didn't. Heineken, who had inherited it, announced its closure in 2022, saying the Victorian kit was no longer economical, and the site is being turned into housing. A hundred and fifty three years of continuous brewing, ended by a spreadsheet.

And yet the story isn't over. A new generation of small breweries has sprung up in and around the city, and the beer they make is often exactly what the Victorians would recognise, pale ales and eighty shillings pulled through hand pumps. Edinburgh's beer, like its whisky, refuses to stay buried.
