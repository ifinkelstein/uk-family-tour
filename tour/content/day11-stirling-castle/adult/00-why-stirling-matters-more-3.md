# The Family That Ties the Whole Trip Together
If there is one thread that stitches your entire British trip into a single story, it is a royal family called the Stewarts, later spelled Stuart, and Stirling is their cradle.

The line begins with Robert the Bruce, the hero of Bannockburn. His daughter married a nobleman named Walter, the High Steward of Scotland, and their descendants took the job as their name, Stewart. From the thirteen seventies onward, Stewart kings ruled Scotland, and this castle was their favourite home. They were crowned nearby, married here, raised their heirs behind these defensible walls, and were sometimes buried within sight of them.

The most famous of them was a girl. Mary, Queen of Scots became queen when she was just six days old, and was crowned in the chapel of this castle at nine months, a baby held up before the nobles of Scotland. Her life became one of the great tragedies of European history, and it ended far away, on an English executioner's block, ordered by her cousin Elizabeth the First of England. But before that, Mary had a son, and that son changed everything.

Here is the surprise I promised you. Elizabeth the First of England, the great Tudor queen, never married and had no children. When she died in sixteen oh three, the English crown passed to her nearest royal relative, and that relative was Mary's son, James, already King James the Sixth of Scotland. Overnight he became King James the First of England as well. A Scottish king, born of a family raised at Stirling, rode south to rule from London, and for the first time one monarch wore both crowns. The old enemies now shared a king.

This is why the same faces keep reappearing across your trip. The Tower of London, Hampton Court, Westminster Abbey, Edinburgh Castle, and this rock at Stirling are not separate stories. They are chapters about one extended family, feuding, marrying, killing, and inheriting its way across two kingdoms over six hundred years. If you have been using the Royal Family Tree in this app, this is the branch it all hangs from.

So as you walk into Stirling, try to see double. It is a soldier's fortress, scarred by siege and cannon. And it is a family home, where children who would wear crowns were rocked to sleep. Keep both in view, and the castle, and half of Britain's history, will suddenly click into place.
