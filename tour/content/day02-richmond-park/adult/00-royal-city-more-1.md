# How a Wet Little Island Grew a World
Let us zoom out for a moment, because the scale of what happened on this island is genuinely odd. When the Romans arrived, Britain was the far, cold edge of their world, the last stop before the grey ocean. Around the year fifty they laid out a town on the Thames and called it Londinium, threw a wooden bridge across the river roughly where London Bridge still stands, and turned it into a busy port. It did not go smoothly. About ten years later a warrior queen named Boudica, furious at Roman cruelty to her people, burned the whole place to the ground. The Romans rebuilt it, bigger, and Londinium became the largest city in Roman Britain, right up until the empire finally loosened its grip and the legions sailed away around the year four hundred and ten.

Then came centuries of Saxons and Vikings and Normans, invasions and marriages and mixings, and out of all that churn came a language, English, cobbled together from German and Norse and French with a heavy seasoning of Latin. Which is a large part of why it is such a magnificent, illogical mess to spell.

Here is the astonishing bit. This one rainy island, no bigger than a medium American state, went on to shape the modern world out of all proportion to its size. The language you are speaking is now the closest thing the planet has to a common tongue. The idea that even a king must answer to a law he did not personally invent, and to an assembly of representatives called a parliament, was fought over and hammered out here across long, bloody centuries. And from the sixteen hundreds onward Britain built the largest empire in history, ruling, at its height, something like a quarter of all the people on earth. That story has a dark side we will not skip past, the conquest, the taking, the profit wrung from other people's lives. But it explains why, standing in a quiet deer park outside London, you are standing very near one of the hinges of the modern age. Not bad for a soggy island the Romans thought of as the end of the world.
