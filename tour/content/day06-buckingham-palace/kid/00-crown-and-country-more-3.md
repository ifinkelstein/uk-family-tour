# Your Spotting Game for Today

Now you know the big secret, that the king reigns but Parliament rules, the whole day turns into a kind of treasure hunt. Here are the things to spot, and what each one is really telling you.

Start right here at the palace, because this is all about the crown, the sparkly, showing off side of the story. Look up at that balcony in the middle. That is where the royal family comes out to be seen by gigantic crowds on the biggest days, a coronation, a royal wedding, a huge celebration. The whole point is simply to be seen. Look at the guards in their bright red coats and their tall furry hats. They are real soldiers, but their job here is to look magnificent and keep a tradition alive that is hundreds of years old. This whole place is saying the same thing over and over. We have always been here. We are still here.

Then, as we head toward Westminster, watch everything change. You will see a gigantic clock tower most people call Big Ben, standing over the Houses of Parliament. That building, not this palace, is where the real arguing and deciding happens. And nearby, on a street called Whitehall, keep your eyes peeled for a plain black door with the number ten on it. That is where the prime minister lives and works, the person who actually runs the country. No golden coach. No balcony. No fuzzy hats. Just a very important black door. That difference, between the dazzling palace and the plain busy doors, is the whole secret standing right in front of you.

And then we go underground, into the Churchill War Rooms, where the story suddenly gets deadly serious. Because a long time ago, in nineteen forty, this whole clever arrangement, the crown, the Parliament, the freedom to argue about anything at all, very nearly got destroyed by a terrible war. A small group of brave people hid in a cramped secret bunker and helped save it. So as we walk today, keep score. The gold and the guards this morning, the doors of power this afternoon, and the bunker at the end. They are all pieces of one big question that Britain spent eight hundred years trying to answer. Who gets to be in charge, and how do you stop them from being too much in charge?
