# The Science of Whisky
Ready for the really cool part? Making whisky is full of proper science, the kind you could almost do at school. Well, not the drinking part. But the how-does-that-even-work part is brilliant.

It starts with yeast, and yeast is alive. It is a tiny, tiny creature, far too small to see, the very same stuff that makes bread puff up. And here is what yeast does. It eats sugar, and when it eats, it burps. Really. It gives off two things. One is alcohol, the strong part of the whisky. The other is a gas called carbon dioxide, the exact same gas that makes fizzy drinks fizzy. So a giant tub of whisky-in-the-making is really a giant tub of billions of tiny creatures, all munching sugar and burping bubbles. That is how every fizzy, boozy drink in the world begins. With burps.

Next comes a neat trick to make it stronger. Alcohol and water are mixed together in the tub, but they are not quite the same. Alcohol turns into steam at a cooler temperature than water does. So if you heat the liquid up gently, the alcohol floats off as steam first, before the water. The whisky makers catch that special steam, cool it down, and it turns back into a liquid that is far stronger than before. Heat it, catch the steam, cool it back. Scientists call it distilling, and you could almost show a grown-up how it works with a boiling kettle and a cold spoon.

Now, where do all the smells come from, the apples and the smoke and the vanilla? From teeny invisible bits called molecules, floating up out of the glass and into your nose. The smoky smell comes from special molecules made by the peat fire. The fruity smell comes from different molecules made inside those big copper kettles. And the vanilla smell comes from the wooden barrel. When they burn the inside of the barrel black and crispy, the burnt wood makes a molecule called vanillin, which is the exact same smell as real vanilla. So the barrel is quietly flavouring the whisky, very, very slowly, for years.

Here is my favourite science fact of the lot. When a grown-up adds a single drop of water to their whisky, it smells stronger, not weaker. How can that be? In strong whisky the smelly molecules get trapped down in the liquid and cannot escape. Add a tiny bit of water, and suddenly they float up to the top and jump into the air, so far more of them zoom up your nose. Real scientists tested this with real computers and proved it. One drop of water, and whoosh, out come the smells. You do not taste a thing, but your nose gets the whole show.
