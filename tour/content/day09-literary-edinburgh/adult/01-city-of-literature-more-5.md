# Modern Edinburgh, From Miss Brodie to a Boy Wizard

Edinburgh did not stop making world famous books when the Victorians packed up. If anything the last seventy years have been another golden run. Take Muriel Spark, one of the sharpest writers of the twentieth century, whose most famous novel, The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie, appeared in nineteen sixty-one. It is the story of a charismatic, dangerous Edinburgh schoolteacher who picks a favoured set of pupils and tries to mould their lives, and it is set in exactly this kind of Edinburgh, all propriety on the surface and something more complicated underneath. If that theme is starting to sound familiar, you have been paying attention.

Then there is the crime. Edinburgh has produced a whole school of dark, brilliant crime writing that Scots half-jokingly call Tartan Noir. Its king is Ian Rankin, whose detective, Inspector John Rebus, has prowled the real pubs and back streets of this city through more than twenty novels. Rankin uses the crime story the way Stevenson used the ghost story, to peel back the respectable face of Edinburgh and show you what lies underneath. Read him and you will never quite see the city's tidy Georgian streets the same way again. Fiercer still is Irvine Welsh, whose novel Trainspotting, published in nineteen ninety-three, told the raw story of a group of young heroin addicts in the Leith district of the city, in the real, blunt, unpolished speech of its streets. It became a defining book, and then a famous film, of its generation. It is emphatically not a children's story, but it is proof that this city's writers still write with total honesty about the life around them.

And then, of course, there is the wizard. In the nineteen nineties a young single mother named Joanne Rowling, short of money and writing partly to keep warm, drafted much of the early Harry Potter in Edinburgh cafes, filling notebooks over a single slowly cooling coffee while her baby slept beside her. That much is true and well documented. Around it, though, the city has grown a happy tangle of fan legend, and it is worth knowing which is which. Fans love to point to one particular cafe as the birthplace of the books, though Rowling actually wrote in several places, and did a good deal of her work in a cafe owned by her brother-in-law. Fans also love to wander Greyfriars Kirkyard, the old graveyard nearby, and find a weathered headstone bearing the name Thomas Riddell, delighting that Lord Voldemort's real name is Tom Riddle. And they gaze up at George Heriot's School, a beautiful old turreted school beside the graveyard, and see in it the towers and houses of Hogwarts. These links are lovely, and the walk is a delight, but they are fan theories, not confirmed by Rowling herself, so enjoy them with a pinch of salt.

What all these writers share, across three hundred years, is that they keep finding their stories in this same small, steep, double-faced city. Miss Brodie's classroom, Rebus's pubs, and Harry's cafes are all a few minutes' walk from where Burns was feted and Stevenson was born. Some cities inspire one great book. Edinburgh keeps doing it, century after century.
