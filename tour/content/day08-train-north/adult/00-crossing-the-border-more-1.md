# Same Island, Still a Different Country
It is easy for a visitor to assume that England and Scotland long ago blended into a single British sameness. They did not, and the reasons are written into the very deal that joined them. Go back to sixteen oh three. Queen Elizabeth the First of England died childless, and the nearest heir happened to be her cousin's descendant, James, who was already King of Scots. So he rode south to London and wore both crowns at once, King James the Sixth of Scotland and First of England. This is what historians call the Union of the Crowns, and it is exactly that and no more, one man wearing two hats. The two countries still had their own parliaments, their own laws, their own everything, merely a shared monarch who mostly preferred the richer, warmer south.

The fuller union came in seventeen oh seven, and it was a harder, colder, more commercial affair, driven partly by a Scottish financial catastrophe and partly by English nerves about who would inherit the throne next. Under the Acts of Union the two parliaments dissolved themselves and reconvened as a single body, the Parliament of Great Britain, sitting at Westminster in London. That is the moment Great Britain became one state rather than two countries that happened to share a king.

But here is the clever, telling detail. The Scots who signed up in seventeen oh seven insisted on keeping the things that made them a nation, and they got it in writing. The treaty specifically guaranteed that Scotland would keep its own legal system, its own separate system of education, and its own national church, the Presbyterian Church of Scotland, quite distinct from the Church of England. Three centuries later, those guarantees still hold. A Scottish lawyer trains in a different body of law from an English one. A Scottish child moves through a different school system toward different exams. And the pound in your pocket may soon look strange, because Scottish banks print their own banknotes, real money, perfectly valid, and utterly different in design from the notes you changed at the airport. English shopkeepers sometimes squint at them, and that little squint is three hundred years of history in miniature. So no, you have not simply crossed into the northern end of England. You have crossed into a country that agreed to share a state while carefully keeping its soul.
