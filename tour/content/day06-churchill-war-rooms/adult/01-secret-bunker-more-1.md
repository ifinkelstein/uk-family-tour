# The Slab
There is an uncomfortable truth beneath the legend of the impregnable bunker. At the start, it was nothing of the sort. When the Cabinet War Rooms opened in 1939, they were an ordinary basement with a reinforced ceiling, and the people who worked here understood perfectly well that a direct hit from a heavy bomb could bring the whole building down on top of them. They came to work anyway. That, more than any concrete, is the quiet heroism of the place.

The remedy came in October 1940, at the height of the Blitz. Engineers installed a vast horizontal layer of reinforced concrete directly above the rooms, up to five feet thick in places, and the staff simply called it the Slab. It transformed a vulnerable cellar into something that could genuinely take a beating. Under its protection, the complex was expanded until it was roughly three times its original size, a warren of offices, dormitories, a kitchen and a canteen threading beneath Whitehall.

And yet, here is the detail worth savouring. Even with the Slab overhead, senior officials privately suspected the bunker would not survive a direct strike from the very largest German bombs. They kept that anxiety to themselves. There was even a more heavily protected fallback site prepared elsewhere in London, should Whitehall become untenable. As you move through these low, pipe-crossed corridors, notice how little was spent on comfort and how much on sheer survival. Nobody designed this place to be pleasant. They designed it, on a tight and anxious budget, to keep the machinery of government breathing while the city above it was pounded night after night.
