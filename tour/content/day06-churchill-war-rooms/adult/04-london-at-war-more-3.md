# London Can Take It: Myth and Record
The London the people down here were fighting to save has come to us wrapped in a phrase, the Blitz spirit, a picture of plucky, unbreakable Cockneys grinning through the rubble, brewing tea in the wreckage, carrying on. It is one of the most powerful national self-images ever created, and to appreciate this bunker properly you should understand both how deliberately it was manufactured and how much genuine truth it nonetheless contains.

Manufactured is the right word, because a great deal of the image was consciously made, and made rather brilliantly, by the British government's own propagandists. In October 1940, at the height of the bombing, the GPO Film Unit produced a short documentary called London Can Take It, directed by Humphrey Jennings and Harry Watt and narrated, pointedly, by an American reporter named Quentin Reynolds. That choice was strategic. The film was aimed squarely at the still-neutral United States, and a neutral American voice describing London's courage was far more persuasive to Americans than any British minister could be. Reynolds calls himself a neutral reporter and then delivers a spare, poetic tribute to a city taking its nightly punishment without flinching. It was superb film-making and superb persuasion, distributed across America by Warner Brothers and nominated for an Academy Award, and it did real work in nudging American opinion toward the British cause. The Blitz spirit, in other words, was partly a weapon, aimed across the Atlantic.

Historians have since taken a cooler look at the record beneath the legend, and it is worth being honest about what they found. The historian Angus Calder, in a book bluntly titled The Myth of the Blitz, argued that much of what looked like cheerful defiance was really something grimmer and more passive, an exhausted determination to carry on simply because there was no alternative. The unity was real but partial. Something like a quarter of London's population left the city. There was looting amid the ruins. Class resentment sharpened when the poor of the East End were bombed out while the wealthy had deeper shelters and country houses to flee to. And the crowds were not uniformly adoring, Churchill and even the royal family were, on occasion, booed while touring shattered streets. The glow of national togetherness was, at times, a good deal patchier than the newsreels suggested.

But here is the thing to hold onto, and it is what saves this from mere debunking. The remarkable fact is not that morale wobbled, but that, under a bombardment that killed tens of thousands and destroyed vast tracts of the city, it never actually broke. The whole German strategy rested on the assumption that terror from the air would shatter British will and force a surrender. It did not. People were frightened, and resentful, and worn down, and some fled, and yet the docks kept working and the factories kept running and the government kept governing, from rooms exactly like these. That is a subtler and more human kind of courage than the poster version, and arguably a more admirable one. Real bravery is rarely the absence of fear or complaint. It is the decision, made by frightened and grumbling people, to get up the next morning and carry on anyway. The propaganda simplified it. But the thing it simplified was genuinely there.
