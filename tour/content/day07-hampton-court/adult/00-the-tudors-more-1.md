# Two Roses Made Into One
To understand where the Tudors came from, you have to start with a civil war. For roughly thirty years before they took power, England had been tearing itself apart in a struggle historians later named the Wars of the Roses, a long dynastic feud between two branches of the royal family, the house of Lancaster and the house of York. Each, so the tradition goes, took a rose as its badge, red for Lancaster and white for York. Cousins killed cousins, kings were crowned and then murdered, and the country bled.

It ended on a single morning. On the twenty-second of August, fourteen eighty-five, at Bosworth Field in the English Midlands, a fairly obscure Lancastrian claimant named Henry Tudor met the reigning king, Richard the Third, in battle. Richard was killed, the last English king ever to die in combat, and the crown, so the story goes, was pulled from a thornbush and set on Henry's head right there on the field. That man became Henry the Seventh, the first Tudor king, and the founder of the whole dynasty.

His masterstroke came next. To end the feud for good, he married Elizabeth of York, joining the two warring houses in a single marriage and a single bloodline. His clever piece of branding was the Tudor rose, a red rose and a white rose folded into one flower. Once you have seen it, you will spot it everywhere in this palace, carved in stone, painted on ceilings, worked into glass. It is not just decoration. It is a constant, deliberate reminder that the Tudors had ended the bloodshed and united the country.

From Henry the Seventh the line ran straight through the century. His son was Henry the Eighth. Henry's three children each took the throne in turn, the boy king Edward the Sixth, then Mary the First, and finally Elizabeth the First, with whom the family, and its extraordinary century, came to an end.
