# Giant Kettles and Sleeping Barrels
Want to know how whisky is made? It starts with something you might have had for breakfast. Grain. Barley, to be exact, the same golden grain that grows in fields all over Scotland. And the first step is a sneaky trick. The barley seeds are soaked in water until they think it is springtime and start to sprout, because a sprouting seed fills up with sugar, ready to grow into a plant. But just as it starts, the whisky makers stop it, by drying the grain with heat. Gotcha.

On one windy island called Islay, they dry that grain over a fire made of peat. Peat is squishy dark stuff, a bit like soil, that you can dig out of the ground and burn, and its smoke soaks right into the grain. That is the big secret of why some whiskies smell like a bonfire. It happens way back here, at the very beginning.

Next the dried grain gets crushed and mixed with hot water until it turns into a warm, sweet liquid. Then they add yeast, the same tiny living stuff that makes bread rise, and something wild happens. The yeast gobbles up all the sugar and burps out bubbles, and the whole giant tub froths and hisses and foams like a monster having a bath. Now the liquid has alcohol in it, a bit like a rough beer.

Here comes my favourite part. That liquid is poured into enormous copper kettles shaped like giant shiny onions, some of them taller than two grown-ups standing on each other's shoulders. They heat it up until it turns into steam, catch the steam, and cool it back into a much stronger liquid. In Scotland they usually do this twice, to make it cleaner and stronger.

And then, the sleepy part. The clear new whisky is poured into wooden barrels and left in a dark, quiet warehouse to sleep. Not for a night. Not for a week. For years and years and years, sometimes longer than you have been alive. While it sleeps, the wood turns it golden and gives it lovely flavours. And here is the truly magical bit. A little of the whisky slowly floats away into the air through the wood, a tiny bit every single year, and nobody can stop it. What do the Scots call the whisky that vanishes? The angels' share. They say the angels drink it, up in the roof of the warehouse, as a thank-you for guarding the barrels. Isn't that a wonderful idea? Somewhere over Scotland, right now, invisible angels having a sip.
