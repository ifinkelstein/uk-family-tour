# Henry's Crown, and the Regalia England Destroyed
In the Royal Pew, the gallery above the Chapel Royal, and usually on display from Wednesday to Saturday, stands something England is not supposed to have: the crown of Henry the Eighth. It is a re-creation, and an unusually serious one. The original, the Tudor Crown, was made in the early sixteenth century for Henry the Seventh or his son, roughly two and a half kilograms of gold set with sapphires, rubies, emeralds and well over three hundred stones and pearls. Henry the Eighth wore it on the great feast days, most theatrically at Epiphany, the sixth of January, when he processed in full regalia to this chapel to offer gold, frankincense and myrrh, playing the fourth king to the Bible's three. Look closely at the little figures standing in the crown's fleurs-de-lis, because they carry the Reformation in miniature. The crown originally held enamelled figurines of the Virgin, Saint George, and three images of Christ. After the break with Rome, Henry had the three Christs replaced with three sainted kings of England, Edmund, Edward the Confessor, and the recently venerated Henry the Sixth. A crown that had framed its wearer as a devout servant of the universal Church was quietly re-edited to frame him as the head of his own, royal sanctity displacing divine imagery on the very object that made a king. The original's fate is the darker story. After Charles the First was executed in 1649, Parliament ordered the royal regalia, the accumulated crowns, sceptres and plate of centuries, broken up and melted, partly for revenue and partly as ideology, the physical abolition of monarchy. The Tudor Crown went into the melting pot at the Tower, its gold struck into coin, its jewels sold off piecemeal, which is why the crown jewels you can see in London today date almost entirely from the Restoration onward. There is one tantalizing footnote. In 2017 a metal detectorist unearthed a small gold figurine of Henry the Sixth, and it has been suggested, though it cannot be proven, that it once stood on this very crown, a single survivor of the furnace. The replica before you was made in 2012 by Harry Collins, then the Queen's crown jeweller, and his goldsmiths, working from Tudor inventories and from the crown's appearance in old royal portraits to restore each of the three hundred and forty-four stones and pearls to its recorded place. It sits where Henry wore it, under the ceiling he built. As a piece of scholarship it is impeccable. As an object lesson, a crown remade because a republic once turned its original into cash, it is hard to beat.
