# A King's Day
Imagine you are the king of Scots, waking in this palace on an ordinary morning around the year fifteen hundred. The first thing to understand is that you are almost never alone. From the moment you wake, servants and courtiers are around you, helping you dress, because dressing the king was not a private chore but a privilege that important men actually competed for. Every small act of your day, washing, dressing, eating, praying, was performed in front of an audience, because a king who could not be seen could not rule. Power in this world was a performance, and the stage never closed.

Early in the day you would hear mass. James the Fourth, who refounded the Chapel Royal here at Stirling in fifteen oh one with a grand new choir, was genuinely devout and heard mass daily, and he liked to be surrounded by music almost constantly. Faith and beauty and power were all, for a king, the same single tool.

A meal was a ceremony before it was food. The king dined in his hall, often raised on a dais above everyone else, and his food was tasted before he touched it, in case of poison. The finest spices, sugar, and wine, all imported at great expense, were paraded as proof of his reach across the world. And yet the king you are playing ruled one of the poorer kingdoms of Europe, with perhaps a fifth of England's population, so much of this magnificence was borrowed dazzle, an argument made in gold that Scotland mattered as much as anyone.

Afternoons often meant the hunt. The royal park below the castle, and the great forests beyond, were stocked with deer, and hunting was more than sport. It was training for war, a display of nerve and horsemanship, and a chance to talk business with your nobles at a gallop. James the Fourth was a restless, curious man who also dabbled in alchemy and even in amateur dentistry, paying his own courtiers small sums to let him pull their teeth, which tells you something about the confidence of a Renaissance king who believed he could master any art he chose.

But the real work of kingship was grinding and dangerous. Scotland was a fractious country of powerful, armed families who did not always obey, and a Stewart king spent much of his life on the move, riding from town to town to hold his travelling courts of justice, showing his face, settling feuds, and reminding everyone who was in charge. He kept no standing army and no police, only his own authority and the loyalty he could win or frighten out of his great men. So behind the feasting and the music and the hunt lay a constant, wary calculation. Who can I trust, who is plotting, who must be flattered, and who must be crushed? That was the true daily labour on this rock, and it never stopped.
