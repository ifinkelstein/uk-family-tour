# Islands That Were Churches, Forts and Prisons
Now count the islands. Go on, look all around the firth and see how many little islands and rocky lumps you can spot poking out of the water. There are lots of them. And here is the amazing part. Almost every single one used to have a job.

Think about it. An island is a bit of land with water all the way around it. That makes it two things at once. It's safe, because nobody can sneak up on you without a boat. And it's a trap, because if you're stuck there, you can't get off. People all through history looked at these islands and thought, aha, I know exactly what to do with that.

Long, long ago, holy men called monks looked at the islands and thought, peace and quiet, perfect for praying. So they rowed out and built little churches, far away from noisy villages, with nothing around them but sea and sky and squawking birds. The island you are heading to, Inchcolm, was one of these. Monks lived out there for hundreds of years.

But other people looked at the very same islands and had very different ideas. Soldiers thought, what a brilliant fort. An island right in the middle of the water could watch every enemy ship and blast it with cannons. So some islands got covered in guns and walls. And some islands got an even spookier job. They became prisons, or places to send people who were terribly sick, rowed out and left there so their illness could not spread to everyone on land. Imagine being dropped on a lonely rock in the middle of the firth, all on your own. Brrr.

So as you sail past each island today, play a game. Ask it, quietly, were you a peaceful church, a mighty fort, or a spooky prison? And here is the twist. Some of them, believe it or not, were all three, at different times in their long, strange lives.
