# The Old Town Jail
A little further down the hill stands a grim, castle-like building with a round tower, and it has a suitably grim history. This is the Old Town Jail, and if it is open you can go in and be shown round by a costumed guide playing a Victorian jailer, which the children will either love or find delightfully alarming.

To understand why it was built, you have to know what came before it. For centuries, Stirling's prisoners were held in the Tolbooth, down in the marketplace, a cramped, filthy, stinking hole. In 1842 a prison inspector visited and declared it, in writing, one of the worst jails in the whole of Britain. That was embarrassing enough to force action, and in 1847 this new jail opened, designed by an Edinburgh architect named Thomas Brown.

It was, believe it or not, meant to be an improvement, even a reform. It followed a strict new idea called the separate system. Every prisoner had his own individual cell, and the whole point was silence and separation. Prisoners were kept apart so they could not teach one another bad habits, set to hard work, and taught to read. In theory it was humane. In practice, being kept in near total silence and solitude, day after day, month after month, drove some prisoners to despair, and a few clean out of their minds. Reform and cruelty, wearing the very same uniform.

The jail later became a military prison, holding soldiers from the castle garrison up above until the 1930s, and then it fell into ruin before being rescued and turned into the visitor attraction it is today. As you are led from cell to cell you will hear about the hangman, the punishments, the escape attempts, and the everyday misery of Victorian imprisonment. It is vivid, a little theatrical, and a useful reminder that the pretty town you are strolling through was, for the people who broke its laws, not pretty in the slightest.
