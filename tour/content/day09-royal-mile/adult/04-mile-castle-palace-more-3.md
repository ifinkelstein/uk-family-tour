# Tell Me More: Festival City

You've picked a good week to walk this street, because in summer Edinburgh becomes the busiest stage on Earth, and the Mile is its main boards. The story starts in nineteen forty-seven. Europe was digging out of the war, and Edinburgh launched a grand International Festival to bring the arts, and a bruised continent, back together. That same year eight theatre companies turned up who hadn't been invited, and rather than go home they found small halls and put their shows on anyway, around the edges of the official programme. A journalist soon called them the fringe of the festival, the name stuck, and that uninvited overspill grew into the Edinburgh Festival Fringe, now comfortably the largest arts festival in the world, thousands of performances a day every August, in every basement, church hall and back room the city can offer.

During the Fringe, the upper Mile becomes one long, glorious scrum of performers. Jugglers, unicyclists, a man escaping from a straitjacket, student Hamlets and stand-up comedians all working the crowd at once, thrusting flyers into every hand, because on this street the pavement is the audition. It is chaotic, delightful, and completely free to wander through.

You're here in July, a few weeks before that August madness, but Edinburgh doesn't wait for August to make music. Right now the city is in the middle of its Jazz and Blues Festival, which began back in nineteen seventy-eight in the city's pubs and now fills ten days each July with well over a hundred concerts. You'll be going to some of it, which puts you inside a genuine Edinburgh tradition rather than merely watching one.

And today, when your walk ends near the Mound and Princes Street, you'll find a festival carnival waiting, with rides and colour and noise laid on right beside those gardens that were once a filthy drained loch. Keep that in mind as you climb onto something that spins. A few hundred years ago this was the worst-smelling corner of Edinburgh. Now it throws a party every summer. The city that invented the Fringe has never really stopped performing.
