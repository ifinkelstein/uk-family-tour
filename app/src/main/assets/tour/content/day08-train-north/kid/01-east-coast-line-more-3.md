# The Bridges of the Tyne and the Giant Angel
Coming up is one of the best window moments of the whole trip, so get ready. When your train reaches the city of Newcastle, it crosses a wide river called the Tyne, high up on a bridge, and for about ten seconds you get a view of a whole family of bridges all crowded together over the water, one after another after another. See how many you can count.

There's a fat green one shaped like a giant arch, called the Tyne Bridge. There's a clever one that actually tilts and lifts up to let boats through, and when it opens it looks exactly like a giant winking eye, which is why everyone calls it the Blinking Eye. And there's an old double-decker bridge with trains running on the top and cars driving underneath, built more than a hundred and seventy years ago by the son of the man they call the father of the railways. All of them, packed into one short stretch of river. It's like the bridges are showing off.

Then, a few minutes later, look to the side for something even bigger, standing up on a hill. A giant. It's a statue called the Angel of the North, and it is enormous. It stands as tall as five double-decker buses stacked on top of each other, and its two outstretched wings are as wide as the wings of a passenger jet, tip to tip. It's made of rust-colored steel, the same tough steel they use to build ships, and it was made rusty on purpose, so it turns a lovely warm orange-brown and never needs painting. It has stood on that hill since 1998, welcoming everyone who travels north, with its wings held out just a little bit forward, almost as if it's about to give the whole of Northern England a hug.

More than thirty million people see the Angel every year, most of them from cars and trains just like yours. Here's a fact to boast about later: the whole thing weighs about two hundred tonnes, heavier than a hundred cars put together, and it was built strong enough to stand up in winds fierce enough to knock you flat on your back. Now you're one of them. Wave hello. And keep counting those sheep and castles, because you're getting close to Scotland now, and the seaside part of the ride, with the real castles by the water, is about to begin.
