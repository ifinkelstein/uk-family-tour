# The Palace Was a City
Every room you walk today was once somebody's workplace, and it is worth spending one chapter on the people who never appear in the portraits. A Tudor royal household on the move numbered six hundred or more entitled mouths, but around and beneath them worked the true population of the palace, the servants of the servants, the suppliers, the specialists, and together they made Hampton Court less a house than a small working town with a king living in the best street. The town had its own government. In rooms near the kitchens sat the Board of Green Cloth, the household's accounting office, named with perfect literal-mindedness for the green cloth covering its counting table, and across that table flowed the wages, the provisioning contracts, and the discipline of the whole operation. It audited what came in, oxen, ale, wax, firewood, and what everyone was owed, because service at court was a salaried, regulated career. Rank determined everything, including dinner. The entitlement was called bouche of court, literally mouth at court, your allowance of bread, ale, candles and fuel, graded precisely by status, so that a gentleman drew wine and wax candles where a groom drew ale and tallow. The town had trades. Laundresses, among the very few women employed in this overwhelmingly male world, and the king's own laundress, a position of surprising trust, since the royal linen touched the royal body and its handling was a security matter as much as a chore. Gardeners by the dozen, keeping the orchards, herbers and fish ponds that fed and decorated the estate. Watermen, the taxi drivers of the Thames, rowing courtiers and messages between here and Westminster on every tide. Purveyors ranging the countryside with the crown's power to buy provisions at fixed prices, a system farmers loathed. Porters at the gates controlling who entered, the palace's police. And the town had housing, of a sort. Great officers received lodgings whose size and location broadcast their standing, while the boys and grooms at the bottom slept where they worked, on kitchen floors warm from the fires, in garrets, in shared beds, tucked into the enormous building like crew in a ship. When the court moved on, most of this town moved with it, carts, servants, furniture, tapestries stripped from the walls, the whole apparatus trundling to the next palace while the cleaners moved in behind. So as you cross the base court, repopulate it. Not kings, but brewers rolling barrels, a laundress with a basket of royal shirts, a page running an errand, six hundred dinners being reckoned at a green table. Magnificence was the product. This was the factory.
