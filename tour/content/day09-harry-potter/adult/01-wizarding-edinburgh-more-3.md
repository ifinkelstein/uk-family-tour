# Diagon Alley and the Legend Machine

Victoria Street may be the most photographed hundred meters in Scotland. It sweeps down from George IV Bridge to the Grassmarket in one long candy-colored curve, shopfronts in red and blue and mustard yellow, with a second tier of shops running along a terrace above. The composition is so good it looks art-directed, and in a sense it was. The street is a planned piece of eighteen twenties and thirties town improvement, built to replace the West Bow, a notoriously steep zigzag lane that had connected the Grassmarket to the high town since medieval times. It was renamed for the new queen, Victoria, in eighteen thirty-seven.

Today the street has effectively been annexed by the wizarding world. There's a Potter merchandise emporium at number forty styling itself Diagon House, another Potter shop further up, and a steady congregation of visitors framing the same downhill photograph, because everyone has read that this curving street of odd little shops was Rowling's model for Diagon Alley. Some versions add that a joke shop traded for years near the bottom, an obvious Weasleys' Wizard Wheezes, and older Edinburgh hands do remember a real joke shop down at the West Bow end, which gave the legend a lovely piece of supporting evidence.

Then in July twenty twenty-two, Rowling pulled the tablecloth off. No real street inspired Diagon Alley, she wrote. It came out of her head. She'd never seen ninety-nine percent of the places claiming to be the inspiration, and she'd never seen Victoria Street at all when she created Diagon Alley, though she has since, she added dryly, because she lives here. In the same thread she waved away the Greyfriars gravestone tours. The city's whole inspiration map, in three tweets. York's famous Shambles has received the same treatment, as have various cafes, schools and alleyways across Britain. She once told a fan that a claimed Diagon Alley in Exeter was nothing like the one in her head.

So why does the legend keep growing when the author herself keeps killing it? Partly money, obviously. Potter tourism is worth serious revenue to Edinburgh, and nobody puts up a plaque saying the author never walked here. But the deeper reason is more forgivable. When a story matters to millions of people, they want it to have a geography. Pilgrims need shrines. If the real Diagon Alley existed only inside one woman's head, it can't be visited, so the collective imagination goes looking for the nearest worthy stand-in, and Victoria Street, curved, colorful and slightly unreal, was always going to be elected. The claim is false and the casting is perfect, and both things are true at once.

There's one more irony, and it's the best thing on the street. Edinburgh didn't need to invent a wizard for Victoria Street, because it already had one. In the sixteen hundreds, the West Bow's most respected resident was Major Thomas Weir, a fiercely devout soldier famous for his public praying. In sixteen seventy, unprompted, Weir confessed to a secret life of appalling crimes and sorcery, and Edinburgh executed him for witchcraft. His sister claimed his walking staff had magical powers. For over a century afterward his house stood empty, the most haunted address in the city, and locals swore his ghost galloped out of the Bow at midnight. The Wizard of the West Bow was terrifying local legend three hundred years before anyone drew a lightning bolt on a boy's forehead. Cities don't grow legends around beloved books by accident. This one has had the habit for centuries.
