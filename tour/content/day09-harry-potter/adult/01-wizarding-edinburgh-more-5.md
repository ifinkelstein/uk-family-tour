# Hogwarts Is in Scotland

Here's a question the books never quite answer out loud, and Rowling only settled in interviews. Where is Hogwarts? The clues are all there. The Hogwarts Express leaves from King's Cross in London and travels north for an entire day, into mountains and moorland, arriving after dark. Rowling spelled it out in a two thousand and two television interview. She had always imagined Hogwarts in Scotland, she said, and a British reader would work it out, because a day's journey north from King's Cross lands you here. The castle by its great lake sits somewhere in the Highlands, unplottable, hidden from Muggle eyes. So for the rest of your Scottish week, you are, canonically speaking, in Hogwarts country.

The filmmakers took the geography seriously, and their choices created a second wave of pilgrimage sites, these ones genuine. The Hogwarts Express itself is played by the Jacobite, a real steam service that still runs in summer between Fort William and Mallaig on the West Highland Line, regularly voted among the most beautiful railway journeys in the world. Its signature moment, in the films and in life, is the crossing of the Glenfinnan Viaduct, a great curved span of twenty-one concrete arches completed in nineteen oh one by the contractor Robert McAlpine, nicknamed Concrete Bob for his enthusiasm for the then-novel material. When Harry and Ron chase the train in the flying Ford Anglia in Chamber of Secrets, it's the Glenfinnan Viaduct beneath them. On summer days now, hundreds of people stand on the hillside to watch the steam train take the curve, and the sound carries across the valley like a film score.

Below the viaduct lies Loch Shiel, which served as the Black Lake beneath Hogwarts in several films, with other Highland lochs, Eilt and Morar among them, filling in for close-ups and shorelines. A tiny islet on Loch Eilt, crowned with a few wind-bent pines, became Dumbledore's island tomb. And in a nice collision of legends, the head of Loch Shiel is where the actual Jacobites began. The tall monument on the shore marks the spot where Bonnie Prince Charlie raised his standard in seventeen forty-five and launched the rising the steam train is named for. One valley, two doomed romantic causes, one real and one fictional, sharing a railway bridge.

Which brings us to your own itinerary, because this family is spending several days inside the landscape that made everyone believe a hidden castle was plausible in the first place. Tomorrow's boat to Inchcolm delivers you to a ruined island abbey in the Firth of Forth, grey towers, seabirds, water on all sides, about as wizarding-world a site as Britain offers without a ticket from Universal. Stirling gives you a working royal castle on a volcanic crag, great hall and all. And from the top of Arthur's Seat, Edinburgh's own extinct volcano, you can look north toward the Highlands and understand the trick. Scotland genuinely is scattered with lochs that hide things and castles with no roads to them. The books only claimed one more than the maps show. In Goblet of Fire, Hermione explains that Muggles who stumble close to Hogwarts see only a mouldering ruin with warning signs. Scotland has hundreds of mouldering ruins. Officially, every single one of them is nothing. Keep an eye on them anyway.
