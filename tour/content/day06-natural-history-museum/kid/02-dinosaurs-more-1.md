# Dippy the Dinosaur's Big Adventure
Before Hope the whale hung in the great hall, somebody else stood there for a very long time. His name was Dippy, and he was a Diplodocus, a long-necked dinosaur so enormous that his neck and tail together stretched more than twenty-six metres, nose to tail-tip, right across the hall.

But here is a secret about Dippy. He is not made of real bones. Dippy is a copy, a cast made of plaster, painted to look like fossil. The real Diplodocus was dug up in America, and a very rich man named Andrew Carnegie was so proud of it that he had copies made and sent them to museums all over the world. London's Dippy arrived more than a hundred years ago, in nineteen hundred and five. For decades children grew up meeting Dippy, and from the nineteen seventies he stood right in the centre of the entrance hall, the first thing you saw when you walked in.

So when the museum announced in twenty seventeen that Dippy would be taken down and a whale would take his place, a lot of people were upset. There were newspaper headlines. There was even a campaign called Save Dippy. Britain loved that dinosaur.

But Dippy did not retire. He went on the adventure of a lifetime. Because he is a cast, not fragile fossil, he could travel, so the museum took him apart, packed him into crates, and sent him on a tour all around the United Kingdom. Dippy stood in cathedrals with stained glass windows soaring above him. He visited museums in cities far from London, in Scotland, in Wales, in Northern Ireland. Millions of people who could never get to London came to meet him, many of them seeing a dinosaur up close for the very first time.

And where is Dippy now? After his grand tour he settled into a museum in the city of Coventry, where you can still go and stand under him today. So both stories have a happy ending. Hope the whale reminds us to protect the living world, and Dippy the dinosaur went out and travelled the whole country, meeting more people than he ever met standing still in London. Two great giants, and the museum found a clever way to share them both.
