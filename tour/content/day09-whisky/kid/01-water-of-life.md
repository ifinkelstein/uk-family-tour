# Scotland's Water of Life
Here is a funny thing about Scotland. It has a very famous drink, and its name means "the water of life." Really! Long, long ago, clever monks made a special drink and gave it a name in the old Scottish language, Gaelic. They called it uisge beatha, which sounds a bit like "oosh-ka bah," and it means the water of life. Over hundreds and hundreds of years, people said it faster and faster, until uisge beatha turned into just one short word: whisky.

Now, here is the important part, so listen closely. Whisky is a strong drink for grown-ups only. It is not for children, not even a tiny sip, and honestly most kids think it smells like fiery medicine anyway. Yuck! But, and this is the exciting bit, the way whisky is MADE is one of the most amazing things in all of Scotland, and it is wonderful to watch and to learn about. Giant copper kettles as tall as a house. Barrels full of golden liquid, sleeping in the dark for ten, twenty, even thirty years. A part that magically vanishes into the air, and people call it the angels' share, because they say the angels drank it. Secret smugglers hiding from the taxman up in the hills. There is even a bit of real science in it, all about smells.

So think of whisky the way you might think of blowing glass, or forging a sword, or baking in a huge old bakery. It is a craft, a Scottish craft that the whole world knows about. Over the next few stories I am going to show you how it is made, why some kinds smell like smoke and some like apples, and the sneaky history of the people who made it in secret. Come on. Let's go and meet the water of life.
