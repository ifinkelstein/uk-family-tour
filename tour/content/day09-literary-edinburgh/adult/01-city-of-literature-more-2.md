# Sir Walter Scott, the Wizard of the North

Imagine a writer so famous that a great city names its main railway station after one of his novels. Not after him, after the book. When you arrive into the heart of Edinburgh by train, you step off at Waverley Station, and it is named for Waverley, the novel Walter Scott published in eighteen fourteen. It is, as far as anyone can tell, the only railway station in the world named after a work of fiction. That single odd fact tells you how enormous Scott once was.

In his own lifetime he was arguably the most famous writer alive anywhere on the planet, read hungrily from Edinburgh to Boston to Moscow. And he more or less invented something we now take for granted, the historical novel. Before Scott, novels were mostly set in the writer's own day. He had the idea of dropping invented characters into real, carefully researched history, the Jacobite risings, the Crusades, medieval England, and letting them live through great events. Waverley did it first, and it sold out in days. Then came Rob Roy, and Ivanhoe, with its jousting knights and its Robin Hood, and a whole torrent of others, written at a furious pace. They called him the Wizard of the North.

Scott did something else, too, that reaches far beyond books. He essentially designed the picture of Scotland that the world still carries in its head, all tartan and bagpipes and misty Highland romance. In eighteen twenty-two, when King George the Fourth came to Edinburgh, the first reigning monarch to visit in almost two hundred years, it was Scott who stage-managed the entire spectacle, wrapping the city and the rather plump king alike in Highland plaid. It was gloriously invented, a Lowland lawyer dressing up history, and yet it stuck so hard that we are still living inside his shortbread-tin Scotland today.

And here is a thread that ties straight back to Edinburgh Castle. A few years earlier, in eighteen eighteen, Scotland's ancient crown jewels, the Honours of Scotland, the oldest crown jewels in the British Isles, had been missing for more than a century, locked away and forgotten after the union with England. It was Walter Scott who got royal permission to hunt for them, and it was Scott who stood there when a sealed chamber in the castle was broken open and the crown, sword and sceptre were found inside, exactly where they had been abandoned. If you saw them glinting behind glass at the castle today, you have Scott the storyteller to thank. He treated his country's history as a treasure worth digging for, and in that case quite literally.

You cannot miss his memorial. On Princes Street, right beside the station that bears his book's name, stands the Scott Monument, a soaring, blackened Gothic spire more than sixty metres high, with Scott carved in white marble beneath it and his beloved dog at his feet. It is often called the largest monument to a writer anywhere in the world. Opinions on his novels have cooled over two centuries, and some find them heavy going now. But no writer ever shaped a nation's image of itself more completely, and no city ever said thank you in taller stone.
