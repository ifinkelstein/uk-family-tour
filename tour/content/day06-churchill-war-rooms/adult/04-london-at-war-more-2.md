# The Vengeance Weapons and the Long View
The war did not spare London even in its final act. Just as Britons dared to believe the worst was behind them, a new and eerie form of attack arrived. From June 1944 came the V-1 flying bomb, a pilotless jet-driven missile the public nicknamed the doodlebug. You could hear its engine droning overhead, and the terror lay in the moment the engine cut out, because that meant it was falling, and you had perhaps a dozen seconds to take cover. Londoners learned to dread the silence.

Worse followed. From September 1944 came the V-2, the world's first long-range ballistic missile, which fell from the edge of space faster than sound. There was no warning whatsoever, no drone, no siren, simply a colossal explosion out of a clear sky, and only afterwards the thunderclap of its descent. The V stood for Vergeltung, vengeance, and these weapons killed thousands in the war's closing months, a cruel coda for a city that had already endured so much.

Step back, then, and consider the arc these rooms witnessed. From the first sirens of 1939, through the desperate solitude of 1940, the long grind of the Blitz, and finally the rockets of 1944, the men and women in this bunker worked on, never certain of the outcome. That uncertainty is the thing most easily lost from a distance of eighty years. They did not know they would win. They chose to carry on as though they might. As you return to the warmth and the noise of a peaceful London evening, that is the inheritance this quiet, cool, concrete place leaves you.
