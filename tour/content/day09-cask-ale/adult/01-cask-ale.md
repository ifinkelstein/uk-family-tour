# Cask Ale: Britain's Living Beer
Beer may be the oldest drink humanity ever made on purpose. In a cave near Haifa, archaeologists found traces of brewing about thirteen thousand years old, from before farming, before the wheel, before writing. Some scholars even argue we settled down and planted the first fields of grain partly to keep the beer coming, which would make beer not a by-product of civilisation but one of its causes. Four thousand years ago the Sumerians were singing a hymn to Ninkasi, their goddess of brewing, and the hymn doubles as a recipe. Wherever people grew grain, beer followed, from Egypt to China to the muddy villages of Bronze Age Britain.

So beer is everywhere, and almost everywhere it is now made the same industrial way, filtered, pasteurised, fizzed up with gas, sealed in a can. Dead, in the kindest possible sense. But you have landed in the one country that never quite let the old way die. It is called cask ale, or real ale, and it is beer that arrives at the pub still alive, still quietly fermenting in its barrel in the cellar below the bar, finished not by a factory but by the publican, and pulled up to your glass with a long hand-worked lever. No other country on earth serves its national beer this way as a matter of course. It is one of Britain's great unsung traditions, hiding in plain sight on every proper pub counter.

The clue to look for is a row of tall handles standing on the bar like oars. Over the next few stories I'll tell you how beer is made at all, what makes cask ale different and why it nearly vanished, why Edinburgh was once as famous for beer as for whisky, how to read a pub like a local, and how British beer went out and conquered the world. Then you can go and find a living pint yourself.
