# Scotch, Irish, and the World's Whiskies
Scotch is not the only whisky in the world, only the most famous. Once you notice the family, you start to spot the cousins everywhere. They share one idea, grain fermented and distilled and aged in wood, but each country bends the recipe its own way, and you can taste who did what.

Start at home. Scotch is made chiefly from malted barley, usually distilled twice, sometimes touched with peat smoke, and the law says it must sit in oak casks in Scotland for at least three years before anyone may call it Scotch. Look at the spelling, too. In Scotland there is no letter e. It is whisky.

Cross to Ireland and an e appears. Irish whiskey is usually distilled three times instead of twice, and that extra pass sands off more of the rough edges, so the classic Irish style is smooth and light and honeyed rather than smoky. Peat is rare there. The story goes that Irish distillers added the e in the eighteen hundreds to set their bottles apart from Scotch, and the habit stuck. Irish immigrants carried the spelling across the Atlantic, which is why America writes whiskey too.

American whiskey is a different creature, and the difference is the grain and the wood. Bourbon, from Kentucky and beyond, is made mostly from corn, which gives it a broad sweetness, and it must be aged in brand-new oak barrels that have been charred, burnt black on the inside. That fresh scorched wood floods it with vanilla and caramel and coconut, which is why bourbon tastes so round and sweet beside a Scotch. And here is the lovely link back to Scotland. Once an American barrel has held bourbon, the law forbids using it for bourbon again, so it is shipped off to Scotland and filled with Scotch. A great deal of Scotch grows up in second-hand bourbon barrels. Rye whiskey is bourbon's sharper sibling, made mostly from rye grain, all pepper and spice.

Then there is Japan, the newcomer that startled everybody. A century ago a Japanese distiller came to Scotland, learned the craft properly, went home and built distilleries that copied the Scottish way almost line for line, down to the shape of the stills. The Japanese spell it whisky, no e, out of respect for the teacher. For decades the world paid no attention. Then Japanese bottles began winning the top prizes on earth, and now they stand among the finest made anywhere, priced to match.

So the label gives you a clue before you taste a drop. Scotland, Japan and Canada write whisky. Ireland and the United States write whiskey. One letter, and behind it the whole tangled history of who learned from whom.
