# Beer's Greatest Journeys
Every beer style is a souvenir of a journey, and the most famous journey of all is the one to India. The romantic version goes like this. British beer spoiled on the six-month sail around Africa, so a clever London brewer named George Hodgson invented a new beer, loaded with extra hops and extra strength to survive the voyage, and India Pale Ale was born. It is a lovely story, and beer historians have taken it apart almost line by line. Hodgson's Bow Brewery, from the seventeen eighties, really did dominate the India trade, but mostly because it sat conveniently close to the East India Company's docks and offered the ships' officers generous credit. The beer he sent was October ale, a strong, heavily hopped pale beer the English had already been brewing for a century, which simply happened to travel magnificently. Nothing was invented for India, and plenty of ordinary porter made the same voyage in the same holds and arrived just fine. What India provided was a market, and eventually a name. So tell the legend if you like, but tell it as a legend.

When Hodgson got greedy and tried to cut out the middlemen, the East India traders took their business to Burton upon Trent, where the gypsum-rich water turned out to make pale bitter beer better than anywhere on earth, and Burton became the beer capital of the Empire. Edinburgh's brewers piled into the same trade, and by Victorian times Younger's and McEwan's ales were drunk from Bombay to Melbourne to San Francisco. The other great British export was the dark family, porter and stout, born in eighteenth-century London as the drink of the porters who hauled the city's goods, brewed black and roasty, and perfected across the Irish Sea in Dublin, where a brewer named Guinness bet everything on it.

Then the world changed teams. In Bavaria, brewers had long stored their beer through the summer in cold caves, and in that cold a different yeast slowly took over, one that ferments at low temperatures and drops to the bottom of the vat, giving a cleaner, crisper beer. Lager, from the German word for storeroom. In 1842 the Bohemian town of Pilsen hired a Bavarian brewer named Josef Groll, who combined that cold fermentation with very pale malt and soft local water, and produced the first golden lager the world had seen. In an age of new clear glassware, a sparkling gold beer was irresistible, and pilsner-style lager went on to conquer essentially the entire planet. Ninety-something percent of all beer drunk on earth today is its descendant.

Britain held out, kept its hand pumps and its living ale, and by the late twentieth century that made it a museum. Luckily. American homebrewers and small craft brewers, in revolt against bland industrial lager, went looking for flavour, found the old British styles, and rebuilt them with turbocharged American hops. The modern IPA, the flagship of the craft beer movement, is a British colonial export re-imported home, louder and pushier than it left. Scotland joined the wave with gusto, from a swaggering brewery up in Aberdeenshire that became a global brand to dozens of small outfits around Edinburgh quietly filling casks again.

Which brings you back to the bar, and the row of tall handles. Beer has sailed to India, gone underground in Bavaria, turned gold in Bohemia, and circled the planet in cans. After all that, the rarest thing you can order anywhere in the world of beer is the simplest thing on offer here, a pint that an hour ago was still quietly alive in the cool dark under your feet. Order that one.
