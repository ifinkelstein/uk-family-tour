# Crossing into Scotland
Just after the town of Berwick-upon-Tweed, your train rumbles over the Royal Border Bridge — twenty-eight huge stone arches, forty meters above the River Tweed, opened by Queen Victoria in 1850. A few minutes later, you cross an invisible line: the border between England and Scotland. No fence, no passport check — but you're now in a different country within the United Kingdom, and you'll notice it fast. Scotland has its own flag, the white X on blue, called the Saltire. Its own banknotes — Scottish pounds, which are real money but look completely different. Its own national animal, which is, genuinely, the unicorn. And its own accent, which you'll be happily confused by within the hour. Poor Berwick, the town you just passed, changed hands between England and Scotland more than a dozen times over the centuries as the two countries fought over it — it's England's most bitten-over town, and its football team, funnily enough, plays in the Scottish leagues to this day. As you get close to Edinburgh, watch the right side for the sea and the left for hills. When you step out at Waverley station, look up: you'll be in a valley with a castle on a cliff right above you. That's tomorrow's adventure staring down at you.
