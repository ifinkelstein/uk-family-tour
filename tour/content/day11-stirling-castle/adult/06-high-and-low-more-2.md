# The Favourite and the Keeper
A king could not run a kingdom alone, so the most important question in Scottish politics was always the same. Who stands next to the throne? The men, and occasionally women, who had the king's ear held the real levers of power, and there were two ways to get there.

The first road was royal favour. A clever, charming, or useful person, sometimes of quite modest birth, could catch a king's eye and rise astonishingly fast, gathering titles, lands, and offices as gifts. But favour was a fragile thing. What the king gives, the king, or the king's jealous nobles, can take away, and Scottish history is littered with favourites who climbed high and then fell suddenly and violently when the mood turned or the old king died. Standing close to the throne was warm, and it was also exactly where the lightning struck.

The second road to power was blood. Scotland was dominated by a handful of great families, the earls and lords whose ancestors had held vast lands and private armies for generations. These men did not need the king's favour to be powerful. They were powerful already, and a wise king had to manage them, marry into them, reward them, and just occasionally destroy them. You have already heard, at a certain window in this castle, what could happen when a king decided that one of these over-mighty families had simply grown too dangerous to live with. Standing near a king was perilous for the greatest noble just as it was for the humblest favourite.

Stirling itself was bound to one such family, the Erskines. For generation after generation the Erskines were the hereditary keepers of this castle, the men entrusted with holding the rock itself, and, remarkably, with something even more precious. Five successive generations of Erskines were made guardians of Scotland's royal children. It was an Erskine who guarded the boy James the Fifth, an Erskine who was handed the infant Mary, Queen of Scots, and an Erskine, by then raised to be Earl of Mar, who was given custody of the baby who became James the Sixth. To be trusted with the heir to the throne was the highest honour a family could hold, and it was also a heavy and dangerous responsibility, because whoever controlled the royal child held a grip on the future itself.

That same Earl of Mar climbed higher still, becoming for a short spell the Regent, the effective ruler of all Scotland during James the Sixth's childhood, before dying, worn out, within about a year of taking the burden up. If you walk down into the town of Stirling you can still see the ruined front of Mar's Wark, the grand townhouse the family built to advertise their standing, a brag in stone that has outlived them by four and a half centuries. It is a good emblem of the whole business of noble power. Reach for the top of the ladder, build something magnificent to prove you got there, and pray the fall, when it comes, will not be too far.
