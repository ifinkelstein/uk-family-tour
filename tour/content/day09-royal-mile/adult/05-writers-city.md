# The Writers' City

Halfway down the Lawnmarket, duck into Lady Stair's Close, and you'll find a tall, crow-stepped house from sixteen twenty-two that holds the Writers' Museum. It's small, free, and devoted to three men who between them more or less invented how the world imagines Scotland: Robert Burns, Walter Scott and Robert Louis Stevenson. Inside are Burns's writing desk, the press that printed Scott's Waverley novels, and Stevenson's riding boots and the ring engraved with his Samoan name. Three writers, one close, and a city that treats them the way other cities treat saints.

That's not sentiment, it's official. In two thousand and four Edinburgh was named the world's first UNESCO City of Literature, the founding member of a network that now spans the globe. And you can see why the honour landed here. This is the city that gave us the first Encyclopaedia Britannica, Scott's invention of the historical novel, Stevenson's Treasure Island and Jekyll and Hyde, Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes, Muriel Spark's Miss Jean Brodie, Ian Rankin's modern crime novels, and, in the cafés around the corner, a good deal of a certain boy wizard. Few square miles anywhere have printed more of what the world reads.

Before you leave the close, look down at the flagstones. This is Makars' Court, and the stones are carved with lines from Scottish writers stretching back six hundred years. Makar is the old Scots word for a poet, literally a maker, one who makes with words, and Scotland still appoints a national Makar, a kind of poet laureate. It's a lovely thing, an outdoor floor you read like a page, quotations underfoot from voices most visitors have never heard of, laid down among the ones they have.

And here's a small private joke for your family. The night before this walk you had dinner at a place called Makars, over bowls of mashed potato and, no doubt, some haggis. Well, now you know what the word means, and there's a fitting footnote to it. Burns wrote the most famous poem ever written about haggis, his mock-heroic Address to a Haggis, and it first appeared in print right here in Edinburgh, in the edition of his poems published in this very neighbourhood in seventeen eighty-seven. So you ate at the poets' table without knowing it. In this city, even the mash has a literary pedigree.
