# The Man Who Found Out the Earth Is Super Old
Now for a true story about a big idea, and it started right here on rocks like these. A really long time ago, most grown-ups thought the Earth was only about six thousand years old. That sounds like a lot, but it is not, not really. Six thousand years is younger than some trees that are alive today.

Then a man from Edinburgh named James Hutton went walking around hills exactly like this one, back in the seventeen hundreds. He was a farmer who loved rocks, and he stared at them really, really carefully. And he noticed something that did not add up. He could see that some rock had once been runny and hot, squeezed up from deep underground. And he could see that other rock was made of tiny grains, squished mud from the bottom of an ancient sea, packed down hard over ages and ages. He realized all of this had taken a mind-bending amount of time. Not thousands of years. Millions and millions and millions of years.

This idea got a name. People call it deep time. It just means the Earth is so unbelievably, ridiculously old that your whole life, even the lives of your great great grandparents all added together, is less than a single blink. Hutton was one of the very first people to figure this out, by reading it in rocks like the ones under your feet. That is why some people call him the father of geology, which is the science of rocks and the Earth. Pretty good for a fellow who liked walking up hills.

And now the wow. The hill you are climbing today, Arthur's Seat, is a real wild mountain. It has long grass, rocky cliffs, and big birds circling overhead, and you might spot rabbits. But look around, because it is sitting right in the middle of a busy capital city, next door to a palace where a king or queen actually stays. Almost no other capital in the whole world has a wild mountain plonked in the middle of it. And you get to climb it, for free, today. Not many kids can say they climbed a volcano before lunch.
