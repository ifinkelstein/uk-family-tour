# Stirling's Old Town: An Optional Walk Downhill
If your legs are willing and the light is good, here is a walk that turns one great sight into two. Everything you have just seen up in the castle happened at the top of a very steep street, and that street is the old town of Stirling, tumbling down the tail of the crag toward the river. You can walk the whole of it in well under an hour, all downhill, and it is arguably the finest surviving medieval townscape in Scotland.

From the esplanade, the route is simply this: follow the road down. Within a minute or two you reach the grand ruined face of Mar's Wark and, tucked just behind it, the medieval town church, the Church of the Holy Rude, with its old graveyard spilling down the hillside. Below the church stands the Old Town Jail. Keep going and the street becomes Broad Street, the old marketplace, with its market cross and its Tolbooth, the little building that was town hall, courtroom and prison all at once. From there you can carry on down through the lower town, or slip onto the Back Walk, the old path that hugs the surviving town wall, and follow it round toward the Old Bridge, the stone crossing that inherited the job of Wallace's famous timber one.

Think of it as the whole story seen from below. Up in the castle you met kings and queens and the great sweep of Scottish history. Down here you meet the town that fed them, jailed its thieves, sold its cloth, buried its dead, and once crowned a king in its parish church. Same hill, same centuries, a completely different view. Whenever you are ready, let us walk down.
