# The Names, the City, and the Iolaire
You've stood, or you will stand, in the Scottish National War Memorial at the top of the castle, so let's talk about what those rolls of honour actually count, because the numbers are argued over to this day. The rolls hold roughly one hundred and forty-seven thousand names from the First World War. Historians point out that the figure includes non-Scots who served in Scottish regiments and some double counting, and the Oxford war historian Hew Strachan has argued the true Scottish death toll is probably nearer one hundred thousand, while others insist Scotland's losses were proportionally far heavier than the rest of Britain's. The honest answer is a range, somewhere between one hundred thousand and one hundred and fifty thousand dead from a country of under five million people. Whichever figure you take, the loss reached into every street, and once you start looking, Edinburgh is quietly covered in the marks of it. On the Royal Mile outside the City Chambers sits the Stone of Remembrance, where the city lays its wreaths each November. At Haymarket, west of Princes Street, a clock tower in the middle of the junction is the memorial to the Heart of Midlothian footballers who enlisted together in nineteen fourteen. Almost every kirk, school, bank and railway station you pass has its bronze plaque of names. The city never built one great monument because it built hundreds of small ones.

The Second World War here was lived rather than fought. Edinburgh was bombed only lightly, nothing like Clydebank's ordeal in the west, but the routines were the same as London's: the blackout, gas masks carried to school, Anderson shelters dug into back gardens, and thousands of children evacuated to the Borders and the Highlands with labels on their coats. Rationing bit deep and lasted absurdly long. Sweets stayed on the ration until nineteen fifty-three, eight years after the war ended, which means every Scot of a certain age remembers exactly where they were the week sweets came off.

And then there is the story Scotland itself could barely speak of for fifty years. In the first hours of New Year's Day, nineteen nineteen, weeks after the Armistice, the naval yacht Iolaire was carrying sailors home to the Isle of Lewis, men who had survived the whole war and were coming back for the first peacetime New Year. In darkness and heavy seas she struck rocks called the Beasts of Holm at the mouth of Stornoway harbour, so close to home that men drowned within sight of the harbour lights, some within sight of their own villages. Two hundred and one of the two hundred and eighty aboard died. One man, John Finlay Macleod of Ness, swam ashore with a line, and along the rope he held taut in the surf, dozens of men hauled themselves to safety. Nearly every family on Lewis lost someone or knew someone lost, after four years in which the island had already given more than most. The grief was so total that survivors and families simply stopped speaking of it, and the Iolaire went almost unmentioned for two generations. Only around the centenary in twenty nineteen did Scotland fully take the story back into memory. It may be the hardest single blow any British community took from the Great War, and it fell after the guns had stopped.
