# The Most Radical Idea in the Building
Stand in the entrance hall for a second and consider how strange this place is, historically speaking. For almost all of human history, if you owned wonderful things, art, antiquities, curiosities from far away, you kept them for yourself and your friends. Great collections were private, princely, locked inside palaces, and you saw them only if you were well born or well connected.

The British Museum broke that. It began with a physician named Hans Sloane, a compulsive collector who by the time he died in 1753 had amassed some seventy one thousand objects, along with fifty thousand books. Rather than let it all scatter, he offered the whole hoard to the nation on one condition, that the government keep it together and open it, free, to the public. Parliament agreed, and in 1759 the doors opened to, in the charter's lovely phrase, all studious and curious persons. Free, and for everyone. In the seventeen fifties that was close to a revolutionary act.

It fit the mood of the age, the movement we call the Enlightenment, the eighteenth century's great confidence that the world could be understood if you observed it, collected it, sorted it, and compared it. The dream was that knowledge should be gathered in one place and shared, not hoarded by the powerful. The National Gallery, opening its own doors a few decades later, carried the same principle into painting, and both museums remain free today, which for many visitors from abroad is the genuine surprise, no ticket, no gate, just walk in.

It is worth savoring, because we tend to take it for granted. The idea that ordinary people, children, foreigners, anyone at all, have a right to stand face to face with the Rosetta Stone or a Leonardo, and that a country holds these things in trust for the public rather than for a king, is only about two hundred and seventy years old. You are about to exercise a right that most humans who ever lived never had.
