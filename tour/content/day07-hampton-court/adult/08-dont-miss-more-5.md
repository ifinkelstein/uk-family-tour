# The Georgians: Last Family in the Palace
The Georgian rooms tend to get walked through at pace, which is a pity, because they hold the palace's final act as a royal home, and it is a genuine family drama. The family in question arrived from Hanover in 1714, when George the First, a fifty-four-year-old German prince with limited English and no appetite for display, inherited the throne. He used Hampton Court cautiously, but his son and daughter-in-law used it in style, and the summers of George the Second and Queen Caroline in the seventeen thirties were the last real seasons of court life here, the chocolate kitchen busy at breakfast, the public dinners staged for spectators, because Georgian monarchs, like Tudor ones, still performed their meals. Caroline of Ansbach is the figure worth meeting. Clever, formidable, and genuinely intellectual, she was the sharpest political mind in the family, managing her irritable husband so effectively that wits said England was really governed by the queen. The rooms themselves show the era's taste, and the Cumberland Suite, fitted out in the seventeen thirties by William Kent for the king's younger son, the Duke of Cumberland, is among the finest early Georgian interiors anywhere, cool, architectural, and a world away from Tudor gilding. But the Georgian story here is really about the family poison, the hereditary loathing between Hanoverian kings and their eldest sons. George the Second and Caroline despised Frederick, Prince of Wales, and in the summer of 1737 that hatred produced one of the strangest episodes in royal history, at this palace. Frederick's wife Augusta went into labor here in late July. Determined that his parents should not preside over the birth, as custom demanded to guarantee a royal baby was genuine, Frederick bundled his laboring wife out of Hampton Court in the middle of the night and drove her a jolting hour and a half to St James's Palace, where nothing was ready, no bed made and no linen to be found, and the princess was delivered of a daughter upon a tablecloth. The king and queen woke at Hampton Court to find the birth had happened behind their backs, and the breach became total, Frederick banished from the royal residences. Within months Caroline was dead, in November 1737, and George the Second, grieving and aging, held court at Hampton Court no more. No reigning monarch has truly lived here since. So the Georgian rooms are not an appendix to the palace, they are its ending, the moment the great machine stopped, mid-breakfast as it were, leaving the stage set you now walk through.
