# Pub Sign Detectives
Time to learn a detective game you can play on any British street. It's called reading the pubs.

First, the word itself. Pub is short for public house, and it means exactly what it says. Long ago, a pub really was somebody's house, where the family opened their front room to visitors, cooked food, and served drinks, while they lived upstairs. That's why a proper pub still feels like a cosy living room, with a fireplace, squashy seats, and very often a dog snoozing on the floor. And here's something visitors never expect. British pubs are for families too, especially at lunchtime. Kids come in with their parents for fish and chips or a big Sunday roast, and order lemonade or ginger beer, which is a fizzy, spicy, completely kid-friendly drink that tingles in your nose. A pub at lunchtime is one of the cosiest places in Britain to be hungry.

Now, the signs. Look up outside any pub and you'll see a painted picture hanging over the street. A red lion. A white horse. A king's head. A coach and horses. Those pictures are there because of a rule from more than six hundred years ago that said alehouses must hang out a sign, and in those days most people couldn't read. Not the customers, not even some of the owners. So instead of words, pubs used pictures, and everyone knew them by sight. Meet you at the sign of the lion. The pictures are still up there, all over Britain, a giant outdoor puzzle museum that most people walk right past. Your detective job this week is to spot as many as you can. Score double for any animal wearing a crown.

And now, the best pub story I know in Edinburgh. In a few days you're climbing Arthur's Seat, the sleeping volcano. On the far side of it sits a tiny old village called Duddingston, and in that village is a pub called the Sheep Heid Inn. Yes, that means sheep's head, and there's a reason. Long ago the sheep that grazed around Arthur's Seat ended up in Duddingston, and the villagers got famous for cooking, well, every single part. The story goes there's been an inn here since 1360, more than six hundred and sixty years, which would make it one of the oldest pubs in all of Scotland. The story also goes that a Scottish king liked stopping here so much that he gave the innkeeper a fancy little box made from a ram's head. Nobody can prove those stories, that's why they're stories, but here is the part that's absolutely real, and it's the best part. Out the back is a skittle alley, a wooden bowling lane from Victorian times, said to be the oldest one left in Scotland. Skittles is bowling's great-grandparent. You roll a heavy ball down old wooden boards and try to knock over nine wooden pins, and the crash echoes off the walls exactly like it did when your great-great-great-grandparents were kids. Families can book a lane and play while their lunch is cooking.

So this is the plan. Climb the volcano first. Then walk down the far side to the little village, find the sign with the sheep's head on it, and go knock some pins over in a bowling alley older than your whole family tree.
