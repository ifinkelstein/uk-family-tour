# London at War
When you climb back into the daylight, you'll re-emerge into a city that still carries the scars and the memory of the most testing years in its history, though you have to know where to look. The Cabinet War Rooms make no sense without the London above them, so let me set the scene the staff down here were fighting to protect. From September 1940 to May 1941, London endured the Blitz, a sustained German bombing campaign intended to shatter British morale and force a surrender. For fifty-seven consecutive nights at its outset the bombers came, and over the whole period tens of thousands of Londoners were killed and vast swathes of the city reduced to rubble.

Life adapted around the danger. Every night the capital vanished into the blackout, a total ban on visible light so that enemy aircraft could not navigate by the glow of the streets. Windows were curtained, car headlamps masked, and Londoners groped through pitch-dark roads. When the sirens sounded, families crowded into Anderson shelters in their gardens, into public shelters, and in their thousands down into the Underground stations, sleeping on the platforms as the ground trembled above. Earlier still, in 1939, the government had evacuated well over a million children out of the cities to the countryside, small figures with labels tied to their coats and a single case in hand, separated from their parents for safety.

The strategic stakes could hardly have been higher. Through the summer of 1940, after the fall of France, Britain stood essentially alone against a Nazi-dominated continent, and a German invasion felt not merely possible but imminent. That everything hung by a thread is the emotional key to this bunker. The people down here were not administering a war they expected to win. They were improvising the survival of a nation that might, within weeks, cease to exist. That is what you should carry back up the steps with you.
