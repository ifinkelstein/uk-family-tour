# The Bullet Train and a Queen Under the Floor
Right now you're sitting on one of the fastest trains in Britain, and here's a secret about it. This train was probably built here in the North of England, in a factory in County Durham that you'll roll right past later today. The design came from Japan, and the name of the fastest of these trains is Azuma, which is a Japanese word that means "east," because you're riding the East Coast line. So you're on a Japanese-named, English-built machine that can cruise at around one hundred twenty-five miles an hour, almost as fast as that record-breaking steam engine from ninety years ago, except yours is smooth and quiet and doesn't need a single lump of coal.

Think back to the platform you left from. King's Cross station has a very famous secret wall, halfway between platforms nine and ten, where a luggage trolley sticks out of the bricks and a sign reads Platform Nine and Three-Quarters. That's the magic platform from Harry Potter, where the train to Hogwarts leaves. Loads of people line up to have their photo taken pretending to push the trolley through the wall. Your train left from a perfectly normal platform nearby, no wizards required.

Now here's the wow for this chapter. A while into the journey you pass the city of Peterborough and its enormous cathedral. Under the stone floor of that cathedral lies a real queen of England, Catherine of Aragon, the first wife of King Henry the Eighth, the king with six wives. And for a few years, another queen was buried there too, Mary Queen of Scots, before her son the king had her moved to London. So without even slowing down, your speeding train just zoomed past the grave of a queen.

One more thing to watch for early in the trip. Soon after King's Cross, the train dived into a dark tunnel, then popped back out into the daylight. If you were watching the left side, you might have spotted a huge palace on a hilltop with a giant glass front. That's Alexandra Palace, nicknamed Ally Pally, where the very first proper television pictures in the world were beamed out, back in 1936. Imagine that, the first ever telly show in history, sent out from a palace on a hill.

Here's a number to chew on while the fields whizz past. Your train is covering more than fifty meters every single second, about half the length of a football pitch. Blink, and a whole field has vanished behind you. Keep your eyes on the window. The best sights are still coming.
