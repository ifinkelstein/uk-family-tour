# The City That Burned and Came Back
Every great city has a fire in its story, but few were as thoroughly remade by one as London. It started just before dawn on Sunday, the second of September, 1666, in a baker's shop on Pudding Lane. A small fire, the kind that happened all the time in a city built of timber and pitch and packed tight. The Lord Mayor was woken, looked at it, sniffed that a woman could put it out, and went back to bed. He was catastrophically wrong.

A dry summer, a strong east wind, and streets so narrow the upper floors nearly touched turned it into an inferno that ran for four days, the second through the sixth of September. When it finally died, it had destroyed something like thirteen thousand two hundred houses, eighty-seven parish churches, and the old St Paul's Cathedral, whose very stones were said to have exploded in the heat. Four fifths of the walled city, gone. Extraordinarily, the recorded death toll was tiny, only a handful of names, though the true number was probably higher, since the poor were rarely counted.

Here is the part that matters for your walk. London had a chance to reinvent itself, and mostly chose not to. Grand plans arrived within days for a rational city of wide avenues and elegant squares. But people owned their little plots, they wanted their shops back now, and so the medieval street pattern was largely rebuilt exactly where it had always been, only now in brick instead of wood. That is why the City today is a glorious tangle of crooked lanes with names like Bread Street and Poultry and Cheapside, Roman and medieval geometry hiding under modern towers.

What did change was the skyline. Christopher Wren, an astronomer turned architect, rebuilt more than fifty churches and crowned them all with the new St Paul's, which you are about to meet up close. Look for the tall stone column called the Monument, too. Wren built it to mark exactly where the fire began, and it stands two hundred and two feet from the spot, which is also, to the inch, exactly how tall it is.
