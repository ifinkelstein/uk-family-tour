# Grave-Hunting for Voldemort

Ready for the creepiest treasure hunt in Edinburgh? Behind a church called Greyfriars Kirk there's an old, old graveyard, full of mossy stones and leaning tombs, some of them hundreds of years old. And somewhere in there, waiting for you to find it, is the grave of Lord Voldemort.

Well, sort of. Head toward the back of the graveyard and hunt for a tall stone against the wall with the name Thomas Riddell on it. Riddell with two Ls, so almost Tom Riddle, the boy who grew up to become You Know Who. The real Thomas Riddell was no dark wizard. He was a gentleman who died in eighteen oh six, aged seventy-two. The same stone remembers his son, another Thomas Riddell, a young army captain who died far away on the island of Trinidad when he was only twenty-six. Fans from all over the world come to find this stone, and you'll often see a little path worn in the grass leading right to it.

And the hunt isn't over. This graveyard also holds William McGonagall, who is famous for being possibly the worst poet who ever lived. His poems were so bad that crowds laughed and threw things at him while he read. Sound familiar? Professor McGonagall, the strictest, cleverest teacher at Hogwarts, shares his name, and Rowling once wrote that it tickled her to borrow the name of such a terrible poet for such a brilliant witch. There's even a stone for an Elizabeth Moodie, and fans like to whisper about Mad-Eye Moody when they find it.

But now, the twist. Did Rowling actually creep around this graveyard collecting names? She says no. A few years ago she explained online that one of her own kids went on a Harry Potter walking tour of Edinburgh, just for a laugh, and came home full of amazing facts that were completely new to her. Imagine that. Your own mum wrote the books, and the tour guide knows secrets about them that she doesn't. She did once say that she collected unusual names from all sorts of places, including gravestones. So maybe a name like Riddell floated into her head somewhere and stuck. Or maybe it's all coincidence. Nobody knows for sure, not even her, and that's the honest truth.

One more thing before you leave. Look over the graveyard wall and you'll see a grand grey building with pointy towers, like a small castle. That's George Heriot's School, built in the sixteen hundreds with money left by the king's own jeweller, so that orphan boys could go to school for free. Its students are sorted into four houses. Four houses, a castle school, right next to Voldemort's grave. People love to say it's the real Hogwarts. Rowling has never said so. But go on, look at it and tell me you can't picture the sorting hat in there.
