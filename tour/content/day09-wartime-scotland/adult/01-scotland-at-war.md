# Scotland at War
Here is a puzzle worth carrying through your Edinburgh week. Scotland sits about as far from the trenches of Flanders and the beaches of Normandy as anywhere in Britain. No battle of either world war was fought on Scottish soil. And yet, twice in thirty years, this quiet northern country found itself at the very center of the fight.

The reason is on the map. Scotland faces Germany across the North Sea, and its coast is slit with deep, sheltered firths, natural harbors big enough to hide a navy. So the Royal Navy came north. The Grand Fleet, the most powerful force afloat, spent the First World War in Scottish anchorages, at Scapa Flow in Orkney and here in the Firth of Forth, watching the sea lanes to Germany. Where the fleet went, the war followed. The first German air raid on Britain in the Second World War happened over the Forth, within sight of Edinburgh's rooftops. The entire German High Seas Fleet steamed into this same firth to surrender in nineteen eighteen. Radar, the invention that saved Britain in nineteen forty, was fathered by a Scot. Commandos and secret agents learned their trade in the Highland glens, and the price of it all is written in a shrine at the top of Edinburgh Castle that holds the names of the Scottish dead.

The traces are all around you this week. On day ten your boat crosses the exact water where Spitfires fought the first air battle and where the German fleet gave itself up. From Arthur's Seat you can see the whole theatre laid out below. Even the train south passes concrete defences still crouched on the beaches. This is the story of how a small country on the edge of Europe became the anchor of two world wars.
