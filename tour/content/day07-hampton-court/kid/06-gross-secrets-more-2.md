# Fleas, Baths, and Keeping Clean the Tudor Way
Here is a question that surprises people. Did Henry the Eighth take baths? Not really, not the way you do, and not very often. Hardly anybody in Tudor England did. A lot of people genuinely believed that soaking in warm water opened up tiny holes in your skin and let diseases sneak inside, so they were actually a bit scared of bathing. Instead of washing their bodies much, they did something clever. They kept their clothes spotless. Everyone who could afford it wore a layer of clean white linen right next to the skin, a shirt or a smock, and changed it often. The idea was that the fresh linen soaked up the sweat and dirt and, sort of, wiped you clean from the inside out. Clean shirt, grubby body, and you were considered fresh as a daisy.

To smell nice, rich people carried a pomander, a little ball packed with sweet-smelling herbs and spices, and held it up to their nose whenever the world got too whiffy, which, given the toilets you just heard about, was quite a lot of the time.

But here is the twist, because Henry, being Henry, wanted the very best of everything. While most people avoided washing, the king actually had his own private bathroom in one of the palace towers, with water piped right in, water that could be heated over a fire so he could soak whenever he fancied. And that clean water travelled to the palace through lead pipes all the way from springs about three miles away, and for part of the journey it even ran underneath the river. Five hundred years ago, that was jaw-dropping, almost space-age plumbing.

And now the last gross truth. Everybody, king and kitchen boy alike, shared their beds and their clothes with tiny uninvited guests. Fleas and lice hopped and crawled through the grandest palace in England just as happily as anywhere else. There were even servants whose job included picking bugs out of the royal furs and fabrics. So the next time your bedroom feels a bit messy, remember this. You almost certainly do not have your own personal flea-picker, and Henry the Eighth, King of all England, absolutely did.
