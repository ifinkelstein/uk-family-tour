# The Secret Tenant: The Royal School of Needlework
Here is a Hampton Court secret hiding in plain sight. The palace still has tenants, and the most remarkable of them is a working school. Since 1987 the Royal School of Needlework has occupied apartments in the Wren half of the palace, and behind those sash windows, on an ordinary weekday, students and professional embroiderers are bent over frames doing by hand what almost nobody else in the world still does at this level. The school itself is a Victorian foundation, established in 1872 to preserve hand embroidery as a living art and, quite deliberately, to give respectable employment to women who needed it. It has been the needle of the British state ever since. The school has worked coronation robes since the coronation of Edward the Seventh in 1902, and its needlework has appeared at nearly every great royal occasion of the last century. Two commissions give the flavor. In 2011, its embroiderers hand-appliquéd the lace for Catherine Middleton's wedding dress, working under secrecy so tight that most did not know what the pieces were for; the often-told detail is that hands were washed every thirty minutes and needles renewed constantly to keep the ivory silk immaculate. And in 2023 the school managed the embroidery of the Anointing Screen for the coronation of Charles the Third, the screen that shielded the most sacred moment of the ceremony, worked as a great golden tree bearing fifty-six leaves, one for each nation of the Commonwealth. There is a nice historical rhyme in the location. The rooms the school occupies are said to stand over ground that once held Tudor courtier lodgings, so needlework returns the palace to one of its oldest luxuries, because in Henry's day embroidery in gold and silk was among the most prestigious and expensive arts in England, the tapestries downstairs being simply its largest expression. You cannot usually wander in. The school opens its doors for pre-booked tours, exhibitions and classes rather than casual visits, so for most of us it remains a presence rather than a stop. But that presence changes how the East Front feels. Hampton Court is not a preserved shell with a museum inside. It is still, in one corner, a place where royal commissions arrive, where a craft five centuries deep in this building's story is taught to new hands, and where something being stitched this afternoon may one day appear at a coronation. As you walk the gardens, glance up at the windows. The palace is still working.
