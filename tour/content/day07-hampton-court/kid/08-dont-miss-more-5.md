# Chocolate for Breakfast, and the Family Who Left
Long after Henry the Eighth, a new royal family moved in, the Georges, who came over from Germany, and they left behind two things worth knowing about, a room full of chocolate and one spectacular family quarrel. First, the chocolate. Hidden near the Georgian rooms is a little kitchen with one job only, making hot chocolate for the king. Chocolate back then was not a bar you unwrap. It was a drink for the super-rich, thick as custard, dark, spicy, sometimes with wine mixed in, and drinking it for breakfast was like announcing, I am fabulously wealthy, good morning. The king had his own personal chocolate maker, a man named Thomas Tosier, and being the royal chocolate man made him quietly famous. Now the best part. This little kitchen was lost. After the kings stopped coming, everyone simply forgot which room it was, for nearly three hundred years. Then a curator studying an old palace list worked out exactly which door to open, and there it all was, the stove, the shelves, the equipment, like a time capsule. It opened to visitors in twenty fourteen, and it is the only royal chocolate kitchen left in the whole country. Go and sniff it out, and if you are lucky, someone will be making chocolate the old way. Second, the quarrel. King George the Second and Queen Caroline lived here in the seventeen thirties, and they could not stand their own grown-up son, Prince Frederick, and he could not stand them. It sounds like a silly family squabble until you hear what happened one July night in seventeen thirty-seven. Frederick's wife was about to have a baby, here at Hampton Court. By the rules, the king and queen had to be nearby when a royal baby was born, to prove no one swapped in a fake baby. Frederick hated his parents so much that he refused to let that happen. So in the middle of the night, he smuggled his poor wife, already in labour, out of the palace and into a carriage, and they rattled and bounced about twenty-one kilometres to another palace in London, where nothing was ready, and no proper sheets could even be found, so the baby girl was born on a tablecloth. The king and queen woke up here to discover the baby had been born behind their backs, and they were volcanically angry. Just months later Queen Caroline died, and the sad old king basically stopped coming. The royals never really lived at Hampton Court again. The palace froze, like a clock that stopped ticking, and that is exactly why you can explore it today.
