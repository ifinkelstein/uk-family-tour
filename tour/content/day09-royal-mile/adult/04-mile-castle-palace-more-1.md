# Tell Me More: The Canongate and the World's End

Two thirds of the way down the Mile, pause at the corner of St Mary's Street, where a pub called The World's End stands, and look for the lines of brass setts crossing the road. They mark the footprint of a vanished building, the Netherbow Port, the great gatehouse that once stood here as the eastern gate of Edinburgh. Nether meant lower or furthest, port meant gate, and this was the main door in the city wall, complete with a portcullis, a bell, and, in the grimmer centuries, the severed heads of executed traitors spiked above it as a welcome. It was pulled down in seventeen sixty-four, judged a nuisance to traffic, but its outline is kept in the roadway still.

The pub's name is the good bit. For the poorest citizens of old Edinburgh, this gate genuinely was the end of the world. Passing out through it cost a toll, and coming back in cost another, and if you hadn't the coin you didn't leave, so a great many people lived their whole lives inside the walls and never once stepped beyond this point. The close beside the pub, and the pub itself, took the name from that hard little fact. Beyond the gate, quite literally, lay the unknown.

And beyond it lay the Canongate, which was not Edinburgh at all. It was founded as a separate burgh in the eleven forties, by a charter of King David the First, to serve the great abbey of Holyrood he had planted at the foot of the hill. Canongate means the gait, or the way, of the canons, the walk the abbey's clergy took up towards the city. For seven centuries it kept its own council and its own courts, a small independent town clinging to Edinburgh's skirts, until it was finally absorbed in eighteen fifty-six.

Walk a little further and step into the Canongate Kirk, the pretty seventeenth-century church with the curved gable, still the parish church of the palace and of the royal household in Scotland. In its quiet kirkyard lie some remarkable neighbours. The economist Adam Smith, whose grave you can find near the wall, and the young poet Robert Fergusson, dead at twenty-four, whose headstone was paid for by an admirer named Robert Burns. There's a statue of Fergusson striding in through the gate. That Burns should have honoured a poet he never met tells you how this city holds its writers, which is a thread worth following further down the Mile.
