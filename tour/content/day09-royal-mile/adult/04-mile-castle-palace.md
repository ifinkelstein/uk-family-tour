# The Mile Itself: Crown to Cross

The Royal Mile isn't really one street, and it wasn't really one town. Read from the top down, it's a sequence, and each stretch carries its own name and its own past. Up at the castle end you have Castlehill, then the Lawnmarket, whose name has nothing to do with lawns and everything to do with the cloth and linen once sold there. Then the High Street, the broad heart of the old city, running down past St Giles and the site of the Tolbooth. And then, crossing an invisible line about two thirds of the way down, you leave Edinburgh altogether and enter the Canongate, which for most of its history was a separate burgh with its own council, its own tolbooth and its own jurisdiction, and did not formally become part of Edinburgh until eighteen fifty-six.

There's a neat way to hold the whole thing in your head. At the top sits a crown, and at the bottom a cross. The crown is the castle on its rock, the fortress and the royal stronghold. The cross is Holyrood, the abbey and palace at the foot, since Holyrood means holy rood, an old word for the holy cross. Crown to cross, fortress to sanctuary, and a mile of city strung between them like beads on a thread. You'll be walking it downhill, which is how the water, and rather worse than water, always ran.

Notice, as you descend, that the street keeps changing character. The upper end is tourist-dense and steep, huddled and medieval. Lower down, once you cross into the Canongate, it opens and calms, and the buildings feel more like the townhouses of nobles, because that is exactly what many of them were, aristocrats who wanted to be near the palace and the court but outside the crush and the smell of the walled city proper. Look for their coats of arms and their dates carved above the doors.

And keep the whole trip's thread in mind. You've been collecting royal front doors on this journey, the great residences down south among them. Here is the northern one. This single mile connects two of them, a war-castle and a working palace, and the monarch still stays at the bottom of it for a week each summer. Few streets on Earth carry that much on one downhill walk.
