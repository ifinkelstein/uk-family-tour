# The Living Pint, and the Four Friends Who Saved It
Cask ale is beer that leaves the brewery unfinished on purpose. It is racked into a metal cask with its yeast still in it, neither filtered nor pasteurised, and sent to the pub still working. Down in the cellar it completes a slow second fermentation, building a gentle natural sparkle, nothing like the aggressive fizz of gassed beer. The pub isn't just selling this beer. The pub is raising it.

That job belongs to the cellarman, and it is a real craft. The cask is set on its side on a rack and left to settle. A porous wooden peg called a spile is knocked into it to let the fermentation breathe, then swapped for a hard one when the condition is right. The cellar is kept at cellar temperature, around eleven to thirteen degrees Celsius, the low fifties Fahrenheit. Cool, in other words. The old joke that the British drink warm beer is wrong. They drink cool beer, served at the temperature where its flavour actually opens up, rather than ice cold, which is the temperature at which you can't taste anything.

Once the cask is tapped, the clock runs. Air comes in as beer goes out, and in a few days the beer is past its best. Cask ale must be sold fresh and looked after daily, like bread. In simple country pubs it is poured by gravity, straight from a tap in the cask. Most pubs use the beer engine instead, the tall hand pump on the bar. Each long pull of that lever draws a quarter pint up from the cellar by suction. The engineer Joseph Bramah patented the classic design in 1797, and it has barely needed to change since. No gas pressure touches the beer at any point. When you see someone pulling a pint in three slow strokes, you are watching a machine from the age of Napoleon doing exactly what it was built for.

All of this nearly disappeared. After the Second World War, Britain's brewers consolidated into a handful of giants, and the giants preferred keg beer, filtered, pasteurised, pushed to the tap with carbon dioxide. Dead beer keeps for months and needs no craft in the cellar, so pub by pub, the handles came off the bars.

Then, in March 1971, four young men from the north-west of England, Michael Hardman, Graham Lees, Jim Makin and Bill Mellor, went on holiday to the west of Ireland. In Kruger's bar in the village of Dunquin, in County Kerry, grumbling over their pints about how dismal British beer had become, they decided, half as a joke, to found a protest movement. They called it the Campaign for the Revitalisation of Ale, soon renamed the Campaign for Real Ale, CAMRA for short. It struck a nerve nobody knew was there. Within a few years it had tens of thousands of members, a bestselling Good Beer Guide, and festivals up and down the country, and the big brewers were scrambling to put the hand pumps back. It is regularly called the most successful consumer campaign in Europe, and it is why the living pint still exists for you to order tonight. Four friends in a bar, annoyed enough to do something. Worth remembering.
