# The Market Cross and the Heart of the Town
Carry on down and the street opens out into Broad Street, and this was the beating heart of old Stirling, the marketplace. Close your eyes for a moment and fill it in: stalls and awnings, the shouts of traders, the smell of fish and tallow and animal dung, cloth merchants and shoemakers and fleshers, that is butchers, all crowded along one steep, cobbled street.

At its centre stands the Mercat Cross, the market cross. Every Scottish burgh had one, and it was far more than decoration. This was the town's official centre, the place where markets were licensed, where royal proclamations were read out to the crowd, where deals were struck, and where people gathered, sometimes to cheer and sometimes to riot. On top of the cross sits a stone unicorn, Scotland's royal beast, and the locals have affectionately called it the Puggy for generations. The unicorn is the genuine old survivor, carved back in the fifteen hundreds. The cross beneath it was actually taken down and carted away for about a hundred years before finally being put back where it belonged.

Looming over the square is the Tolbooth. Now, a tolbooth was the Swiss Army knife of a Scottish town. It was the town hall, where the council met. It was the courthouse, where justice was handed out. It was the place where taxes and tolls were collected, which is exactly where the name comes from. And it was the jail, the very same grim lockup that was later condemned as one of the worst in Britain, before the new jail up the hill replaced it.

Justice here was public, and it was meant to shame. A wrongdoer might be locked into the jougs, an iron collar chained to the wall or the cross, and left there for the whole market to jeer at. Others were branded, or put in the stocks, or worse. It is easy to stand in this handsome square and forget that for hundreds of years it was also the place where the town punished its own, out in the open, in front of everyone. Market, courtroom and stage of shame, all in one steep street.
