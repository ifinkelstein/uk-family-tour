# How Far Did the Vikings Really Go?
Let's play a game of just how far. When you think Viking, you probably think of a fierce warrior with an axe. And sometimes, yes. But here is a secret about the word itself. Viking was not the name of a country, or even a kind of person. In their language, going viking meant going off on a raiding trip in a boat. So being a Viking was more like a job you went and did than who you were, and most people back home in Scandinavia were not raiders at all. They were farmers and fishers who stayed put. But the ones who did climb into the ships went to unbelievable places, and it was all thanks to their amazing boats. A Viking longship was long and thin and light, so it could ride across a stormy ocean and then slide right up onto a beach, or up a shallow river, where no other ship could follow. With boats like that, where did they go? Hold on tight. They sailed west to a cold island called Iceland, then to an even colder one they cheekily named Greenland, and then, around a thousand years ago, they crossed the whole Atlantic Ocean and stepped onto North America. That is Canada. Five hundred years before the explorer Columbus, whom your schoolbooks might call the man who found America, the Vikings had already been there. They went east too, rowing rivers deep into the land we now call Russia. And a few of them travelled so unbelievably far south that they ended up standing guard for an emperor in a great golden city, the place we now call Istanbul. Now think about that for a second. The very same people. Some robbing a church in England, some guarding an emperor thousands of miles away, some chopping wood in Canada. Not bad at all for a bunch of farmers with really, really good boats.
