# The First City of Literature

In two thousand and four, Edinburgh was named the first UNESCO City of Literature in the world. Not the first in Britain, the first anywhere, the founding member of a club that now stretches from Dublin to Melbourne to Baghdad. The idea itself was hatched here, and then handed to the world. Stop and think how strange that is. Scotland is a small country, barely five million people, roughly the population of a mid-sized city, and yet an astonishing share of the books the whole world grew up on were dreamed up within a short walk of where you are standing.

Consider the roll call. The man who invented the historical novel. The man who wrote Treasure Island and gave every later pirate his parrot and his map. The city where detective fiction was essentially born, in Sherlock Holmes. The cafes where a struggling single mother wrote a boy wizard. Add Burns, Scotland's national poet, whose songs you have sung without knowing it, and Muriel Spark, and a whole shelf of modern crime, and you have a few square miles that have printed more of what the world reads than almost anywhere on earth.

Why here? Part of the answer is machinery. By the seventeen hundreds Edinburgh was one of Europe's great centres of printing and ideas, the engine room of the Scottish Enlightenment. The very first Encyclopaedia Britannica was published right here, in seventeen sixty-eight. Presses, publishers, booksellers and arguing philosophers were packed into these closes cheek by jowl, and books poured out of them.

And part of the answer is the city itself. Edinburgh is a place of two faces. The cramped, dark, medieval Old Town of winding closes and towering tenements, and across the valley the calm, orderly, elegant Georgian New Town. A city literally split in two, respectable and rational on one side, tangled and shadowy on the other. That double nature seeped into its writers, and you will hear it echo again and again in the stories that follow. Halfway down the Royal Mile, tucked into Lady Stair's Close, a small free museum honours three of them. Let us meet them, one by one.
