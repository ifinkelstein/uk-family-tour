# The Book That Came Out of the Conference
It is worth lingering on what actually happened, because the making of the King James Bible is one of the great collaborative feats in the history of the language, and its seed really was planted at Hampton Court in the winter of sixteen oh four. When John Rainolds, the Puritan president of an Oxford college, rose during the conference and moved that there be a fresh translation of the Bible, he did so almost as an aside. The existing English Bibles, he argued, the Bishops' Bible read in churches and the Geneva Bible beloved of Puritan households, were flawed and inconsistent. King James seized on the proposal, though for reasons of his own. He detested the Geneva Bible's marginal notes, some of which hinted that it could be lawful to disobey a wicked ruler, an idea a monarch who believed devoutly in his own divine right could hardly stomach. A new official version, stripped of such notes, suited him perfectly.

The scale of what followed was remarkable. Around fifty of the finest scholars in England, the figure usually given is forty-seven who saw it through to the end, were organized into six companies based at Oxford, Cambridge, and Westminster, each assigned a portion of scripture. They worked from the original Hebrew and Greek, leaned on the earlier English translations rather than starting from a blank page, and cross-checked one another's drafts, reading passages aloud to test how they sounded, because this was a Bible meant above all to be read out in church and heard. The work took roughly seven years. The result appeared in sixteen eleven.

Its influence is almost impossible to overstate. Phrases we now use without a second thought, a labour of love, the salt of the earth, a law unto themselves, the writing on the wall, how the mighty are fallen, a thorn in the flesh, entered everyday English through this translation. For three centuries it was, quite simply, the Bible in the English-speaking world, carried to America and to the far ends of the empire, read by the devout and the doubting alike, quoted by everyone from Lincoln to the poets. And its committees of scholars, laboring in their separate rooms, produced not the flat, cautious prose you might expect from a project commissioned by government order, but some of the most majestic English ever written. The next time you pass the Chapel Royal, remember that the command which set all of that in motion was given only a few steps away, by a Scottish king who was mostly trying, and largely failing, to win an argument about church discipline.
