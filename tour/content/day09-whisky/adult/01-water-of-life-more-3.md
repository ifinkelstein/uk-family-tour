# Smugglers, Excisemen and the World
The written story of Scotch begins with a single line in the royal account books. In fourteen ninety-four, a clerk of King James the Fourth noted a delivery of malt "to Friar John Cor, to make aqua vitae." Eight bolls of it, a great heap of grain, enough by some reckonings for well over a thousand bottles. So the first named whisky-maker in history is a monk, in an abbey in Fife, and by fourteen ninety-four he plainly already knew his business, which means the craft was older still. That is where the paper trail starts. The real story runs back into the mist behind it, and nobody knows who made the first drop.

For a long time whisky was just something Scots made, on farms, in cottages, a handy way to turn a wet barley harvest into something that kept and warmed and could be sold or bartered. Then the government worked out it could be taxed, and everything changed. The duty on spirits climbed, and whisky-making dived underground, quite literally. Thousands of illegal stills were tucked into the glens, in caves and barns and heather, their smoke passed off as morning mist, the whisky carried out under cover of dark. In the early eighteen hundreds the authorities were seizing something like fourteen thousand illegal stills a year, and it was reckoned that more than half the whisky drunk in Scotland had never paid a penny of tax.

Against the smugglers stood the excisemen, the taxmen, known in Scotland as gaugers because they gauged, or measured, the barrels to work out the duty owed. They were the most hated men in the glens. And here is one of the great ironies of Scottish history. The nation's beloved poet, Robert Burns, spent his last years as one of them. Burns, the man who wrote Auld Lang Syne, needed a steady wage to feed his family, and from seventeen eighty-nine until his death in seventeen ninety-six he rode the roads around Dumfries as an exciseman, measuring whisky for the taxman by day and, one suspects, drinking cheerfully with the smugglers by night. He even dashed off a merry poem about the Devil carrying the exciseman away, to everyone's delight.

The turning point came in eighteen twenty-three. A new law, the Excise Act, slashed the duty and made it possible, for the first time, to distil legally without being ruined. One by one the smugglers came in from the cold and took out licences. A farmer named George Smith, at Glenlivet, was among the very first, and so unpopular with his old smuggling neighbours for going straight that he carried a pair of pistols for years. Out of that new legal trade, and above all out of the clever art of blending, Scotch grew from a Highland secret into one of the world's great drinks, shipped from Leith, the port just down the hill from where you stand, to every corner of the Empire and beyond. Edinburgh, with its harbours and its merchants and its blenders, sat right at the centre of it. The water of life had gone off to conquer the world.
