# Robert Louis Stevenson and the City's Double Life

Robert Louis Stevenson gave the world two things that have never left it. One is the pirate. Treasure Island, published in eighteen eighty-three, is the source code for almost every pirate story you have ever seen. The buried gold and the map marked with a cross, the one-legged rogue with a parrot on his shoulder, the black spot, the sea chest, the rum, all of it flows from this one Edinburgh writer's imagination. Before Stevenson, pirates were mostly grubby criminals. After him, they were adventure. He followed it with Kidnapped, a breathless chase across the Scottish Highlands, and cemented himself as one of the great storytellers in the English language.

But his deepest and strangest book grew directly out of the city you are standing in. In eighteen eighty-six Stevenson published Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, the story of a respectable doctor who brews a potion that lets him transform into a monstrous second self, Mr Hyde, so that he can do in the dark what he would never dare do in daylight, and who slowly loses control of which man he is. It has become one of those rare stories whose very title turned into everyday language. When we call someone a Jekyll and Hyde, everyone understands.

Now, where would a boy get such an idea? Look around you. Stevenson was born here in eighteen fifty, and he grew up in a city with two faces. There was the elegant, buttoned-up, respectable New Town, where his own comfortable family lived, and there was the tangle of dark Old Town closes just a short walk away, full of drink and poverty and secrets, where a respectable gentleman might slip off at night to misbehave. Stevenson, a sickly, restless young man, knew both sides intimately. The whole city was a Jekyll and Hyde before he ever wrote the words.

And there was a particular ghost at his shoulder. Edinburgh had a genuine local legend of a double life, a man named Deacon William Brodie. By day Brodie was the very model of respectability, a skilled cabinetmaker, a member of the town council, a pillar of the community. By night he was a burglar, using his good name and his knowledge of people's locks to rob the very houses he was welcomed into. When he was finally caught and hanged in seventeen eighty-eight, the story went that he was executed on a gallows he himself had helped to improve, though historians think that final twist is more legend than fact. Here is the eerie part. The Stevenson family owned a cabinet made by Brodie's own hands, and it stood in young Robert's bedroom. He grew up sleeping beside furniture built by Edinburgh's most famous respectable criminal. Years later, that childhood image of a man with two selves, one shown to the world and one hidden, poured out as Jekyll and Hyde.

Stevenson never really thrived in Scotland's cold, wet air. His weak lungs eventually drove him to the far side of the world, and he died in eighteen ninety-four on a tropical island in Samoa, only forty-four years old, mourned by the islanders who had come to love him. But the two things he gave the world, the pirate and the divided soul, were both born here, in a city of two faces.
