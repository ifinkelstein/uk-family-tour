# Saved by Being Left Behind
Here is the paradox at the heart of York. The thing that makes it so wonderful to visit, its astonishing completeness, is a direct result of its decline. For centuries York was the great city of the North, second only to London. But when the industrial revolution came in the seventeen and eighteen hundreds, the money and the smoke and the sheer explosive growth went elsewhere, to Leeds and Bradford and Manchester, cities built on coal and cloth and iron. York, with no coalfield of its own, was largely passed over. It stayed a market and cathedral town while its neighbors doubled and tripled in size. That was, at the time, a disappointment. In hindsight it was the luckiest thing that ever happened to it.

Because a booming city knocks itself down and rebuilds. A quiet one keeps what it has. The old fabric of York, the walls, the churches, the tangled medieval lanes, simply stayed put because there was no fortune to be made in clearing it away. It very nearly went otherwise. In the year eighteen hundred the city corporation actually applied to Parliament for permission to demolish the walls, calling them crumbling and inconvenient, exactly as London and most other English cities were tearing theirs down. What saved York's walls was an early conservation campaign, led by influential citizens including the painter William Etty, who fought the demolition and won. Because of that fight, York today has more surviving city wall than anywhere else in England, a nearly complete circuit you can walk for free.

So what should you notice today? Notice that the completeness is real. That medieval street is not a film set; those walls are not a replica; the Minster is the actual medieval cathedral, not a Victorian imitation. Notice, too, the compromises that keep a living city alive, like the great railway arches punched through the ancient walls in the eighteen thirties so the trains could reach the center. York is not a museum where nothing may change. It is a place that learned, earlier than most, that the past is worth keeping, and then went on living inside it. That is the rarest thing here, and it is all around you.
