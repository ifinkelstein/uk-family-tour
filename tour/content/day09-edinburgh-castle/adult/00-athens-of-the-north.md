# Welcome to the Athens of the North

Welcome to Edinburgh, the capital of Scotland, and, if this is your first stop north of the border, welcome to Scotland itself. Take a moment, before the castle swallows your whole attention, to meet the city as a whole, because Edinburgh is one of the most theatrical capitals in Europe, and knowing its shape will make everything you see over the next few days click into place.

Start with the ground. Edinburgh is built on the bones of ancient volcanoes. The rock beneath this castle, the green hill of Arthur's Seat away to the east, the crags and ridges all around you, are the worn stumps of eruptions from a time long before there were dinosaurs. The city drapes itself over these humps and hollows, which is why Edinburgh is a place of sudden views, steep stairs, and streets that dive out of sight. No one designed that. The land insisted on it.

Then notice that this is really two cities in one. On the ridge below the castle sits the Old Town, medieval, crowded, and dark, a tangle of tall stone tenements and narrow lanes. Across a valley to the north lies the New Town, an elegant grid of broad streets and handsome townhouses, deliberately laid out in the seventeen sixties as an escape from the crush. Old and new, tangled and orderly, staring at each other across a green ditch. You will feel that split everywhere you go.

And here is the real surprise. This small, cold, northern city, in its day barely bigger than a market town, became during the seventeen hundreds one of the great thinking capitals of the world, so admired for learning that people called it the Athens of the North. Hold that thought. We will come back to it.
