# Past to Future: How Modern Art Starts Before Modern Art
This route is for giving children context without turning the National Gallery into a lecture. The collection does not take you far into the twentieth century, and it is not a Futurist museum. Its strength is different: it lets you see the preconditions for modern art forming inside older pictures. The modern break does not come from nowhere. It grows out of illusion, science, trade, optics, industrial change, new pigments, new leisure, and artists gradually losing faith in single-point certainty.

Begin with Van Eyck's Arnolfini Portrait in Room 52. It is easy to present this as pure old-master realism, but that misses the point. It is not simply accurate; it is constructed. The convex mirror expands the room, places unseen visitors behind us, and turns the viewer into part of the event. The objects perform social work: oranges, textiles, bed, chandelier, dog, shoes, signature, reflected doorway. This is early Netherlandish painting as a machine for attention, status, and presence. Children can find the clues; adults can notice that the painting already knows representation is artificial and persuasive.

Then go to Holbein's Ambassadors in Room 4. The painting is practically a manifesto for Renaissance knowledge: celestial and terrestrial instruments, books, globes, music, diplomacy, religion, and political fracture arranged with terrifying control. Yet the most famous object is the one that refuses normal viewing. The anamorphic skull only resolves from the side. Holbein makes perspective both a triumph and a problem. Stand in the correct place and the hidden truth appears; stand in the ordinary place and knowledge is distorted. That is not remote from modern art. It is already a picture about unstable seeing.

Turner's Fighting Temeraire in Room 40 moves the story into industrial time. The old warship, famous for Trafalgar, is towed toward breaking by a steam tug. The National Gallery notes that Turner imaginatively recreated the scene and heightened its symbolism: the age of sail giving way to the age of steam. Children see a ghost ship and a smoky little engine. Adults can read the deeper argument: modernity is ugly, useful, unstoppable, and emotionally complicated. Turner is not a Futurist, but he is one of the painters who makes speed, atmosphere, machinery, and dissolution available to later art.

From there, Cezanne's Bathers in Room 45 is the bridge from past subject to future structure. The subject reaches back to classical and Renaissance bathers, but the painting does not behave like a window onto a stable world. Bodies become architectural. Space is flattened and compressed. Blue outlines bind figures to trees, sky, and ground. The National Gallery describes the work as hugely influential on early twentieth-century art, especially Cubism, and that is the key for families: Cezanne teaches later artists that a painting can be built, not merely copied.

Now reconnect Monet and Seurat. Monet makes perception unstable through light and time: the world changes as soon as you try to catch it. Seurat makes perception systematic: colour can be divided, placed, and recombined by the eye. Cezanne makes perception structural: objects can be rebuilt as relations of plane, colour, and mass. These are different answers to the same late nineteenth-century problem. Once painting no longer has to pretend to be a transparent window, it can show how seeing works.

That prepares the one-step-away Futurist context. Futurism begins after the National Gallery's usual chronological comfort zone, in 1909, with artists and writers who celebrated speed, machines, crowds, industry, noise, and violent rupture with the past. Their politics and rhetoric were often ugly; do not present them as simple heroes. Present them as an extreme symptom of the same modern pressure already visible here. Turner gives them steam and motion. Monet gives them flicker. Seurat and divisionism give them broken colour. Cezanne gives them constructed form. Cubism takes Cezanne apart; Futurism tries to set the fragments in motion.

For the family, keep the route practical. Ask one question in each room. Van Eyck: how can a mirror change the story? Holbein: where must your body stand for the truth to appear? Turner: what does progress destroy while it moves forward? Cezanne: when does a body become a structure? Monet and Seurat: when does paint become light? Futurism, even if it is not hanging here, becomes the final thought experiment: what would a painting look like if it tried to show the speed of the modern world all at once?
