# The Man Who Was Often Wrong
Hero-worship is the enemy of understanding, and Churchill deserves better than a halo, so let us do him the honour of taking his failures seriously. The stubbornness that saved Britain in 1940 was the same trait that, in other decades, marched him into disaster after disaster. The man was not a genius who occasionally erred. He was a gambler whose whole career was a sequence of enormous bets, and a great many of them lost.

Consider Gallipoli. In the First World War, as First Lord of the Admiralty, Churchill championed a bold plan to force the Dardanelles straits, knock the Ottoman Empire out of the war, and open a route to Russia. On paper it was imaginative. In execution it was a catastrophe, a bungled naval assault followed by a doomed land campaign on the Gallipoli peninsula that bogged down into slaughter and dysentery and cost something on the order of a quarter of a million Allied casualties for no gain whatsoever. Churchill was blamed, stripped of his office, and cast into disgrace. To his enormous credit, he did not hide. He went off to command an infantry battalion in the trenches of the Western Front. But Gallipoli haunted him for the rest of his life, and it should temper any easy picture of an infallible war leader.

Then there is 1925, and the Gold Standard. As Chancellor of the Exchequer, against the warnings of some sharp minds, Churchill returned Britain to the gold standard at the pre-war exchange rate, a decision that overvalued the pound, throttled exports, deepened unemployment and helped provoke the industrial strife that led to the General Strike of 1926. The economist John Maynard Keynes was so appalled that he wrote a pamphlet with the withering title, The Economic Consequences of Mister Churchill. Churchill himself later called it the worst mistake of his life.

And then there are the parts that are hardest for admirers to hear, because they are not blunders of tactics but failures of imagination and humanity. Churchill was a Victorian imperialist to his marrow, and he never really stopped being one. He fiercely opposed self-government for India, and he spoke of Gandhi, a man of extraordinary moral force, with contempt, sneering at the sight of a half-naked fakir striding up the steps of the viceregal palace. During the Bengal famine of 1943, when millions died in a British-ruled province, his government's priorities and his own dismissive attitude toward Indian suffering remain the subject of serious and painful historical debate, and no honest account can wave it away.

Hold all of this alongside the triumph, because the two are inseparable. The very qualities that made Churchill dangerous in ordinary times, the certainty, the romanticism, the refusal to bend to expert consensus or to the mood of the room, were precisely the qualities that made him irreplaceable in the one extraordinary moment when Britain needed a man who simply would not entertain the thought of surrender. History handed him a crisis that finally fit his temperament. It is a sobering thought, standing in the bunker from which he ran that finest hour, that the same man had already been, by any fair reckoning, spectacularly wrong about a great deal. Greatness and grave error are not opposites. In him they were the same stubborn engine, pointed, at last, in the right direction.
