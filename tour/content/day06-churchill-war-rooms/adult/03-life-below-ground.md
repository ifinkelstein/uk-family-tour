# Life Below Ground
Strip away the legend and picture the reality of daily existence in this place, because it was far from glamorous. There were no windows and no natural light, only the flat glare of electric bulbs burning around the clock, so that staff lost all sense of whether it was noon or midnight. The air was thick with dust, cigarette smoke and Churchill's cigars, poorly ventilated, and often stiflingly hot. Rats were a genuine problem. This was not the gleaming command centre of a film. It was a cramped, stuffy basement in which people did extraordinary work in thoroughly miserable conditions.

And there were many of them. Beyond Churchill and the chiefs of staff, the bunker ran on a small army of clerks, typists, telephonists, cipher officers, cooks, orderlies and Royal Marines, a great number of them young women in their teens and twenties. During the worst periods some of them lived down here for weeks, sleeping in a sub-basement of bunks so airless and grim that it was nicknamed the Dock, a place of snoring, chamber pots and no privacy whatsoever. They were sworn to absolute secrecy and could tell no one, not even their families, what they did all day.

The lack of sunlight created real medical concern, so the complex was fitted with sun lamps to stave off vitamin D deficiency, with the surreal result that some staff developed sunburn while working underground in wartime London. Small human touches abound. A notice board that acted as a weather indicator would read windy, not to describe a breeze but as code for an air raid in progress overhead, the one meteorological fact that actually mattered to people who could not see the sky.

As you move through the corridors, resist the urge to see only Churchill. Look for the evidence of the ordinary people who kept this machine running, the typists' keys, the switchboard, the bunks. Their discipline and endurance, sustained in secret and unrewarded by fame, are the real texture of this place.
