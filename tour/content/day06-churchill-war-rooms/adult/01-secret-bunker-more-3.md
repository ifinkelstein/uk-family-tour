# The Keeper of the Keys
Every great secret needs a custodian, and the secret of this bunker rested, to a remarkable degree, on the shoulders of one modest, methodical man named George Rance. He is not a household name. He commanded no armies and made no speeches. But the physical existence of this place, fitted out and provisioned without the wrong people ever getting wind of it, was very largely his doing.

Rance was a civil servant, an ex-soldier of the Rifle Brigade, roughly the same age as Churchill, and before the war his duties ran to the wonderfully unglamorous business of the pay sheets for government charladies. When the decision came to convert this reinforced basement into an emergency seat of government, he was quietly handed the job of equipping it. And here lay the difficulty that makes his achievement so neat. You cannot furnish a top-secret war headquarters through the front door. Order a hundred beds, a switchboard, filing cabinets and crates of tinned rations to a Whitehall office, and questions get asked, and questions in wartime are dangerous.

So Rance improvised a beautifully simple deception. He attached his own requests to legitimate ministry orders, quietly inflating them, and had the surplus delivered to an address that meant nothing to anyone, care of Mister Rance. That innocuous little phrase became, in effect, the codeword for the whole operation. Goods arrived addressed to him, and vanished discreetly into the rooms beneath Whitehall, and the paper trail led nowhere in particular. It was hidden in plain sight through sheer administrative dullness, which, as deceptions go, is the most reliable kind.

And crucially, Rance held the keys. Not a set of the keys, the keys, the only ones, for much of the operation. The entire nerve centre of the British war effort could be entered, in the practical daily sense, only through one man. He ran the place throughout the war, sweating the small stuff so that the great decisions could be taken in safety, and he was eventually appointed a Member of the Order of the British Empire for his pains.

It is worth pausing on that as you walk. We are trained to remember wars through their leaders and their set-piece battles, but they are also won, or lost, in the invisible plumbing, the logistics, the discretion, the man who makes sure the beds arrive and the secret holds. Churchill supplied the words and the will. George Rance supplied the address that led nowhere, and the keys that stayed in his pocket. Both, in their way, were indispensable.
