# Kings, Queens, and the Shape of the Ground
Here is a thing that often surprises visitors from newer countries: in Britain, the monarchy is not just ceremony and gossip and people in splendid hats. For most of the last thousand years, kings and queens were the government, and their appetites, for war, for money, for God, and yes, for hunting, physically reshaped the country. You are standing in the proof. This park exists because in sixteen thirty seven King Charles the First wanted deer to chase near London, so he threw a wall around nearly a thousand hectares of other people's farmland and declared it his. The land still holds the shape of one man's hobby, almost four hundred years later.

That is the thread we will pull all the way through this trip. Notice how often the answer to why is this here turns out to be a king or a queen. The great fortress you will visit in the city, the Tower of London, was begun by a French speaking conqueror to overawe a town he had just seized. The sprawling palace out at Hampton Court was a cardinal's dream house until Henry the Eighth simply took it, the way powerful people took things. And when we travel north into Scotland, to Edinburgh and to Stirling, we walk into a whole separate royal saga, a rival kingdom of Scottish kings and queens who spent centuries fighting, marrying, and finally merging with the English crown.

So think of the monarchy as the spine of nearly everything you are about to see. Not because kings and queens were admirable, plenty of them were monstrous, and their power was often stolen and cruel, but because for a thousand years they held the money and the armies, and money and armies are what build walls and castles and cathedrals and, as it turns out, deer parks. Keep score as we go. By the time we reach the last castle on this trip, you will have met a good stretch of the family, and you will never look at a wall in England quite the same way again.
