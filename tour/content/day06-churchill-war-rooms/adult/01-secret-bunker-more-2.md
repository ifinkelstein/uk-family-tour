# Frozen and Rediscovered
The most poignant thing about the Cabinet War Rooms is not what happened here during the war but what happened after it. In August 1945, the staff turned off the Map Room lights, locked the doors, and left, and for the most part the rooms were never cleared out. Sugar sat in a drawer. Pins stayed in the maps. A wall calendar kept its final wartime date. The place became an accidental time capsule, sealed by a combination of official secrecy and simple indifference.

For decades a handful of people knew it was down here and quietly kept an eye on it, giving occasional private tours to the well-connected, but the public was locked out. Pressure to preserve it grew through the 1970s, and in 1984 the Imperial War Museum finally opened the site to visitors, with a curator's determination to change as little as possible. That restraint is why the rooms feel so uncannily alive. You are not walking through a set dressed to look like 1945. You are walking through 1945 itself, give or take a pane of protective glass.

In 2005 the museum added a superb modern counterpoint, the Churchill Museum, occupying a large adjoining space and devoted entirely to the man himself. Do go and find its centrepiece, a fifteen-metre interactive table called the Lifeline. It's an electronic timeline of Churchill's whole extraordinary career, and if you touch a date it opens up documents, photographs and film. Given that this is the cool, quiet finale to a punishing day of sightseeing, it's the ideal place to let the children loose while you catch your breath and take the measure of the century that was decided, in part, right here.
