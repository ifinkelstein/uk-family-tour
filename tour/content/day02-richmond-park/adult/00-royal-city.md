# First Morning in a Two Thousand Year Old City
Good morning, and welcome to London. If this is your first real day in the city, take a moment on the edge of this park to get your bearings, not just north and south, but in time. London is old. The Romans founded it as Londinium around the year fifty, a trading post on the north bank of the Thames, and people have lived and argued and built here more or less continuously ever since. Two thousand years. That is the number to hold in your head today.

Here is the strange part. This old city sits on a smallish, damp island off the edge of Europe, and yet the language you are listening to right now, the shape of a courtroom, the idea of a parliament, the map of half the modern world, all of these were bent into their present form here, or carried out from here, over the last few centuries. A small island punched astonishingly far above its weight, for better and for worse, and London was the fist.

So why begin in a field full of deer? Because London will surprise you. It is one of the greenest big cities on earth, stitched through with royal parks, and this one, Richmond Park, is the largest of them all. It exists because a king wanted somewhere to hunt. That is the thread we will follow all the way through this trip, from London north to Edinburgh and York, the long, tangled story of kings and queens and the land and buildings they shaped. It starts here, under these old oaks, with the deer watching you right back.
