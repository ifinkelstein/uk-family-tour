# The Tiny Island That Surprised the World
Let me tell you how surprising this little island really is. Picture a globe and find Britain. It is small, and it is tucked up in the cold, grey top corner of the world, with the sea splashing all the way around it. To the ancient Romans, this felt like the edge of everything, the very last stop before the endless ocean. And yet.

The Romans crossed the water and built their town, Londinium, right here by the river, with a wooden bridge and busy little boats. But not everyone was happy about the Romans marching in. A fierce warrior queen named Boudica led a huge rebellion, and her army burned the whole city down to ash. Can you imagine it? All of London, gone in flames. The Romans built it straight back again, even bigger, and it grew into the most important city in the whole country. Then, after about four hundred years, the Romans packed up and left, and other peoples arrived, Saxons and Vikings and more, all mixing together over time.

Out of all that mixing came something you use every single day: the English language. It is a jumble of pieces from lots of different languages stitched together, which is exactly why English spelling is so gloriously weird. Ever wondered why there is a silent letter k at the start of the word knight? You can blame this island for that.

And here is the really big thing. This tiny, rainy island ended up changing the entire world. The words you are speaking right this second are spoken by more people around the planet than almost any other language. Long ago, Britain's ships sailed nearly everywhere, and it came to rule a giant slice of the world, which made some people very rich and treated a great many others terribly unfairly. So when you stand here in this peaceful park, you are standing in the place where a surprising amount of the modern world got its start. Not bad for a small island right at the edge of the map.
