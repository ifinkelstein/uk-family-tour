# Whisky: Scotland's Water of Life
Every country has a national drink, but few have woven theirs so completely into their identity as Scotland has with whisky. The word itself tells you how seriously they take it. It comes from the Gaelic uisge beatha, which means, quite literally, the water of life. That in turn is a translation of an older Latin phrase, aqua vitae, the name medieval monks gave to any distilled spirit. Over the centuries uisge beatha was worn down by everyday speech, from uisge to usky to, at last, whisky. So the next time someone hands you a dram, remember that you are being offered a small glass of the water of life.

And it is, in a real sense, a taste of the land itself. Scotch whisky is made from just three things: barley, water, and yeast. Yet into it goes the whole character of a place. The water that ran off a particular hillside. The barley grown in a particular field. Sometimes the smoke of peat, the dark spongy turf dug from the bogs, which gives certain whiskies their unmistakable bonfire tang. And always the oak of the cask, where the raw spirit sleeps for years, drawing colour and sweetness out of the wood. A good whisky is a place you can drink.

Over the next few chapters we will follow the whole story. How whisky is actually made, from a field of barley to a glass by the fire. Why a whisky from one corner of Scotland tastes so unlike another. The wild history of smugglers and taxmen and hidden stills in the glens. And, for the grown-ups, where to find a proper dram here in Edinburgh, a city that has always lived close to the trade. Slàinte, as they say here. To your health.
