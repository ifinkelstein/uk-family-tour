# A Beach Smell and Invisible Singers
Here's a trick. Stop walking for a second, close your eyes, and just smell the hill. If it's a sunny day and you're near those big bushes covered in bright yellow flowers, take a good deep sniff. Coconut. Honestly, coconut, like sun cream or a tropical beach, right here on a cold Scottish hill. Those spiky bushes are called gorse, and their little yellow flowers really do smell of coconut when the sun warms them up. It's one of the best secrets on Arthur's Seat, and almost nobody tells you about it. Careful though, don't grab the gorse, because underneath the coconut smell it's covered in sharp green spikes. Smell, don't touch.

Now keep your eyes shut and listen. On a fine morning you might hear a bird singing and singing and singing, a tumbling silvery song that never seems to stop, but when you open your eyes and look, there's nothing there. Where is it? Look straight up, higher than you think. That's a skylark, and it does something amazing. It flies almost straight up into the sky, so high it turns into a tiny dot, and it sings the whole time it's climbing, then it parachutes back down still singing. People have loved that invisible song for hundreds of years. See if your family can be the first to spot the singer.

There's more life here than you'd guess on a bare-looking hill. The rabbits, of course, are everywhere, and this whole park really does belong to them. Watch for the white flash of a bobbing tail as one bolts for its burrow. But look up sometimes too, because the cliffs are home to one of the fastest animals on the whole planet, a bird called a peregrine falcon. When a peregrine folds its wings and dives on its dinner, it goes faster than a racing car, faster than any other creature alive. Somewhere on these crags, that little grey speck could be the fastest thing in the world just sitting there having a rest.

In summer there are butterflies bobbing over the grass, tough little birds hiding in the gorse, and wildflowers you'd never see in a tidy garden. Listen for a big black bird up on the crags making a deep, croaky call, more of a bark than a tweet. That's a raven, the biggest of all the crow family, and it loves rocky, wild places exactly like this. And feel the wind, because up here it can go from a gentle push to a proper shove, whistling round the rocks, so hold onto your hat and your little ones near the edges. So this hill isn't just a lump of old volcano. It's alive, top to bottom. Use all your senses as you climb. Smell the coconut, hunt for the invisible singer, watch for the world's fastest bird. A good explorer doesn't just look. A good explorer sniffs and listens too.
