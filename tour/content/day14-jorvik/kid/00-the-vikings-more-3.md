# How Do We Even Know This?
Here is a question you should always ask about the past. How does anyone actually know? For a very long time, the stories about Vikings came from just two kinds of people, and both of them stretched the truth. First, the monks and priests the Vikings robbed. They were so frightened and so angry that they made the Vikings sound like monsters straight out of a nightmare, all blood and fire. Can you blame them? Second, the Vikings' own storytellers, who told grand, exciting tales of gods and giants and heroes around the fire. Those tales are brilliant, but they were a bit like a superhero movie about your great-great-great-grandparents, made up hundreds of years later. Very exciting. Not exactly true. So who tells the real story of everyday Viking life, the ordinary stuff? The ground does. Right here in York, the dirt under this street kept Viking things safe and hidden for a thousand years, until archaeologists finally dug them up and got to see how real Vikings truly lived. And that is the amazing thing you are about to notice on the ride. Watch very carefully as your time car rolls through the street. You will not mostly see battles and warriors. You will see people working and cooking and making things, dyeing wool into bright colours, carving cups, mending shoes, feeding their animals, and yes, sitting on the toilet. That is the real Jorvik. The true one. The one the scared monks and the boasting storytellers both left out. You are about to see the Vikings not as a scary legend, but as they really were, on an ordinary, busy, smelly day. Keep your eyes wide open.
