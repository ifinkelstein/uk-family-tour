# The Most Famous Engine in the World
You've heard about Mallard, the duck-shaped engine that broke the speed record. Now meet the other superstar of this railway line, and this one might be even more famous. It's a big green steam engine called the Flying Scotsman, and it is probably the most famous locomotive on the entire planet.

First, a little puzzle. Flying Scotsman was the name of a train service, the ten o'clock express that ran from London up to Scotland every single morning. But it was also the name given to one particular engine, painted a beautiful deep green with the number four-four-seven-two on its side. And that engine became a legend.

Here's the first amazing thing it did. Back in nineteen twenty-eight, it pulled a train all the way from London to Edinburgh without stopping even once. Now that might not sound hard, but think about it. A steam engine needs two people driving it, a driver and a fireman, and shovelling coal into a roaring fire for eight hours without a break would completely exhaust anybody. So how did they swap the tired crew for a fresh one without stopping? They came up with a genius trick. They built a secret tunnel, a narrow little corridor, running right through the middle of the coal-and-water wagon behind the engine. So a fresh driver and fireman could squeeze through that tiny passage from the train, all the way to the engine, and take over while it was still thundering along at full speed. The tired ones squeezed back the other way for a rest. A crew swap at sixty miles an hour, through a tunnel in a pile of coal. Brilliant.

Then Flying Scotsman became the first engine ever officially timed going a hundred miles an hour, back in nineteen thirty-four. But its adventures were only getting started. When it grew old and the railway didn't need it anymore, people loved it far too much to scrap it. So it went travelling. It was shipped across the ocean to America, where huge crowds came to see it puff along past skyscrapers. Then it sailed all the way to Australia, on the other side of the world, and while it was there it ran the longest-ever non-stop trip by any steam engine, more than four hundred miles across the outback. By the time it came home, the Flying Scotsman had actually travelled right the way around the whole world. A steam engine that went round the globe.

Today it still runs, all cleaned up and gleaming green, taking lucky passengers on special trips, and enormous crowds still gather on bridges just to watch it go by. So on this journey you're following in the wheel-tracks of a genuine legend. Somewhere on this very line, that famous green engine roared north, with a fresh crew crawling through a tunnel of coal. Listen closely, and you can almost hear it.
