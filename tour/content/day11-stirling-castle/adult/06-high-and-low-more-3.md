# The Hands That Built the Palace
Everything you have admired on this rock, the carved facade, the golden hall, the ceiling of emperors, did not appear by royal wish alone. It was made, stone by stone and cut by cut, by the hands of skilled working men, and their world is worth a moment of your time.

At the top of the building trade stood the master mason. A mason was not a simple labourer but a trained professional who had served years as an apprentice, then as a journeyman, before being judged skilled enough to be called a master, and the very best of them were true artists and engineers who could design a building, cut the most delicate ornament, and command a whole worksite. For a great royal project like this palace, the crown appointed a Master of Work to run the money and the men, and hired the finest masons it could find, often bringing them from abroad. James the Fifth's palace was shaped by French craftsmen, because French taste was the height of fashion, and the king wanted the very latest look imported wholesale to Stirling.

The masons worked in a lodge, a workshop hut on the site, and many of them signed the stones they cut with a personal mason's mark, a little scratched symbol that was part signature and part paperwork, a way of proving whose work was finished and ready for its wages. Look closely at old stonework in Scotland and you can still find these marks today, the fingerprints of individual men five centuries gone.

Then there were the woodcarvers, and here we can actually name a name. The Stirling Heads, those magnificent oak medallions a metre across that you have met upstairs, are thought to be the work of a French carver known in the Scottish records as Andrew Mansioun, working around fifteen forty alongside a Scottish colleague called John Drummond. Mansioun was a court craftsman of the most useful kind. The royal accounts show him making furniture, carving fittings for the king's own yacht, even making a cradle for the royal baby, and later drawing a wage as a gunner in the king's artillery at three pounds a month, because a clever man with his hands could turn them to almost anything. He lived in Scotland for decades and died an old man, a foreign craftsman who built himself a whole career here.

And over everything came the painters, because none of this was ever meant to be bare stone and honey-coloured oak. The carvings and walls and ceilings were painted and gilded in loud, glorious colour, and that too was skilled, expensive work by specialists.

These men were organised into guilds, the trade bodies that controlled who was allowed to practise a craft, trained the apprentices, set the standards, and looked after members who fell sick or died. A skilled craftsman earned comfortably more than a common labourer, and a favoured royal one could do very well indeed. They were not nobles, and history has mostly forgotten their names, but the beauty you came all this way to see is theirs, not the king's. He paid for it. They made it.
