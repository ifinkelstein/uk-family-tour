# One Island, Two Old Nations
Somewhere in the next hour or so, with no bridge to announce it and no guard to stamp anything, your train will slip out of one country and into another. It is worth pausing on what that actually means, because for a newcomer the geography of these islands can be quietly baffling. So here is the shape of it. You are riding up the spine of a single island, the one properly called Great Britain, and that one island has long held more than one nation. England fills the south and the centre, Scotland the north, and Wales the western bulge you cannot see from here. Three old countries on one island, with a scatter of smaller isles around them and, across the water, the separate island of Ireland.

England and Scotland are the two that concern you today, and they are genuinely two, not two halves of one thing. They were rival kingdoms for the better part of a thousand years, each with its own kings and its own armies, and a border between them that was fought over so often and so savagely that its scars are still on the map. They were joined, eventually, in stages. In sixteen oh three they came under a single crown, when a Scottish king inherited the English throne as well. Just over a century later, in seventeen oh seven, their two parliaments merged into one, and the United Kingdom as we know it began to take shape. And yet Scotland never dissolved into England. To this day it keeps its own laws, its own schools and universities, its own national church, even its own banknotes, and, fiercer than any of these, its own emphatic sense of itself. You are not changing counties this afternoon. You are crossing from one nation into another, on one island, under one crown, and the rest of your week belongs to the northern one.
